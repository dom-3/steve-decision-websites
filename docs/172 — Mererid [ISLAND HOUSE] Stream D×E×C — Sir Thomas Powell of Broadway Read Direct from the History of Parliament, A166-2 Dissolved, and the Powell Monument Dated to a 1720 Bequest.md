## 2. The five-line finding

1. **A166-2 dissolves — it was a false dichotomy.** HoP gives the *father*, Sir John Powell, as "of Pentre Meyrick, Llanwrda, Carm. **and** Broadway". The two seats were held **together**, not as alternatives. Four passes of waiting on a church tablet were spent on a question whose premise was wrong; the conjunction is printed in the object.
2. **The Powell monument at Laugharne parish church is a bequest of 1720 and commemorates the father, not the testator.** Sir Thomas "left a sum of money for the erection of a monument in Laugharne parish church to his 'most honoured and dear father', with whom he wished to be buried." He died at Broadway on 22 August 1720. This settles from the desk what has been queued for an on-site read — with two cautions (§4.2).
3. **A dated royal grant in 1697 of a market and two annual fairs at Laugharne**, to Sir Thomas as continuing royal favour. Hard, dated, and new to the folder — but it is **town fabric** and is flagged to the parallel branch, and it must never be confused with the Corporation's late-thirteenth-century charter.
4. **The Broadway Powell male line failed immediately.** "His only son died within a year, s.p., leaving an estate of around £3,000 p.a. to be divided among the surviving daughters." Division among coheiresses is the mechanism by which Broadway could pass to a differently-surnamed family without a sale — which bears directly on the unproven Powell → Watkins seam.
5. **A FALSE BLOCKER WAS SITTING IN OUR OWN REGISTER.** The thread note said this page was "JavaScript-rendered; needs the browser route, not web_fetch". It is not. `web_fetch` returned the complete article on the first attempt. Doc 169 named browser reliability as "on the critical path"; on this evidence it was not, and a blocker recorded once had begun to propagate as fact.

## 4. The four things that matter

### 4.1 A166-2 was the wrong question

The register had been carrying "Broadway House **vs** Pentre Meurig" as a live either/or. HoP shows both were Powell seats, held by the same man at the same time. Broadway is the Laugharne seat; Pentre Meyrick lies in Llanwrda parish, elsewhere in the county. There is no conflict to resolve.

Two housekeeping notes. HoP spells it **Pentre Meyrick**; our register carries **Pentre Meurig**. Same place, two spellings — do not let them become two records. And the tablet is no longer needed for *this* question, though it remains wanted for others.

> **The wider lesson.** Four passes were spent deferring a question to an archive visit. The blocker was not the archive. It was that nobody had tested whether the question was well-formed. A queued item should be re-read for premise, not just for route.

### 4.2 The monument — a real gain, with two brakes on it

We now have, from a signed scholarly object: the monument in Laugharne parish church was funded by Sir Thomas Powell's will, is therefore **post-August 1720**, and its subject is **Sir John Powell**, his father, with whom he wished to be buried.

Before this touches a script:

- **A bequest is an instruction, not proof of execution.** Money left for a monument is not a monument. Do not say it was built until the fabric, a faculty or a Cadw description says so.
- **We have not established that the surviving tablet *is* this monument.** State the bequest; let the tablet remain a separate object until something joins them. Joining them by assumption is precisely how the record gets contaminated.

### 4.3 The seven bishops — the best story of the pass, and the one not to tell

HoP says Sir John Powell — father of the Laugharne squire — was dismissed from the bench by James II for giving his opinion **in favour of the seven bishops**, was restored in 1689, and that King William had originally offered him the lord keepership. If the identification holds, one of the most celebrated acts of judicial independence in English law sits on the Laugharne gentry spine.

**It is registered Referenced-pending and must not be narrated.** Two live problems:

1. **The name-trap is already in our register**: "Sir John Powell of Gloucester (1645–1713) [NAME-TRAP]". Contemporaries of the same name sat on the same benches. HoP gives our man **no dates at all**.
2. **HoP contradicts itself on the court** — "j.c.p." in the pedigree, "King's bench" eight lines later.

There *is* a discriminator: HoP's "suc. fa. 1696" puts the Laugharne father's death in 1696, against the Gloucester namesake's 1713. But that discriminator comes from HoP itself, so it does not close the trap. The obvious next read is the DNB article HoP cites at footnote 4.

This is exactly the shape of story that gets a heritage programme caught: vivid, nationally famous, and resting on a shared surname.

### 4.4 The 1697 grant — flagged across the branch line

"A grant in 1697 of a market and two fairs to be held annually in his town of Laugharne", cited to *CSP Dom.* 1697, p. 86.

- **It belongs to the parallel branch** (`mererid-house-of-culture`) as town fabric. Registered here only because the grantee sits on this branch's occupancy spine. Logged so the other queue can take it.
- **It is a royal grant to a private gentleman, not a Corporation charter.** Never conflate the two: different instrument, different grantee, four centuries apart. And the charter date remains "late thirteenth century" — never a numeral.
- **"His town of Laugharne" is Hayton's shorthand.** It is quotable, vivid, and wrong in exactly the way trail copy loves. It does **not** mean the Powells owned or governed the borough. Laugharne is governed by its Corporation under its Portreeve — one Portreeve, and one of only two medieval corporations to survive in Britain, alongside the City of London.
- It stays **Referenced-pending**: the Calendar is subscription-gated on British History Online and was not read. Paid source, archive day.

## 5. Two traps flagged before they sprang

**The second Mansel marriage.** Powell's first wife was Elizabeth (d.1697), daughter of Thomas Mansel of **Briton Ferry** and Penderyn, granddaughter of Bussy Mansel, sister of Thomas Mansel II. Doc 125 traced a Hughes × Mansell-**of-Trimsaran** marriage into the Island House spine. Different branch, different seat. Two Mansel marriages into Laugharne-connected gentry is genuinely interesting; merging them would be the Doc 141 near-collision error in a new costume.

**Three birth years in one article.** The header says "c.1665"; the 1698 marriage entry says "aged 32" (→ c.1665/66); the death notice says died 22 Aug 1720 "aged 56" (→ c.1663/64). A two-year spread internal to a single object. **Do not print a birth year.** Where one object disagrees with itself, the honest output is the disagreement — the same discipline as the Arthur Bevan four-way death date (Doc 169 F2).

The register's own thread note said: *"JavaScript-rendered; needs the browser route, not web_fetch."*

That was wrong. `web_fetch` returned the entire article — pedigree, offices, narrative, footnotes — first attempt, no browser needed.

**Why this matters more than one page.** Doc 169 closed by putting Chrome-extension reliability "on the critical path" and flagging that two of the three best remaining targets needed the browser route. At least one of those three did not. A blocker recorded once becomes a fact in the register and routes future passes around an open door.

**RULE ADDED — re-test a recorded blocker with the cheap tool before routing around it, and date-stamp every blocker at the moment of recording.** A blocker is an observation about a tool on a day, not a property of a source.

**One real constraint, correctly characterised:** `web_fetch` will only accept a URL that has already appeared in a search result or a prior fetch. A `WebSearch` must precede the first fetch of a new domain. That is a provenance gate, not a rendering problem — and it is almost certainly what the original note misdiagnosed.

**Consequence for the queue:** HoP member pages are plain-HTML and desk-readable. The whole 1690–1715 and 1715–1754 Carmarthenshire run is now desk-runnable, which materially lengthens the queue rather than shortening it.

**Dependence test applied, per Doc 169.** D. W. Hayton is both the article's author *and* a volume editor. An appeal to "the editors" is not independent corroboration of Hayton — the same pattern as Dr Mary Clement in the DWB, and the Coflein-dependent-on-Cadw rule before that. HoP is now on the dependence watch-list.
