Globe Bakery · Talacharn · Laugharne

Three authentic directions

Not three versions of the same “premium” look — three genuinely different souls, each grown from a real truth about the bakery. Pick the one that feels like us, and we build it out properly (and can brief a Welsh studio to take it further).

Why the last one felt generic: it borrowed the default “premium” template (serif + italic word, muted wine/cream, tidy grids) instead of coming from something only Globe Bakery in Laugharne has. Each of these starts from a real thing: our place & language, our craft, or our honest promise.
Direction 1
Popty'r Globe — Rooted in Laugharne
Bara go iawn,
from the old township.
Welsh-first and unmistakably here. Bilingual naming (Popty'r Globe · the Globe Bakery), the loaves named in Welsh and English, a hand-drawn roundel that could be an old enamel sign on Duncan Street. It carries the estuary, the castle and Dylan Thomas's township in its bones — a brand no one anywhere else could own.

Palette — estuary & hearth

Slate-green & estuary tones, warm terracotta, oat cream — drawn from the town and the marsh, not the “hospitality” default.

Type & voice

Fraunces — a warm, characterful old-style serif (soft, a little wonky) for a heritage-signwriting feel; plain Jost for the everyday.

Torth y dydd.
Today's loaf.
  • Ownable: the Welsh place (Talacharn) and language can't be copied by a chain.
  • Ties naturally to The Globe and to Laugharne's tourism / Dylan Thomas pull.
  • Warm and local — the opposite of slick-anonymous.
Direction 2
The Bench — built from the craft
Scored by hand.
Baked before dawn.
The identity comes straight off the workbench — the score-lines the baker slashes into each loaf become the graphic motif (exactly how Rose Bakery built theirs). Ink-stamped kraft paper bands, flour-dust textures, a hand-cut look. Raw, tactile, honest — it looks made, not designed.

Palette — kraft & ink

Kraft-paper brown, black ink, one oxblood stamp. Monochrome + one hit — cheap to print, looks handmade.

Type & voice

Oswald (bold condensed, stencil-ish) for headlines; Space Mono for the stamped labels & prices — like a workshop docket.

FLOUR ON THE FLOOR.
  • Ownable: the actual score-pattern & the kraft band Steve already wants — a true thing.
  • Tactile & imperfect — reads as genuinely artisan, never AI-slick.
  • Cheap and practical to produce (stamps, one ink, kraft).
Direction 3
The Honest Loaf — people & health
Flour. Water.
Salt. Time. that's it.
Plain-spoken and human — the soul is honest bread that helps people eat well. Big confident type, the bakers' hands and faces front and centre, real testimonials (the customer who couldn't eat bread for years, and now can). A zine-ish, warm, no-nonsense feel. Not fancy — true.

Palette — paper & a good-for-you green

Paper white, honest black, a wholesome green, a wheat-gold accent. Warm-minimal, not cold-minimal.

Type & voice

Space Grotesk — a confident, plain, modern grotesque; Caveat handwriting for the human asides.

Bread you can
actually eat.
  • Ownable: the real testimonials & the health-first promise are yours, and rare.
  • Puts the real people up front — instantly authentic, hard to fake.
  • Confident and clean without being the “luxury template”.
These are starting points — the winner gets built out fully, and we can hand a brief to a Welsh studio (Formation Creative) to push it further.
