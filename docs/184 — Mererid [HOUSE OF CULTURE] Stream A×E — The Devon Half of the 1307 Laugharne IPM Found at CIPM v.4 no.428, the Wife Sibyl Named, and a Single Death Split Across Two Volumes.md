- **The heir:** Guy, son of Guy and Sibyl, aged 24.

## 3. The identification with the Laugharne lord — the near-collision test run explicitly

The search traps warn that a near-matching name on a co-occurring surface is exactly how a false identification enters the record. So the join between vol.4 no.428 ("Guy de Briane", Devon) and vol.5 no.64 ("Guy de Brian, lord of Laugharne", Wales — Doc 182) was tested, not assumed:

- **Same name.** Both "Guy de Brian(e)".
- **Same death.** Both are *post-mortem* inquisitions, and the two writs — 18 July **35 Edw. I** (vol.4) and 26 July **1 Edw. II** (vol.5) — bracket Edward I's death of **7 July 1307**. They are inquisitions on one death, taken either side of the reign change.
- **Same heir.** Both name the heir as Guy, aged **23–24**, b. c.1283/84.
- **A family fingerprint.** **Slapton**, Devon, is a canonical de Brian manor: the later Sir Guy de Brian, K.G. (d.1390) founded the chantry/college at Slapton. The Devon estate in no.428 is the same family's, not a coincidental namesake's.

**Tier: Verified** (not Verified-strong) for the identification itself, because the two calendar entries do not cross-cite each other — the join rests on prosopographic convergence rather than a single document stating it. The individual facts *within* no.428 (Sibyl; the Devon holdings; the heir aged 24) are **Verified-strong** as direct object reads.

This is the "which Guy" question moving forward. Doc 182 had separated three Guys at a fixed date: A (grandfather, d. before 1307, widow Joan holding dower); B (the deceased, d. 1307); C (heir, aged 23, b. c.1283/84). Vol.4 no.428 pins **B**: it gives B a wife (Sibyl), a Devon estate, and confirms C as B and Sibyl's son.

## 4. The method finding — a single death split across two volumes

The substantive methodological result of this pass generalises beyond Laugharne:

> When a tenant-in-chief dies within months of a reign change, the Calendar of Inquisitions Post Mortem can **split that one death's inquisitions across two volumes** — one under each king — because the writs and returns are filed by regnal year, not by person.

Guy de Brian died July 1307. His Devon inquisitions (writ 18 July 35 Edw. I) were calendared under Edward I in **vol.4**; his Welsh barony of Laugharne (writ 26 July 1 Edw. II) under Edward II in **vol.5**. A run that reads only the Laugharne-indexed volume (as Doc 182 did, correctly, for its own scope) will capture the barony but miss the rest of the man — here, his Devon estate and, crucially, his wife's name.

**Rule for the desk:** when an IPM sits within a few months of a reign change, check the CIPM volume on **both sides** of the boundary for the other half of the same person's inquisitions, using each volume's own index under all name and place variants. This is an extension of Doc 182's index-as-allocator method, not a contradiction of it.
