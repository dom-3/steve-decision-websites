The Globe · Coffee Shop

The daytime door

The Globe's daytime trade — specialty coffee, the bakery's bread and pastry over the counter, light daytime food, and the first rest stop on the Dylan trail. Not a standalone café: one of the Globe's doors, read as the Globe. The working hub for the research, the comparables and the decisions in front of Steve and Phil — research stage, no build until they've decided.

One kitchen, one brand, one operation. The bakery makes the bread; the coffee makes it pay; the trail brings the town to the door.

The opportunity, in one line

What the competitor scan tells us — and the gap we fill.

Laugharne has plenty of places to get a coffee, but none that treats coffee itself as the point. The Boathouse tearoom pours only filter and shuts two days a week; the Owl & the Pussycat is a beloved cake-tearoom; the Ferryman is a good deli on a fair-trade blend; Poon's serves specialty (Coaltown) but inconsistently; the rest are pubs. There is no design-led, early-opening, seven-day, specialty-grade coffee house engineered for both the estuary sit-in crowd and grab-and-go walkers heading to the Boathouse. That is the third Globe door.

The three crossovers

Why this door exists — the hinges it sits on.

Bakery → coffee

The coffee shop is the bakery's daytime retail front. One kitchen, shared production and waste. Bread is low-margin; coffee is high-margin — modelled together on the existing EPOS break-even model, never as two businesses.

House of Culture → coffee

The Dylan day-loop needs a morning anchor and a mid-walk rest. Trail footfall becomes Globe customers at a £4 coffee, then upsells the evening table. Built on the blueprint's arrive → coffee → reading-room spine.

Brand → coffee

The third face of the Globe Studio design system — Wine Bar dark, Bakery light, now this. It extends the system, it does not invent a new identity. No separate café name.

The research backbone

The three deep threads, in brief. Full write-ups: Doc 170 (decision list) and Doc 171 (deep research) in the register.

Supply — the roaster

Coaltown (Ammanford, ~30 mi) is the lead: UK's first B Corp roaster, 2026 SCA design award, La Marzocco's South-Wales service partner, and the "black gold / liquid gold" story. Caveat: already poured in town (Poon's) and at Wright's — so the room, hours and takeaway differentiate, not the bean. Add a guest single-origin (Origin / Round Hill) on a second grinder. Specialty beans £16–24/kg; machine-loan removes ~£10–18k of capital but ties beans 2–5 yrs. Milk: Totally Welsh. Decaf: Swiss Water.

Awards — "liquid gold"

The fast, credible win is a Great Taste star on a signature house blend — every entry can win, ~£77, and we needn't roast it ourselves. Enter the moment the 2027 window opens (~Dec 2026). Welsh route: Food & Drink Awards Wales + public-vote Welsh café awards. Coaltown is the blueprint. Correction: "Golden Bean UK" does not exist — the real title is the SCA UK Roasting Championship (a roaster's long game).

Competition & footfall

Real daytime rivals: Ferryman, Poon's, Owl & the Pussycat, Boathouse tearoom — not "Dexter's", which is a steakhouse at Brown's. Regional bar set by destinations people drive to: Wright's (Llanarthne), Crwst (Cardigan), Ginhaus (Llandeilo), Coaltown's own canteen. Footfall is literary-tourism led (Castle ~14.6k/yr 2018; Boathouse significant but unquantified), summer- and weekend-weighted — the business must survive winter mid-weeks on local trade.
