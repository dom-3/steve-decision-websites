Island House · The Flagship

The Architecture of the Story

The blueprint for turning Island House into a full published work — a book, an art-and-history piece, and a living record of the restoration. One building that holds the whole story of a Welsh medieval port — and a dig uncovering national history as it happens.

The idea: not a brochure, but a body of work — history and art combined, told evidence-true, with the restoration as its living present tense.

Island House isn't three paragraphs on an “about” page. It's a subject worthy of a book, an exhibition and original research. The 2025 excavation — funded by the owners and led by Ken Murphy of Heneb — uncovered the first medieval quays ever excavated in Wales, buried under the house. That is primary evidence no one else has, so we're not re-tellers, we're contributors. This page is how all of it is structured into something lasting and publishable.

1 · The spine — the layers of the story

The chapters of the book, read like strata — from the ground up to today. Each carries an honest evidence tag.

The setting

The tidal inlet — why there's an “island”

The house sits on what was a tidal inlet at the mouth of the River Coran, with a river channel still running beneath it (the reason the building is structurally unstable). At high tide the spot was all but an island — which is how Island House got its name. The name is written into the fabric, too: the Cadw listing of the house's own boundary wall dates the wall's origins “to the time when this was an island site.”

Fact — excavated & documented
13th century

The charter & the medieval town

The de Brian family held Laugharne from 1247, and under them it became a chartered borough — burgesses, a weekly market and burgage plots let at 12d a year. (The charter itself is undated; historians place it later in the 1200s.) This is where the working port begins, on the record.

Fact — documented
13th–16th century

The quays — the first found in Wales

Under the house lay a run of successive stone quays: an “arms race” pushed further out as the estuary silted, each one infilled behind with cockle shells (Laugharne's “Cocklemen”; the mayor's gold cockle-shell chain, c.1740). A jetty and a surviving flight of stone steps ran down onto the keyside. The first medieval quays ever excavated in Wales — parallels only at Bristol's Redcliffe and in London.

Fact — excavated
Late medieval

The merchants' hall houses

High-status hall houses stood on the keyside — central hearths, clay floors, first floors added later, and a garderobe (a first-floor toilet): the homes of merchants and gentry grown wealthy on the port. The pottery among the finds points to trade with Bristol, France and Spain — this small quay reached out into the medieval Atlantic economy.

Fact — excavated
c.1610–1630

Island House is built

The house itself was built around 1630. Its roof timbers dendro-date to a 1437 felling — but the truss style is c.1610–1620, so the medieval timbers were reused. A massive garden wall (~3m, clay-waterproofed) held the water back; its later breach is why the site floods today.

Fact — dated
14th century

The Admiral — Sir Guy Bryan

A later Guy Bryan (c.1311–1390) — Admiral, Knight of the Garter, standard-bearer to Edward III — carried the family name into national history. Was the port a muster point for royal fleets? The hook to test against the archives.

To test — archives

Dylan Thomas's Laugharne

The town Dylan made famous. A small, marketable thread: did his world touch Island House itself? (A resident overlapped with him.)

To test — literary archive
Now

The restoration — the living chapter

Bought in 2018 by Steven Kirkwood and Philip Bond to become a small hotel, the house is being saved: after the dig a 1.5m-thick wall collapsed and has been stabilised, with an interior fit-out and two new wings (kitchen/restaurant and bedrooms) to come — roughly five years to opening. Scientific dating from the excavation is due just after Christmas.

Fact — happening now
The source under the “facts”

The excavated evidence on this page comes from the 2025 dig led by Ken Murphy of Heneb — the Trust for Welsh Archaeology (formerly Dyfed Archaeological Trust), presented at Archaeology Day 2025, and from the accompanying dendrochronology and historical research — all funded by the owners. Post-excavation scientific dating results are expected just after Christmas and will firm up several dates further. Two ideas Steven has raised for the finished hotel: rebuild the open medieval hall as a reproduction reception, or trace the quay and building lines as features in the garden.

2 · What makes it original (not a retelling)

The research angles that could add genuinely new knowledge — and attract a university, grants and national press. (Full detail in research doc 24.)

Flagship
The quays ↔ the cargoes

Tie the excavated quays to the surviving customs accounts & port books — the first synthesis of the port's real trade. The physical evidence is now in hand; the archives are mostly desk work, highest impact.

Reconstruction
The lost waterfront

Maps + LiDAR + the dig → a dated animation of Laugharne's waterfront over 800 years, and why the port died. A killer “then vs now”.

National hook
The Admiral's fleet

Cross-reference Sir Guy Bryan's naval career with Laugharne as a working harbour — a national-history lift.

Desk archaeology
LiDAR & map-regression

Overlay historic maps + open LiDAR to detect lost features without digging — new archaeology from data alone, plus a beautiful interactive map.
