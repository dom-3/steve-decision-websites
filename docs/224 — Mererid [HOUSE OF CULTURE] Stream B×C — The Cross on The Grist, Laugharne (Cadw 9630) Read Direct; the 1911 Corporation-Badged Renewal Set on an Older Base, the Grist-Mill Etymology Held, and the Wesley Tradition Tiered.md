## 0. One-line finding

The Cross at the west end of the Grist is a Grade II listed stone Celtic crucifix (Cadw 9630) whose 1911 base plaque — "This New Cross was placed on the Old base 1911, G Williams, Sculptor, Laugharne" — is stamped with the badge of Laugharne Corporation, giving a clean, object-level link between a surviving piece of town fabric and the ancient civic body; the plaque also fixes that the 1911 cross was set on an **older, pre-existing base** of undated age, the grist-mill etymology is Cadw's own assertion (held Referenced-pending), and the John Wesley preaching story is recorded by Cadw explicitly as tradition.

## 1. Why this target

Doc 217 read the Town Hall (seat of the Corporation) and Doc 218 the Globe's adjoining frontage; St Martin's church went in at Doc 215. The Cross on the Grist is the next un-done town-core listed monument with a clean open-source object, and it sits at the junction of two streams the desk is building — **B (Corporation)**, because the monument literally carries the Corporation's badge, and **C (Church)**, because it is a wayside/market cross of the Religious, Ritual and Funerary class with a preaching tradition attached. It is not an Island-House target and carries no Dylan hook. Depth was available on a single discrete object, which suits the one-target rule.

## 2. The object, read direct

Primary read at object level, 23 July 2026, from Cadw's own Full Report for Listed Buildings, id 9630, and cross-checked against the British Listed Buildings transcription (300009630). Both carry identical description text; the Cadw report is the primary and is quoted here.

- **Designation:** Grade II, designated 25 September 1986. Reference 9630. Broad class: Religious, Ritual and Funerary.
- **Location:** "At the western end of a broad tarmac area close to the foreshore." OS grid SN 30006 10724 (Easting 230064, Northing 210724); lat 51.7693, long −4.4642.
- **Exterior (verbatim, incl. Cadw's own typos):** "Stone Celtic Crucifix on rubble drum base; rope ornamentaiton. Base plaque inscribed \"This New Cross was palced on the Old base 1911, G Williams, Sculptor, Laugharne\" and stamped with the badge of Laugharne Corporation."
- **History (verbatim):** "The name of the area refers to a grist-mill that existed here. John Wesley is said to have preached from the cross."
- **Reason for designation:** Group value.

## 3. What the object earns — facts and tiers

**Fact 1 — VERIFIED.** The Cross on the Grist is a stone Celtic crucifix on a rubble drum base with rope ornament, listed Grade II (Cadw 9630, designated 25 September 1986). Straight off the designation record.

**Fact 2 — VERIFIED.** The present cross bears a base plaque reading "This New Cross was placed on the Old base 1911, G Williams, Sculptor, Laugharne", stamped with the badge of Laugharne Corporation. Two additive things sit inside the plaque and both are earned by it:
  1. the 1911 cross was set on an **older base that already existed** — the plaque says so ("on the Old base"). The age of that older base is **not stated** and must not be invented. There is no date, no "medieval", no "13th-century" — the honest record is simply "older, undated".
  2. the ancient Laugharne Corporation stamped its **badge** on the 1911 renewal. This is the House-of-Culture payload: a direct, object-level tie between a piece of surviving town fabric and the civic body, dated to a specific act of renewal in 1911. It also gives a named local craftsman, **G. Williams, sculptor, of Laugharne**.

**Fact 3 — REFERENCED-PENDING.** The name "The Grist" derives, per Cadw, from a grist-mill that formerly stood on the site. Cadw states this as fact in its History field, but the mill itself is not independently evidenced by this object. Held Referenced-pending: do not upgrade on Cadw's assertion alone. (A grist-mill is a corn/grain mill; the common noun makes the etymology plausible, but plausibility is not evidence of a specific mill on this specific spot.)

**Fact 4 — TRADITION.** John Wesley is said to have preached from the Cross. Cadw itself hedges — "is said to". Recorded as Tradition, **not** promoted. Wesley's Welsh itinerancy (many visits, 1740s–1780s) is real history, but his preaching *from this cross* is unconfirmed and has not been back-checked against Wesley's own Journal.

## 5. Guardrails observed

- **No invented base date.** The "Old base" is older than 1911 and otherwise undated. It is *not* called medieval, ancient, or 13th-century. This monument is a natural place for a "13th-century" style over-reach; it has been refused.
- **Corporation wording.** The badge fact is stated as what it is — the Corporation stamped its badge on the 1911 cross — with no claim about the Corporation's survival status, charter date, or Portreeve/Attorney offices dragged in unsourced.
- **No Dylan hook, no Grist-name gloss.** The Grist is glossed only as far as Cadw glosses it (grist-mill), and that is held at tier, not asserted.
- **Publish gate.** Nothing here is proposed for the public trail; tiering only.

## 6. Open routes (recorded, not walked)

1. **The "Old base".** The strongest un-answered question this object raises: what and how old is the base the 1911 cross was set on? Routes: the tithe/OS large-scale town plans for a cross symbol on the Grist; Mary Curtis (1880) and other antiquarian descriptions of Laugharne for a pre-1911 cross; RCAHMW/Coflein for any NPRN attached to a Grist cross or its base.
2. **G. Williams, sculptor, Laugharne, 1911.** A named local craftsman — trade directories (Kelly's 1901/06/14), the 1911 census, or a local-press notice of the cross's re-erection could put a first name and a workshop to him.
3. **The Wesley tradition.** Wesley's *Journal* and the Welsh Methodist historical record (Bathafarn) for a dated Laugharne visit, to move Fact 4 off Tradition or refute it cleanly.
4. **The grist-mill.** A map/tithe or documentary object showing a mill at this location would move Fact 3 to Verified.
