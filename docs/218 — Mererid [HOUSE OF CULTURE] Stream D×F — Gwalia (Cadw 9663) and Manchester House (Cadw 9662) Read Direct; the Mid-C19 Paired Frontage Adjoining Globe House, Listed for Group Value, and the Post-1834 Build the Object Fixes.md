## 1. Why this target

After the Town Hall (Doc 217), the Globe's own listing (Doc 198), Great House (Doc 202) and the King Street inns (Docs 194, 196, 204), the obvious un-done piece of the Globe's own townscape was the pair of frontages that Cadw repeatedly locates **"Opposite the Town Hall and adjoining Globe House"** — Gwalia (Cadw 9663) and Manchester House (Cadw 9662). They are the buildings a visitor standing at the Globe's corner actually sees to the side; documenting them closes the built edge of the Globe/Town-Hall/Market-Street junction — the civic heart of the town — building by building.

Neither had a Docs-Index entry. Both carry a readable open-source object. Target picked; taken as one paired cluster (precedent: Doc 135 Clifton House + Dragon Park; Doc 210 Castle House + gazebo).

## 2. The objects read

Read at object level from the Cadw statutory listing text (reproduced by British Listed Buildings, which mirrors the Cadw record verbatim). The Cadw FullReport API links are logged in Sources as the authority. **The two records carry an identical exterior description** — they were listed as a matched pair under one architectural account, differentiated only by two clauses.

Verbatim exterior text (identical on both records):

> "Paired mid C19 (not shown on 1834 survey map) late Georgian rendered 3-storey fronts with cill band to double pile structure; slate roof and projecting eaves. Small-pane sash glazing, 2-windows to 2nd floor (higher and with pitched dormer roofs to Gwalia); 2-storey bay windows with cornices and later glazing to shop fronts. Bracketed hoods to doorways, 4-panel door to Manchester House."

Shared designation facts (both):

- **Grade:** II (both).
- **Listing date:** 25 September 1986 (both) — the 1986 town sweep, not the earlier 1966 tranche that caught the Great House and the Grist-corner houses.
- **Building Class:** Commercial (both) — i.e. shop-houses, consistent with the "shop fronts" in the description.
- **Reason for listing:** "Group value." (both) — listed for townscape/group contribution, not individual architectural or historic distinction.
- **Location (Cadw's own words):** "Opposite the town Hall and adjoining Globe House." (both).
- **Grid:** SN301108 (OS 230188/210850 Gwalia; 230187/210857 Manchester House) — the two sit one immediately above the other on the frontage.
- **Wikidata:** Gwalia Q29488420; Manchester House Q29488419.

Distinguishing clauses within the shared text:
- **Gwalia** is the taller of the two ("higher") and has the **pitched dormer roofs** to the second floor.
- **Manchester House** has the **4-panel door**.

## 3. What the object establishes (findings)

1. **The pair is mid-19th-century, not Georgian in date.** Cadw's phrase is "**mid C19 … late Georgian**": late-Georgian in *style* (rendered three-storey fronts, small-pane sashes, cill band, bracketed door-hoods), but mid-19th-century in *build*. The vernacular Georgian idiom persisted in the town well after the reign. Anchor to the description, not to a numeral: the honest dating is "mid-nineteenth-century, in a surviving late-Georgian manner."

2. **A firm terminus post quem from the map.** Cadw states the pair is "not shown on the 1834 survey map." The 1834 survey is the same map the branch identified in Doc 164b. So the pair was **built after 1834** — a documentary floor for the date that is independent of style. This ties the Globe-frontage cluster to the town's post-1834 rebuilding phase (the Globe itself, per Cadw's Globe note, was a going "Globe Hotel" in the same period).

3. **They exist on the register for the Globe's sake as much as their own.** "Group value" is the *only* reason for listing. In heritage terms these two are protected because they hold the line of the historic frontage beside more important neighbours — the Grade II* Town Hall opposite and Globe House on the corner. That is a documentable statement about the Globe's setting: the Globe's corner is framed by a statutorily-protected group, of which Globe House is itself a member.

4. **Matched-pair, double-pile plan.** "Double pile structure" with a single shared description means one build event produced two mirror units — a speculative paired development typical of a mid-C19 market-town frontage. Gwalia (taller, dormered) and Manchester House (4-panel door) are the two halves.

5. **Corroboration of the Globe's own trade note (not re-opened here).** The Cadw record for the adjoining Globe House, quoted on both neighbour pages, reads: "Formerly the Globe Hotel, sold in 1869." This is consistent with — and independent of — the Globe listing already read at Doc 198. It is logged here only as a cross-reference; the Globe's own record is Doc 198's, and the open High-street/Duncan-Street address question (Doc 167b, plot 333) is **not** reconciled by this pass.
