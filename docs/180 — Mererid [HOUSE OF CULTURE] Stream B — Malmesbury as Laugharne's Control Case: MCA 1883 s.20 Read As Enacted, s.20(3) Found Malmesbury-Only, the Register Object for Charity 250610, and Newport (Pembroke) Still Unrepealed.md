That pass established the route but left the obvious control unrun: Laugharne is always described in company — "one of only two medieval corporations to survive in Britain" — and the statute that carried it into the modern world names it in the same breath as another town. The thread as registered contained an assumption in its own wording: *"Both were saved by MCA 1883 s.20."* That assumption turns out to be wrong in a way that matters. This pass is therefore a back-check as much as a gather.

**Scope note.** Malmesbury is not a Laugharne subject. It is registered on this desk strictly as a comparator, so that Laugharne's survival can be described by comparison rather than by assertion. Nothing here is a Malmesbury research programme.

## 1. The five-line finding

1. **MCA 1883 s.20 is headed "Saving as to Laugharne and Malmesbury" in the Act as enacted** — the pairing of the two towns is in the statute book itself, not an invention of guidebooks.
2. **The saving in s.20(1) is conditional, not absolute.** It operates only "In the event of a charter not being granted to Laugharne or Malmesbury." It is a default branch — what happens if nothing else happens — and not a positive parliamentary grant of survival.
3. **Subsection (3) applies to Malmesbury alone.** The extension of the freemen savings to landholders, assistant burgesses and capital burgesses is expressly limited to Malmesbury; Laugharne is not named in it. One section did two different jobs in two towns, and the thread's "both were saved by s.20" is not a safe description of either.
4. **The preamble frames both towns as property problems, not constitutional treasures** — the recited mischief is that members of the Corporation "have the right to occupy without rent, or at low rents certain property belonging to the Corporation."
5. **Section 20 is the one saving in that Act that was later taken away.** It was repealed entire by the Charities Act 1960, Sch. 5, while ss. 17 (Romney Marsh), 19 (Alnwick), 21 (Newport, Pembroke) and 24 (Corfe) remain unrepealed in the current statute book — including, in s.21, an express refusal to dissolve the corporation of a second Welsh town.

## 2. The object: s.20 as enacted, verbatim

Read at `legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1883/18/section/20/enacted`. The page is server-rendered and returns full text to a plain fetch; no browser was needed. The enacted table of contents was read as an independent control on the rubric.

> **20 Saving as to Laugharne and Malmesbury**
>
> Whereas in Laugharne and Malmesbury divers members of the Corporation, whether called burgesses, landholders, or any other name, have the right to occupy without rent, or at low rents certain property belonging to the Corporation, and it is expedient to make provision with respect to such property, be it enacted as follows:
>
> (1) In the event of a charter not being granted to Laugharne or Malmesbury, the property of the corporation of the place to which a charter is not so granted shall continue to be held, managed, and enjoyed as heretofore, in like manner as if a scheme of the Charity Commissioners, in pursuance of this Act, had provided for such holding, enjoyment, and management, and for that purpose the corporation in the said place shall continue undissolved in like manner as if it were constituted by the said scheme.
>
> (2) The corporation of such place, subject to the approval of the Charity Commissioners, may sell all or any of the property of the corporation for the best price that may be got for the same; and, after compensating or saving the rights of any person in such property, whether existing or prospective, may pay the proceeds to any public authority in the locality, to be applied by such authority for the benefit of the inhabitants of the said place.
>
> (3) The provisions of this Act and of the Municipal Corporations Act, 1882, for saving the rights and interests of freemen and of persons who might have become freemen shall extend to the rights and interests of persons who are or if this Act had not passed might have become landholders, assistant burgesses, or capital burgesses in Malmesbury, and for that purpose freemen of Malmesbury may continue to be elected landholders, assistant burgesses, and capital burgesses.

### 2.1 What the section actually does

Three subsections, three jobs.

**(1) is a fallback.** It is drafted around an absence — a charter *not* granted. The town it protects is whichever town failed to receive one. This desk already holds a Done thread (`rec9SjWPS9sGLGk1U`) establishing that no royal charter was granted to Laugharne before 29 September 1886 while one *was* granted to Malmesbury. If that holds, then s.20(1) — the survival clause proper — operated on **Laugharne alone**, notwithstanding that the rubric names both towns. Laugharne's corporation continued undissolved because nothing else was done for it.

**(2) is a power of sale**, exercisable only with the Charity Commissioners' approval, with the proceeds going to a public authority for the benefit of the inhabitants. Parliament's instinct in 1883 was that this property should probably be turned into money for the town.

**(3) is Malmesbury-only, on the face of it.** The named ranks — landholders, assistant burgesses, capital burgesses — are Malmesbury's ranks, not Laugharne's, and the subsection says "in Malmesbury" and "freemen of Malmesbury". Laugharne's Portreeve and Common Attorneys appear nowhere in the section.

So the section is a Janus. Its rubric pairs the towns; its operative provisions separate them. **"Both were saved by s.20" is a shorthand that collapses this and should not be used.** The accurate form is: *one section of the 1883 Act dealt with both towns, but by different limbs — the survival limb bit at Laugharne, the freemen-and-burgesses limb at Malmesbury.*

### 2.2 The preamble is the important part for tone

The recital is unsentimental. What brought Laugharne and Malmesbury into the Act was that corporation members were occupying corporation property rent-free or at low rents, and Parliament thought that wanted regulating. There is no antiquarian affection in the drafting.

This matters for the House of Culture. Any public copy that presents the 1883 Act as Parliament reaching out to protect a beloved medieval constitution is putting a motive into the statute that is not there. **The safe framing is that Laugharne's corporation survived because the Act's default branch caught it, and because its property was treated as a charity problem rather than a governance one.** That is a better story anyway — it is true, and it explains why the body's modern life is a charity-law life.

## 3. The repeal, and what was *not* repealed

Read from the revised text of the whole Act with its textual-amendment notes (`data.xht?view=snippet`).

| Section | Subject | Status in the current statute book |
|---|---|---|
| s.17 | Romney Marsh — bailiff jurats and commonalty; lords bailiff and jurats | **Unrepealed.** Both reputed corporations "shall … continue to exist, and to elect officers, and to hold the property vested in them" |
| s.19 | Alnwick, under the Alnwick Corporation Act 1882 | **Unrepealed.** "shall continue to be a corporation" |
| **s.20** | **Laugharne and Malmesbury** | **REPEALED** — Charities Act 1960 (c.58), Sch. 5 |
| s.21 | Newport (Pembroke) | **Unrepealed** |
| s.24 | Lord of the manor of Corfe Castle | **Unrepealed** |

Sections 8–11 (the Charity Commissioners' powers and the property provisions) went by the same Schedule 5 of the 1960 Act. Section 6 as enacted — "Power to Privy Council to preserve certain courts and officers" — survived until 1977.

The bare fact is arresting: **of the Act's savings for surviving bodies, Laugharne's is the one that was taken away.** What the 1960 repeal actually did to the Laugharne body is a separate question and this pass does not answer it — a repeal of the saving is not necessarily a dissolution of what the saving preserved, and the register entry for charity 218121 shows a body still very much regulated in 2003. That question stays open.

### 3.1 s.21, and a warning attached to a standing claim

The current statute book contains, unrepealed:

> **21 Saving for Newport, Pembroke.**
> Nothing in this Act shall be deemed to prevent the election of the mayor of Newport (Pembroke) as heretofore, or to dissolve the corporation of Newport (Pembroke), or deprive the lord of the manor or the burgesses of any tolls, rights of common, or other rights of a pecuniary value.

A second Welsh town, in the same Act, expressly not dissolved, with its mayoral election expressly protected — and its saving never repealed, unlike Laugharne's.

**This is a DO-NOT-CLAIM flag, not a refutation.** The standing guardrail — "one of only two medieval corporations to survive in Britain, alongside the City of London" — is not overturned by this. A statutory saving is not proof of a living institution; nothing has been established here about what exists at Newport today, and the Malmesbury body's civic powers demonstrably went in 1886 whatever it still calls itself. But the desk should know that the 1883 Act itself names four other bodies it declined to dissolve, two of them Welsh-adjacent in the sense that matters to a visitor with a phone. **Recommendation: the "only two" line stays as it is for now, but it is registered as a claim under pressure, and the House of Culture should not build a headline on it.** Resolving it properly needs a definition of "medieval corporation" that someone other than us has written down.

## 4. Malmesbury today: the register object

The Warden and Freemen's own website prints no registered charity number anywhere — a real negative, and one worth noticing, since the Athelstan Museum in the same town prints "Registered Charity: 1174110" in its footer. The body's charitable estate was found instead under a different name.

**ST JOHN'S HOSPITAL, registered charity 250610**, working name "The Almshouses". Read direct at the register's `/full-print` view, which is server-rendered; the ordinary charity-details view is not.

- **Governing document, verbatim:** "LETTERS PATENT OF KING WILLIAM III DATED 14 NOVEMBER 1696, SCHEME OF THE PRIVY COUNCIL OF 18 OCTOBER 1886 AND SCHEME OF 17 JUNE 1980"
- **Objects, verbatim:** "ALMSHOUSES FOR POOR PERSONS WHO ARE RESIDENT IN THE BOROUGH OF MALESBURY AS CONSTITUTED 1 JANUARY 1968 OR WITHIN A DISTANCE OF ONE MILE FROM THE BOUNDARY OF THE SAID BOROUGH ON THAT DATE." (*"MALESBURY" is the regulator's own spelling, and it appears twice — in Objects and again in Area of benefit. Noted, not corrected.*)
- Registered 2 February 1967, standard registration; organisation type "Other"; **thirteen trustees**; owns and/or leases land or property
- Income y/e 31 March 2025: £13,318. Expenditure: £17,058
- Contact: 1 Market Lane, Malmesbury SN16 9BQ — and the email `wardenandfreeman@btconnect.com`, the Warden and Freemen's own office mailbox

The identification of this charity with the Warden and Freemen rests on the shared office address and the shared mailbox printed on the register entry. That is strong, but it is circumstantial, and the claim is tiered **Verified** rather than Verified-strong accordingly.

**The thirteen trustees match the thirteen Capital Burgesses.** That is suggestive and no more. The register does not say the trustees hold office as Capital Burgesses ex officio, and no trustee name has been matched to any burgess list. It is not logged as a finding.

### 4.1 The comparison, stated carefully

| | **Laugharne** | **Malmesbury** |
|---|---|---|
| Charity vehicle | Laugharne Corporation Lands, **218121** (Doc 174) | St John's Hospital, **250610** |
| Governing instruments on the register | Scheme dated 27 May 2003; regulator's charter date given as "date unknown c.1300" | Letters Patent of William III, 14 Nov 1696; Privy Council Scheme, 18 Oct 1886; Scheme, 17 Jun 1980 |
| Limb of MCA 1883 s.20 that bit | (1) — the survival limb | (3) — the freemen-and-burgesses limb |
| Charter under the 1883 Act | none granted (per Done thread `rec9SjWPS9sGLGk1U`) | granted |
| Governing officer today | Portreeve, with two Common Attorneys | Warden, with High Steward and Macebearers |
| Court | Big Court and Little Court | a Court meeting four times a year with the Sergeant of the Mace |
| Self-description | "the Corporation" | "the Old Corporation" |

The shape of the two survivals is genuinely similar — an ancient landholding body, a court that still sits, offices filled annually, and an estate now regulated as a charity. **The honest difference is one of continuity of office rather than of existence:** Malmesbury's town government passed to a reformed borough council in 1886, with a Mayor, four Aldermen and twelve Councillors, and what remained of the old body was the land and the ranks. Laugharne's corporation was not replaced by anything; it simply continued.

That is a defensible and more interesting claim than "the only one left", and it does not depend on a count nobody has verified.

## 5. What the town's own memory got wrong — a calibration

Both Malmesbury sources say the reform was the "Municipal Reform Act of **1886**". There was no such Act. The statute is the Municipal Corporations Act **1883**, Royal Assent 29 June 1883, which came into force in 1886.

The Athelstan Museum's Governance page is worse than a single slip: within one page it gives 1835, 1883 and 1886 for what is evidently the same event, and calls the statute both "the Municipal Reform Act of 1886" and "the reform act of 1886".

This is worth logging as more than pedantry. **A town's institutional memory of the statute that reshaped it can be a decade out, on the town's own website, in 2026.** The same discount rate applies to Laugharne's oral account of its own charter date, its courts and its offices — which is precisely why this desk holds the rule that we never print 1278, 1290 or 1307 as the charter date, and say "late thirteenth century" instead.

The Warden and Freemen's own claim that their rights were preserved "by an Order in Council of Queen Victoria" is likewise a memory, not a citation. It may well be right — MCA 1883 s.6 as enacted provided exactly such a Privy Council route, and charity 250610's governing document names a Privy Council Scheme of 18 October 1886, in the right month of the right year. **The dates fit and nothing else does yet.** The instrument has not been read, and the register names it only as a governing instrument of the almshouse charity, not as an instrument affecting the Warden and Freemen as a body. Logged as F180-10, a deliberate non-finding, so that a later pass cannot mistake the coincidence for proof.

## 6. Method notes worth keeping

**The Charity Commission register has one readable surface.** The ordinary charity-details view does not return content to a plain fetch. The `/full-print` view does, complete with governing document, objects, area of benefit, trustee table, financial history and contact block. Route, for any charity number NNNNNN:

```
https://register-of-charities.charitycommission.gov.uk/en/charity-search/-/charity-details/NNNNNN/full-print
```

**The register's own search form was not used and is not needed.** The charity was found by a domain-restricted web search that surfaced the `/full-print` URL directly. The search engine's *summary* of that page named the governing document correctly — but the summary was not trusted; the page was fetched and read. That distinction is the whole discipline.

**legislation.gov.uk gives you two different documents and you need both.** The revised text tells you what survives and carries the textual-amendment notes; the *enacted* text is the only place the original wording lives. On this Act the difference is total: s.20 appears in the revised text as a row of dots and a repeal note, and nowhere else. The enacted section pages sit under a different path shape (`/ukpga/1883/18/section/20/enacted`) from the revised ones (`/ukpga/Vict/46-47/18/section/20`), and the enacted table of contents is the reliable way to discover section rubrics.
