## 0. Why this target

Docs 216, 221 and 223 each named the same un-done Island House lead: *what became of the house after the 1900 estate auction, when "Capt. T. Jones, Llanelly" bought it for the estate's Island House lot* — the next link in the twentieth-century ownership chain that runs on toward the Walters ownership recorded for 1964 (Doc 61). Doc 216 left two things open: (a) whether the 1900 buyer was a real resident or a speculator, and (b) the identity question, flagged because "Thomas Jones" is onomastically weak. This pass works that lead on Welsh Newspapers Online (WNO), under strict contiguity discipline, and resolves the C20 occupancy spine from 1900 to 1910.

This is squarely in the Island House lane: the house itself and the people who owned and occupied it. Where the evidence touches the town fabric (the church, the Grist, the RDC), that material is flagged as House-of-Culture territory and is not chased here.

## 2. The occupancy spine, 1900–1910

**1900 — the purchase (established in Doc 216).** At the 13 August 1900 break-up auction of the Hughes-Garbett Laugharne estate at Brown's Hotel, the Island House lot was knocked down to "Capt. T. Jones, Llanelly."

**1902 — Capt. Thomas Jones, shipowner, in residence (F225-1, Verified).** The *Carmarthen Journal* of 19 December 1902 (p.8) reports a Carmarthen County Court trespass suit: *"Captain Thomas Jones, shipowner, Island House, Laugharne, v. George Evans, butcher, Market-street, Laugharne."* His solicitors are **Williams & Watkins of Llanelly** — the same Llanelly connection that attaches to the 1900 buyer. The 1900 purchaser is thereby confirmed as a real owner-resident of Island House, and the two figures are the same man. (Standing caution retained: the name is common; it is the *combination* that carries the identification.)

**1905 — still at the house (F225-2, Verified).** *The Welshman* of 14 July 1905 (p.7) adds "Capt. Jones, Island House" to the Laugharne regatta committee; and in June 1905 the *Evening Express* (23 June p.3, repeated 24 June p.4) records that "Captain Jones, of Island House," using grappling irons, recovered the body of a drowned boy, Irvine Edwardes, from the estuary. He is unambiguously resident in mid-1905.

**By January 1908 — ownership has passed to Thomas Titley (F225-3, Verified-strong).** Three independent papers reporting the 1908 Corran Brook drainage dispute name, in a single clause, *"Mr. Thos. Titley, the owner of Island House, Laugharne, and Major Schneider, the occupier of the same"* (*Carmarthen Journal* 6 March 1908 p.5; *Carmarthen Weekly Reporter* 13 March 1908; *The Welshman* 13 March 1908 p.2). Titley's solicitors wrote from 4 Bedford Row, London, and he corresponded by registered letter: he is an **absentee owner-landlord**, letting the house. The change of hands from Capt. Jones therefore falls **between 1905 and early 1908**.

**1907–1908 — Major Schneider, occupier (Schneider entity; F225-3).** Independently of the drainage case, "Major Schneider, of Island House" appears repeatedly in the Carmarthen press of 1907–1908 as an active townsman — chairing a flower-show meeting, serving as the show's secretary, and petitioning the Portreeve's jury and the RDC to plant street trees. His household included Mrs Schneider and a visitor, Miss Skinner.

**1909–1910 — Major Congreve, occupier (F225-5, Verified).** By September 1909 the flower-show secretary is "Major Congreve, of Island House" (*Carmarthen Journal* 3 September 1909, p.8); and at the December 1910 flood "Island House, the residence of Major Congreave to the north" was inundated (*Carmarthen Journal* 23 December 1910, p.6). Congreve is the second tenant under Titley's ownership.

**Result:** after 1900 Island House ceased to be an owner-occupied gentleman's house and became a **let property** — owned by an absentee (Jones sold to Titley by 1908) and lived in by a succession of military tenants (Schneider, then Congreve).

## 3. The house and its setting (F225-4, Verified)

The 1908 dispute also fixes the house's physical setting. The Corran Brook *"flowed all round the premises"*; the Island House **garden reached a south-west corner adjoining the Mill tail race**, where Titley disputed a footbridge and an arch the RDC had built (he claimed the arch stood by his permission). The December 1910 flood — which submerged the Cross on the Grist to within a foot of its top and flooded Island House "to the north" — corroborates the low-lying, water-girt position at the Frog-street / Grist end of the town. This is durable site/fabric detail; **no access or step-free claim is made** (the ground is precisely the kind that floods).

## 4. A named pre-1900 occupant: Frederick Wienholt (F225-6, Verified as a press statement)

A separate 1910 item pushes the spine *backward*. Reporting the gift of a church screen, the *Carmarthen Journal* (1 April 1910, p.8) names *"the late Mr. Frederick Wienholt, of Island House, uncle of the donors… always considered one of the most charitable gentlemen who ever lived in Laugharne. When the church was restored about 37 years ago it was he who bore the whole expense of repairing the tower, north and south transepts, and main porch,"* and built the infants' school. "About 37 years" before 1910 is **c.1873**, placing a Wienholt at Island House a generation before Percy Hughes-Garbett (resident c.1890–1900, Docs 207/209/212). This is a genuinely new name on the occupancy spine.

Two disciplines apply. First, the **church-restoration detail is House-of-Culture territory** (St Martin's fabric — see Docs 162/215); it is carried here only as evidence of Wienholt's *Island House residence*, not chased. Second, his exact place in the pre-1890 sequence — and whether the Wienholts owned or merely occupied — is **not yet pinned** and is an archive-day question.

## 5. What this resolves, and what remains

**Resolved / advanced:**
- Doc 216's open question (a): the 1900 buyer was a real owner-resident, not a speculator — Capt. Thomas Jones, shipowner, lived at Island House 1900×1905.
- Doc 216's open question (b): the 1900 "Capt. T. Jones, Llanelly" and the 1902 "Captain Thomas Jones, shipowner, Island House" are the same man (Llanelly link recurs), to a Verified standard, caution noted.
- The 1900s ownership chain is now: **Jones (owner-resident, 1900–c.1905) → Titley (absentee owner, by 1908) → [tenants Schneider 1907–08, Congreve 1909–10]**, a clean link toward the mid-C20 Walters ownership (1964, Doc 61).
- A new, earlier occupant — **Frederick Wienholt, c.1873** — is registered for the pre-Hughes-Garbett sequence.

**Remains (archive- or portal-gated, not desk-runnable):**
1. The **Jones→Titley conveyance** and Titley's acquisition deed — solicitors' or estate papers; would date the sale precisely.
2. **Who Thomas Titley was**, and whether he ever sold to an occupier or held the house long-term into the 1920s–30s.
3. **Frederick Wienholt's** dates and tenure, and his position relative to the Hughes-Garbetts.
4. The **1856 Island House sketch** in Curtis, Part IV (Doc 221 next-target).
