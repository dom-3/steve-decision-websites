## 1. The finding in five lines

1. **The thread's question is answered YES.** The Watkinses of Brecon and Pennoyre (Breconshire) and the Watkinses of Broadway (Carmarthenshire) are **one family**, and Pennoyre Watkins compiled its genealogy himself, c. 1751.
2. **Broadway was BOUGHT, not inherited.** Theophilus Jones states the Broadway estate near Laugharne was *"bought of a descendant of Judge Powel"* — which corrects our register's standing framing of a Powell-to-Watkins **descent**.
3. **"G. P. Watkins" expands** to George Pryse (or Price) Watkins, b. 4 July 1752 o.s., d. 23 May 1843 aged 91 — Pennoyre's eldest son, to whom Broadway was devised.
4. **Our register's "Pennoyre Watkins, d. by 1787 (will proved)" is REFUTED.** Two objects put his death in 1791 or 1792. The 1787 date attached to Elizabeth Ravenscroft's inheritance cannot be a probate date for him.
5. **A ninth search trap was found and nearly cost us a true finding** — line-break masking in the browser text layer produced a clean false negative on a phrase that was actually present.

## 3. The evidence, verbatim

### 3.1 One family, not two — NLW MS 13849E

Scope and content:

> Genealogical memoranda, 1721-1851, relating to the Watkins family of Brecon and Pennoyre, co. Brecon, and of Broadway, co. Carmarthen compiled, c. 1751, by Pennoyre Watkins, with additions by his son, the Reverend Thomas Watkins and a later hand.

Subject access points include **"Broadway (Carmarthenshire, Wales) -- Genealogy."**; name access points include an authority record **"Watkins family, of Broadway"**. The creator authority record is **"Watkins, Pennoyre, 1721-1791"**.

This is decisive at catalogue level without needing the manuscript: the family that Pennoyre Watkins wrote up as *his own* is catalogued as the Brecon–Pennoyre–**Broadway** family.

### 3.2 Pennoyre Watkins himself styled "of Broadway" — St Mary's, Brecon

Jones II, p. 90, north transept monument (read direct):

> In memory of Mary, wife of Pennoyre Watkins, of this town, and Broadway, in the County of Carmarthen, Esquire. She was the youngest daughter of David Lloyd of RhosfPerrig, in this county, Esquire She died lamented by all who knew her December 27, 1762, in the 34th year of her age.

This is the cleanest single attachment of **Pennoyre Watkins himself** — not merely his family, not merely his son — to Broadway in Carmarthenshire.

Because S1 and S3 are independent of each other (an archival catalogue and a monumental inscription, neither derived from the other), **F183-1 is tiered Verified-strong**.

### 3.3 ⭐ The load-bearing passage — Broadway was bought from a Powell

Jones II, p. 198, section "PENNOYRE, AND THE WATKINS FAMILY" (read direct):

> The family of Watkins, from the beginning of the reign of Henry the Seventh, to the end of the seventeenth century, were settled at Llanigon, when they removed to Hay and the neighbourhood. The late Pennoyre Watkins resided at Brecon, where by the practice of the law and by fortunate purchases, he accumulated a very considerable property. The Broadway estate near Laugharne in Caermarthenshire, bought of a descendant of Judge Powel, so well known for his conduct in favour of the bishops, in the time of James the Second, he devised to his eldest son, George Pryse Watkins, esq., and principal part of the Breconshire estate to his son Thomas.

Three things fall out of this sentence.

**(a) The mechanism is a purchase, not a descent.** Our entity note on Broadway Mansion (`recwprmovw8hbmuyd`) has carried "the Powell-to-Watkins **descent**" as a standing research target. Jones says the Watkinses **bought** it. This is not a quibble — it redirects the search. A descent is evidenced by a pedigree and a marriage; a purchase is evidenced by a **conveyance**. Anyone continuing this thread should be looking for a deed in the Broadway or Pennoyre estate papers, not for a marriage settlement.

**(b) "Judge Powel" is Sir John Powell** (1633–1696), the Seven Bishops judge buried at Laugharne — already in the register from Docs 139 and 172. The purchase is therefore from one of his descendants, a generation or more after 1696, which is chronologically comfortable for a buyer who was born in 1721.

**(c) Pennoyre Watkins was a lawyer who bought into gentry, not a gentleman who inherited it.** "By the practice of the law and by fortunate purchases." This changes the *social* character of the Watkins arrival at Laugharne, and it is worth getting right in any copy: a Brecon lawyer buying a Carmarthenshire seat is a different story from a county family succeeding to one.

Tiered **Verified**, not Verified-strong: this is one source, a county historian writing over a century after the event, who does not cite the instrument.

### 3.4 "G. P. Watkins" expanded

Jones II, p. 90 (read direct):

> This monument is erected by his nephew and executor, Lloyd Vaughan Watkins, Esq., M.P., to the memory of George Price Watkins, of Rhosferrig, in this county, and Broadway, in the county of Carmarthen, Esquire, who was born July 4, 1752 (old style), and died May 23rd, 1843, at the advanced age of 91.

This answers the standing action to expand "G. P." (A152-2 / A153-2).

⚠️ **Spelling conflict inside one volume**: "George **Pryse** Watkins" at p. 198, "George **Price** Watkins" at pp. 90–91. Both recorded; neither standardised.

### 3.5 The estate split

Broadway to the elder son, the principal part of Breconshire to Thomas. From the early 1790s the **Laugharne (Broadway) Watkinses are a distinct line** from the Breconshire (Pennoyre) Watkinses. This is immediately useful: the "G. Watkins, Esq." named impropriator of Laugharne parish in 1849 (`recaH29EDNeowt36j`) should be tested against the Broadway line first.

## 4. What this REFUTES in our own register

Entity `recygbBN64rSMZE62` currently reads **"d. by 1787 (will proved)"**. Entity `recYwgaUC3Leb5puE` says Elizabeth Ravenscroft *"Inherited Laugharne Castle and its grounds in 1787 under the will of her grandfather Pennoyre Watkins."*

Both objects read in this pass put his death **after** 1787:

- NLW authority heading: **1721-1791**
- Llandefaelog wall slab, Jones II p. 186, read direct: *"of Penoyre Watkins, Esq., a good and a great man, who lived from 1721 to 1792, known and respected in this his native country."*

A man cannot have a will proved in 1787 and die in 1791 or 1792.

**Three possibilities, none tested here:**

1. The 1787 transaction is **not a probate**. A marriage settlement or a lifetime conveyance would fit the facts perfectly — and would be the more interesting answer, since it would mean Elizabeth Ravenscroft took Laugharne Castle from a living grandfather.
2. The 1787 inheritance came from a **different grantor** and has been mis-attributed to Pennoyre Watkins.
3. The 1787 date is simply wrong.

**Per the additive-only rule, this pass did not edit the Elizabeth Ravenscroft record.** It is flagged for a human. **The "1787 will" framing must not be repeated in copy until this is settled.**

### 4.1 And the death year itself is not settled either

1791 (NLW) versus 1792 (the slab). The birth year agrees; the death year does not. Neither object is decisive — the NLW record is an unsourced heading, and the slab is a later commemorative tablet, not a burial register, giving no day or month. **No monument or burial entry for Pennoyre Watkins himself was found in either volume.**

**Resolving route, not run:** the Llandefaelog parish burial register, or a probate grant in the NLW / PCC indexes for 1791–1793. Until then, copy should say **"died in the early 1790s"** and never print a bare numeral.

## 5. Two more conflicts opened, honestly

Sarah d. David Lloyd of Rhosferig."* Both cannot be right as written. A third possibility not yet tested: **Sarah is the daughter, not the wife** — which would sit neatly with Pennoyre Watkins being Elizabeth Ravenscroft's *grandfather*, as our register already has it. Not resolved. Neither name is publishable as settled.

Danet Penoyre of the Moor"* — suggesting "Pennoyre" entered the family as a surname taken as a forename, exactly the pattern Doc 175 established for "Abra" in the Hughes line. Plausible, unproven.

## 7. METHOD — the NINTH SEARCH TRAP: line-break masking

**This trap almost destroyed a true finding in this very run, and it is the most important thing in this document.**

Verifying the relayed quote about the Broadway purchase against the archive.org full text, an exact-phrase search for `Broadway estate near Laugharne` returned **NOT FOUND**. So did `fortunate purchases`, `devised to his eldest`, and `Pryse Watkins`. Three clean negatives in a row on the single most load-bearing sentence in the pass.

The provisional conclusion — *the relayed quote was fabricated* — **was wrong.**

A frequency check on the component words showed them all present: "fortunate" ×30, "devised" ×22, "Pryse" ×2. The cause: **OCR line-wrapping inserts newlines mid-phrase**, and `document.body.innerText` preserves them, so an exact `indexOf` spanning a line break fails silently. Normalising whitespace first found every passage instantly:

```js
const n = document.body.innerText.replace(/\s+/g, ' ');
n.indexOf('fortunate purchases');   // found
```

Raw text: **1,564,111** characters. Normalised: **1,306,958**. Roughly **257,000 whitespace characters** were sitting inside the text, any one of which could break a phrase search.

**THE RULE: on any OCR full-text surface, normalise whitespace before phrase-searching, and never record a phrase-level negative from a raw `indexOf`.**

This is distinct from the traps already on the desk. Trap 4 (Doc 168) is OCR *spelling* variants. Trap 6 (Doc 171) is tool-level blindness. Here the tool works, the spelling is right, and the text is present — **only the whitespace differs**.

**The corollary is the uncomfortable one.** The verification step existed to catch a fabricated relay. Because of this trap it very nearly produced the opposite and worse error: recording a *true* quote as fabrication, and discarding the best finding of the pass. A verification method that can invert like that needs its own control. The control is cheap: when a phrase search fails, **check whether the component words are present** before concluding the passage is absent.
