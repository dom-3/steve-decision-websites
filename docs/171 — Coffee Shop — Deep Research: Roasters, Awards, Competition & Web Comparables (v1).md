## 0. The one-page version

The bar in west Wales is already high and already specialty-literate — people drive to Wright's, Crwst, Ginhaus and Coaltown for a *total experience*, not just a cup. **Coaltown Coffee in Ammanford — same county, ~30 miles, the UK's first B Corp roaster, a 2026 SCA design-award winner, official La Marzocco service partner for South Wales, with a "black gold / liquid gold" mining-heritage story — is the standout supply route.** The catch: Coaltown is *already* poured in Laugharne (by Poon's) and at Wright's, so serving it is table stakes, not a differentiator, unless we pair it with a far better room, consistency and takeaway — or add a guest single-origin from a national elite roaster (Origin, Round Hill) to signal curation.

The award most worth chasing is not exotic: a **Great Taste star on a signature house blend, entered the moment the 2027 window opens (~early December 2026)** — cheap (~£77), every entry can win, and Food & Drink Wales is the headline sponsor so a Welsh winner gets picked up. The "Golden Bean UK" named in the original brief **does not exist**; the real UK roasting title is the SCA UK Coffee Roasting Championship, which is a roaster's competition and a long game.

The genuine gap in Laugharne: **nobody treats coffee itself as the point.** There is a heritage tearoom pouring only filter (the Boathouse), a traditional cake-tearoom (Owl & the Pussycat), a good fair-trade deli (the Ferryman), one rough-edged specialty café (Poon's), and premium pubs. No design-led, early-opening, seven-day, specialty-grade coffee house engineered for both the estuary sit-in crowd and grab-and-go walkers. That is exactly the third Globe door.

**Correction to the brief:** the daytime competitor set is not "Brown's, Dexter's, the Boathouse tearoom." "Dexters" is *Dexters at Brown's* — a farm-to-fork steakhouse, not a café. The real daytime rivals are the **Ferryman deli, Poon's (specialty), the Owl & the Pussycat (tearoom), and the Boathouse tearoom (filter only).**

## 1. Supply — who roasts the liquid gold

### The Welsh story: Coaltown is the lead
Coaltown Coffee (Ammanford, SA18 — same county, ~30 miles / 45 min from Laugharne) is the single strongest route for a venue that wants both quality and an authentic Welsh story. It was the UK's first B Corp-certified specialty roaster (2019, B impact score 98.0 against a ~50.9 median), a Real Living Wage employer, and won the SCA World of Coffee 2026 Design Award for branding. Its identity is built on Ammanford's anthracite mining past reborn as coffee — "black gold / liquid gold" is literally their line, which is uncannily on-brief. Their trade offer is turnkey: they are **La Marzocco's official machine and servicing partner for South Wales**, run a UK-wide engineer network, train baristas at the in-house Coaltown Academy, and provide POS/branding. They frame it as "partnership, not contracts" — which is warm, but must be pinned down in writing. Their exact per-kg price and any tie/exclusivity terms are not published.

The one strategic risk with Coaltown is ubiquity: it is *already* poured in Laugharne (Poon's) and at Wright's Food Emporium in Llanarthne. Serving Coaltown does not let us claim "the first specialty coffee in Laugharne." It lets us out-execute on room, consistency, hours and takeaway — and we should consider a guest single-origin on a second grinder to add a curation layer.

**Welsh runners-up:** Hard Lines (Cardiff, ~50 miles) — cult modern roaster, verified 6kg minimum with equipment, training and account support, strong "Welsh but urban-cool" brand; field support in west Wales less proven than Coaltown's. Poblado Coffi (Gwynedd) — good quality, but north Wales and a smaller support operation. James Gourmet (Ross-on-Wye, just over the border) — respected, discreet, quality-led food-service roaster with no loud consumer brand; good if we want quality without a co-brand, but it forfeits the Welsh-provenance angle. At the hyper-local micro end: Alfie's Coffee (Carmarthenshire, a Great Taste gold star, tiny scale), Capital Roasters (Pembroke Dock), Gourmet Pig (Fishguard), Allaways (Ceredigion — notably offers machine servicing).

**National elite (for quality/prestige or a guest slot):** Origin (Porthleven, Cornwall) — one of the two or three most respected UK roasters, Loring roasters, full SCA training school, deliverable to west Wales by courier; premium, no Welsh story, servicing relies on third-party engineers in SA33. Round Hill Roastery (near Bath) — cult single-farm boutique, closest of the English elite to Laugharne. Square Mile (London, James Hoffmann) — the most influential UK name, coffee-led rather than hospitality-hand-holding. Assembly, Workshop, North Star (Leeds B Corp), Extract (Bristol, pragmatic and deliverable) round out the credible field.

**Unverified:** "Dead Man's Jacket" (named in an earlier note) — no current Welsh roaster of that name found; treat as defunct/mis-remembered.

### How the trade model actually works
Four models exist: simple per-kg with no tie; **machine-loan-for-exclusivity** (roaster lends a 2-group machine, sometimes a grinder, in return for 2–5 years' exclusive purchasing — removes roughly **£10,000–£18,000** of opening capital, but watch buyout clauses and mandated engineers); **flexible tiered pricing** with no tie (the quality-independent norm, price falls with volume); and white-label (only viable at ~20kg/week+, a growth-stage play). Verified 2026 specialty price is roughly **£16–£24/kg**, with exceptional lots higher; budget **£20–£26/kg** for a serious house espresso. Minimum orders are small (Hard Lines starts at 6kg) and not a barrier. The decisive rural-location factor: a Welsh roaster can deliver on its own vans and send its own engineer, whereas national roasters ship beans by courier but leave on-site servicing to a third-party network — a real argument for Coaltown or another nearby roaster in SA33.

### What makes a programme "award quality" from supply
A seasonal house espresso blend on the main grinder (consistent, milk-friendly) plus a rotating single origin on a second grinder is the professional norm — two grinders is itself the "we're serious" signal. Milk is where most cups are won or lost, and west Wales hands us a genuine local-dairy story: **Totally Welsh** (Haverfordwest, milk from farms within ~40 miles, daily delivery across west Wales), Carningli Dairy, and the Welsh Milk co-op; specify a higher-fat barista whole milk and a good barista oat (Minor Figures / Oatly standard) as the default plant option. Decaf must be done properly — Swiss Water or sugarcane/EA process, never a cheap solvent decaf. Equipment that reads as serious: La Marzocco (Linea PB / KB90), Victoria Arduino (Eagle One, Black Eagle) or Sanremo on espresso, paired with Mahlkönig grinders — and the grinder plus freshness plus the person dialling in matter more than the badge on the machine.

**Three ranked supply routes:**
1. **Best Welsh story (lead recommendation): Coaltown** — same county, B Corp, award pedigree, La Marzocco service partner, academy, perfect narrative. *Trade-off:* you fly a strong co-brand and it's already poured locally, so the room and execution must do the differentiating.
2. **Best outright quality/prestige: Origin (with Round Hill as the boutique alternative)** — the names coffee people respect most. *Trade-off:* premium pricing, no Welsh story, courier-plus-third-party servicing in SA33.
3. **Best balanced terms: Hard Lines or Extract** — low minimums, equipment + training + support, deliverable. *Trade-off:* Hard Lines' west-Wales support footprint is less proven; Extract loses the Welsh angle.

**Suggested play:** lead with Coaltown for beans, equipment, servicing and story; run a rotating guest single-origin from Origin or Round Hill on a second grinder for range and curation credibility; pair with Totally Welsh milk and a Swiss Water decaf.

## 2. The awards route ("liquid gold")

### Great Taste is the fast, credible win — enter December 2026
Great Taste (Guild of Fine Food) is the flagship and the right first target. Products are blind-tasted **on their own merit, not against each other**, so every entry can win a 1-, 2- or 3-star rating; coffee is entered as beans or ground (not brewed), and — importantly — **we do not have to roast it ourselves**: a house blend produced under our name by a partner roaster qualifies. Entry is roughly **£77 per product** for a small non-member business (£49 as a member). The 2026 cycle opened 2 Dec 2025 and closed 27 Jan 2026, with results in late July 2026; the **2027 window will mirror this (open ~early December 2026, close ~late January 2027)** and places are capped first-come-first-served — so we enter the moment it opens, not at the deadline, ideally with two or three SKUs. Food & Drink Wales is the headline sponsor, so a Welsh winner gets Business Wales PR on top of the star.

### "Golden Bean UK" does not exist — correction
The original brief named Golden Bean UK. There is no verifiable UK edition; Golden Bean runs Australasia, the Americas and East Asia only. The genuine UK roasting title is the **SCA UK Coffee Roasting Championship** — but that is a roaster's competition (you must roast) and a long game. Treat "Golden Bean UK" as a misconception.

### The Welsh and regional route
English regional schemes exclude Wales (Taste of the West is Cornwall-to-Wiltshire; Beautiful South is South-East England) — so our regional route is explicitly Welsh. **Food & Drink Awards Wales** is a business-format award (a 15-minute judged interview, not a blind tasting) that rewards story, provenance and business quality — a strong fit for a Welsh independent with a Laugharne/Dylan Thomas narrative; the 2027 entry window likely opens autumn 2026. Public-vote awards — the **Welsh Café/Coffee Shop of the Year** (Oceanic; "Best Coffee Shop in Wales" went to Barry's Coastal Coffee in 2026) and **Muddy Stilettos** (which has a Wales region) — are low-cost, winnable by mobilising a customer base, and give usable headlines. Note: **True Taste Wales is discontinued** (ended 2012) and Blas Cymru/Taste Wales is a trade conference, not an awards scheme.

### The long game
SCA UK competitions (Barista, Brewers Cup, Latte Art, Roasting) are individual-skill national championships with tiny fields that sell out in seconds — huge specialty credibility, but they need a dedicated trained competitor and months of prep. Hold them for year two-plus. Venue/trade awards (London Coffee Festival "Best Independent Coffee Shop," BRITA Grounds of Innovation) and AA/Michelin listing for the Globe as a whole are pursuable once there's a track record and a consistent five-star online reputation.

### The model to study
**Coaltown, 30 miles up the road, is the blueprint:** multiple Great Taste stars, first B Corp coffee roaster in Wales, Most Sustainable Coffee Roasters at the 2021 Welsh Enterprise Awards, and the 2026 SCA design award — the exact combination of technical quality + provenance story + ethics that wins across both blind-tasting and narrative awards. Crwst (won on hospitality/welcome) and Barry's Coastal Coffee (won on reputation) show the public-vote route.

**Sequenced award strategy in one line:** lock a signature blend now → Great Taste star (enter winter 2026) → Welsh business + public-vote awards (2027) → national café/trade awards → SCA barista/roasting as the prestige long game.

## 3. Local & regional competition

### Inside Laugharne
- **The Ferryman Delicatessen** (King Street) — the current quality benchmark in town: a licensed deli-café with an in-house barista pouring a fair-trade house blend, cheese/charcuterie, local pies and cake. 4.7 Tripadvisor (#4 of 15). *Gap it leaves:* fair-trade commodity blend, not seasonal specialty; "deli with good coffee," not a design-led coffee room.
- **Poon's Street Food** (Market Street) — the specialty threat: already pours **Coaltown**, open ~9–5 daily, Thai takeaway some evenings. Reviews mixed/inconsistent (~181). *Status for July 2026 unverified — confirm it still trades and still pours Coaltown.* Significance: Laugharne already has award-winning Welsh specialty coffee, so we out-execute rather than claim a first.
- **The Owl & the Pussycat** (The Grist) — beloved traditional tearoom, homemade cakes, bara brith, ~784 reviews at 4.8 (high footfall). *Gap it leaves:* classic cake-tearoom, not specialty, not design-led.
- **The Dylan Thomas Boathouse tearoom** (Dylan's Walk) — council-run, best view in town, open 10–5 Thu–Mon (closed Tue/Wed), serves **only filter coffee, no espresso**. *Gap it leaves:* heritage tearoom pouring filter, opens at 10am (misses early walkers), shut two days a week — huge passing footfall it under-converts on coffee.
- **Brown's Hotel / Dexters** (King Street) — the upscale pub/hotel/steakhouse; "great coffee" but daytime identity is guest breakfast + bar + evening grill, not a walk-in coffee counter.
- **Pubs pouring coffee:** New Three Mariners (pours Cariad coffee), The Cross House Inn, The Carpenters Arms — all pub, evening-weighted. **Food-led/takeaway:** Tŷ Glo (stone-baked pizza, ~4.9), Milkwood House (estuary views), chippy/burger takeaways. **Corran Books** — antiquarian bookshop, effectively online-only now; not live footfall.

### Footfall (proxies only — no town-level data published)
Laugharne is ~2,700 residents plus a literary-tourism flow. **Laugharne Castle (Cadw): ~14,620 visitors in 2018** is the last reliable order-of-magnitude (post-2019 numbers not published; source is a secondary aggregator — treat as approximate). The **Boathouse** is a significant international draw but publishes no current admissions figure — any specific number is unverified. The **Dylan Thomas Birthday Walk** channels a steady year-round pedestrian flow along a defined estuary route (directly relevant to takeaway-for-walkers). The **Laugharne Weekend** festival (March; capped at ~500) is a high-value but small, single-weekend spike, not volume. Seasonality is the classic west-Wales coastal profile: summer- and weekend-weighted, quiet mid-week and winter — the business must survive winter mid-weeks on local trade, then capture the tourist surge.

### The regional bar (within ~40 miles — proof people drive for quality)
Wright's Food Emporium (Llanarthne, ~15 miles) is the key benchmark: a destination deli/café in a former roadside pub that people drive across Wales to reach, pouring Coaltown filter — proof that destination status comes from the total experience, not the location. Ginhaus Deli (Llandeilo) runs the deli+café dual-revenue model with a signature "wall of gin" hook and celebrated bakes. Crwst (Cardigan) is a design-forward, bakery-led brand that scaled to multiple sites on brunch, doughnuts and coffee. The Warren (Carmarthen) built loyal trade on values/community/provenance. Coaltown's own roastery-canteen in Ammanford is a coffee-tourism destination in its own right. (Manorafon — could not verify a current operating café; do not rely on it.)

### The gap, in one paragraph
Laugharne has plenty of places to get a coffee but none that treats coffee itself as the point. Between a captive heritage tearoom pouring filter, a beloved traditional cake-tearoom, a good fair-trade deli, one rough-around-the-edges specialty café, and premium pubs, the town lacks a design-led, all-day, seven-day, early-opening specialty coffee house that pours destination-grade coffee (Coaltown-level or better), backs it with a serious bakery, and is engineered for both the sit-and-linger estuary crowd and grab-and-go walkers heading to the Boathouse. That is the white space the Globe daytime should occupy: the first proper specialty coffee house in Laugharne — earlier, better-designed, more consistent and more coffee-serious than anything currently open — turning the town's existing literary footfall into a daytime destination in its own right.

## 4. Website comparables

The best coffee sites lead with **place, people and provenance** and let commerce follow; generic ones lead with product and price. Ten repeatable moves stood out: one ownable origin story told in photography (not a paragraph); provenance on every product (origin/process/varietal/tasting notes, never just "House Blend"); a "Visit/Find Us" page that sells the place like a magazine spread; a named good-better-best range architecture; a journal for compounding SEO; an intent-led shop with a "help me choose" quiz; frictionless subscription as the default upsell; a single dignified wholesale enquiry funnel; borrowed-credibility walls (press logos, B Corp, review counts); and a consistent product-shot system.

The shortlist to put in front of Steve and Dominic:

| # | Site | Why this one | The one thing to borrow |
|---|------|-------------|------------------------|
| 1 | **Coaltown** — coaltowncoffee.co.uk | West-Wales neighbour; 2026 SCA branding award — proves heritage-industrial storytelling wins at the top level, in our region | The single-story provenance block (archival B&W heritage photo → purpose → producer). Do the Dylan-Thomas/Laugharne version. |
| 2 | **WatchHouse** — watchhouse.com | The definitive "one brand, many doors" execution and the premium design ceiling | Naming venue types ("Brunch Houses / Espresso Houses") under one system with a reconciling tagline — the template for the Globe's three doors. |
| 3 | **Origin** — origincoffee.co.uk | Closest tonal + business match: premium rural B Corp roaster with cafés inside cultural venues (British Library) | A Locations page that sells each place as a destination, plus named-producer profiles. |
| 4 | **Crwst** — crwst.cymru | Nearest format twin: a Welsh destination bakery-café-deli on a lean, warm site | Tight structure + bilingual `.cymru` authenticity — proof we don't need to over-build. |
| 5 | **Ozone** — ozonecoffee.co.uk | Best framing of café-as-equal-pillar (Roasting / Sourcing / **Hosting**) | The three co-equal pillars homepage model; consider a house illustrator instead of photography. |
| 6 | **Square Mile** — shop.squaremilecoffee.com | The restraint benchmark — expertise-first, minimal, confident | Product-page discipline: origin + process + tasting notes, iconic names, nothing extraneous. Premium = less. |

The "one brand, many doors" angle is the most valuable for us: **WatchHouse** literally names its door-types under one identity and places Houses inside cultural destinations (Somerset House); **Ozone** frames Hosting as co-equal with Roasting and Sourcing; **Origin** merchandises the experience of visiting a café tied to a cultural institution. The template for the Globe is a shared visual system, a house-name per door ("The Globe — Coffee / — Table / — Bakery"), a reconciling tagline, and a top-nav that lets a visitor hop between doors.
