## 0. Why this thread and not another

Doc 169b established that the Bridget Bevan / Island House association is **unsourced**. That was a finding about silence. Silence is a weak position: it is always vulnerable to the next person who says "ah, but everyone knows Madam Bevan lived at Island House," because an absence of evidence invites the tradition to fill it back in.

This thread was spun out of Doc 169b to convert that silence into something stronger. The question is not "is the association proved?" — Doc 169b answered that — but **"if not Island House, then which house?"** A named rival candidate is worth far more than a shrug, because it gives the claim somewhere else to go.

The target sits squarely in the Island House lane: it tests an association attached to Island House itself. The Great House appears in this document only as the rival, and is handed on rather than pursued.

## 1. The two authoritative objects, read in full

### 1.1 DWB — Griffith Jones (Mary Clement, 1959)

Read at object level at `https://biography.wales/article/s-JONE-GRI-1683`. The operative sentence, verbatim:

> "Griffith Jones's wife, Margaret, described as a pious and charitable woman, died 5 January 1755; Griffith Jones died 8 April 1761, aged 77, at Madam Bevan's home in Laugharne, where he had resided after his wife's death. Both were buried in Llanddowror church."

**The house is not named.**

The article also yields a fact that belongs to a different open thread and is handed to it below:

> "His first curacies were at Penbryn, Cardiganshire (1708), Penrieth, Pembrokeshire (1709), and Laugharne, Carmarthenshire (1709), where he was also master of a S.P.C.K. school founded by Sir John Philipps and John Pember."

### 1.2 DWB — Bridget Bevan (Mary Clement, 1953), Welsh edition

Read at object level at `https://www.bywgraffiadur.cymru/article/s-BEVA-BRI-1698`. Verbatim:

> "Ychydig ar ôl marwolaeth ei wraig yn 1755, aeth Griffith Jones i fyw yng nghartref Mrs. Bevan yn Lacharn, lle y bu farw 8 Ebrill 1761…"

("A little after his wife's death in 1755, Griffith Jones went to live in Mrs Bevan's *home* in Laugharne, where he died 8 April 1761…")

**The house is not named here either.**

### 1.3 What that double negative is worth — and what it is not worth

Both articles are by **Mary Clement**, the leading scholar of the S.P.C.K. in Wales and editor of the S.P.C.K.-Wales correspondence. She had the S.P.C.K. manuscripts, the Ottley Papers and the Griffith Jones–Bevan letters in front of her. If the house had a name she was confident of, she was the person to give it, and she had two separate articles in which to do so.

The honest qualification, recorded because it cuts against the finding's apparent strength: **this is one author writing in two languages, not two independent witnesses.** The Welsh and English articles are not corroboration of each other. What we have is one very well-placed authority declining to name the house, twice.

That is still the load-bearing point. **Any source that does name the house is going beyond the DWB, and must be made to show its own evidence rather than being allowed to borrow the DWB's authority.**

## 2. The discriminating object: RCAHMW Historic Place Names of Wales

The pass then found something better than silence.

RCAHMW's Historic Place Names of Wales carries a **distinct recorded place-name, "Madam Bevan's House"**, in Laugharne Township. Read at object level at `https://historicplacenames.rcahmw.gov.uk/placenames/recordedname/84c4c385-29e6-4eb8-b12c-a7ea3b7ed913`. Fields verbatim:

| --- | --- |
| Head Name | Undefined |
| Type | Unknown |
| **Grid Reference** | **SN 30315 10850** |
| Parish | Laugharne Township |
| County | Carmarthenshire |
| When recorded | *(blank)* |
| Primary Source | *(blank)* |
| Secondary Source | *Carmarthenshire: An Inventory of the Ancient Monuments in the County*, RCAHMW, 1917 |
| Notes | "House owned by the philantrapist Madam Bevan, pulled down in 1859. Transcribed from RCAHMW Inventory by student volunteers on 20/03/2017." |

("philantrapist" is the record's own spelling.)

### 2.1 The inference, and why it is not promoted

Island House stands. It has never been pulled down. The house RCAHMW records under Madam Bevan's name **was pulled down in 1859**. On the record's own terms, these cannot be the same building.

That inference is clean, and it is **deliberately held at Referenced-pending rather than promoted to Verified**, for three stated reasons:

1. **"Primary Source" is empty.** So is "When recorded". The record does not tell us what document underlies it beyond a general pointer to the 1917 Inventory.
2. **The transcription is by student volunteers.** That is not a criticism of the volunteers; it is a statement about the chain of custody. This is a transcribed secondary record, not a primary read.
3. **The 1917 Inventory itself has not been read.** It is the obvious next object and it was not reached this pass.

### 2.2 The test that was NOT run, recorded as such

The single cheapest remaining test is to **compare SN 30315 10850 against Island House's own grid reference**. That comparison would either close this question in one step or blow the inference open.

**It was not run this pass, and no conclusion is drawn from it.** It is written into the entity record as the first action of the next pass. Nobody should state that the two are different places *on grid evidence* until that read is actually made — the grid reference above is currently a fact about a record, not yet a fact about the ground.

## 3. The rival candidate, and a name-collision trap

Laugharne Lines — the local heritage site — carries a "The Great House" page identifying the Bevan house as a **large demolished house behind the Town Hall**, on the site of the present row of three houses including Engine House, with its stable block on the site of the present Portreeve's restaurant. It states:

> "It had been the home of Zachary Bevan… Arthur Bevan (1691-1743) inherited it and probably enhanced it ready for his marriage to Bridget Vaughan (1698-1779) or 'Madam Bevan'…"

> "Griffith Jones actually moved into The Great House for the last six years of his life so the whole movement was then administered from here."

> "…it was pulled down in 1859."

### 3.1 How much of this to believe

**Not much, at this tier.** Laugharne Lines is T3 and is the very source that open thread `recTNaLHTKQVorA8j` exists to audit — it is where the *refuted* "13th-century cellars" came from. F173-7 is logged at Referenced-pending accordingly.

Two things are nonetheless worth recording:

- The **1859 demolition date agrees with the RCAHMW record**. But this is *not* independent corroboration: both accounts may descend from the same 1917 Inventory. Logged as agreement, not as confirmation.
- The **"last six years" is internally consistent with the DWB** (wife died January 1755, Jones died April 1761 — six years and three months). That corroborates the *chronology*. It says nothing whatever about the *house*.

### 3.2 THE NAME-COLLISION TRAP — flagged for the whole desk

Laugharne Lines states it outright:

> "before those three houses were built a large house stood here - **The Great House (but not Great House!)**"

**"The Great House" (demolished, behind the Town Hall) and "Great House" (standing, Grade II\*, Queen Anne, Cadw-listed) are two different buildings in one small town.**

This is the same shape as the duplicated tithe plot number (Doc 169a) and the Edwards/Edmunds near-collision (Doc 141) — a near-identical name that will silently attach the wrong evidence to the wrong object. **Any source naming a "Great House" at Laugharne must be resolved to one building or the other before the reference is recorded.** Recommended for ratification into Doc 140.

## 4. The negative from the tradition's own mouthpiece

The Laugharne Lines **"Island House"** page was read in full as a control. It is the fullest popular narrative account of Island House in existence, and its occupancy story runs: Wienholt (Edward Wienholt born there 28 March 1833) → Major Congreve from 1903 → Major Thomas Picton Rose-Richards → David "Gosh" Waters → sold 2020, restoration under way.

**It does not mention Madam Bevan, Bridget Bevan, Arthur Bevan or Griffith Jones anywhere.**

The evidential weight of that silence comes from the source's character. This page cheerfully prints a Roman baths, a round tower and a wall "as thick as the castle" — it is demonstrably *not* a reticent source. If a Bevan/Island House tradition were circulating in the town, this is the page that would be carrying it.

It is carrying no such thing. That is evidence about **the tradition**, not about the fabric.

## 5. The method failure caught in the act — and the false negative not recorded

The pass attempted Mary Curtis, *The Antiquities of Laugharne* — cited in the source lists of *both* DWB articles, and already known to this desk as the root of the 1658 door date (Doc 158b). She is the local antiquarian most likely to name the house.

A fetch of the archive.org full-text stream URL returned **80,485 characters across 1,542 lines**. Healthy-looking. A search of it for *Bevan*, *Griffith Jones*, *Island House* and *Llanddowror* returned **zero matches across all four terms**.

That zero was a lie. Inspection of the payload showed it was Internet Archive **page furniture** — menus, donate links, collection listings — and not Curtis's text at all. The `/stream/` view is client-rendered; the fetch succeeded and delivered nothing.

**Had that result been trusted, this document would have recorded a false negative: "Curtis does not mention Madam Bevan."** That is precisely the sixth trap (Doc 171, tool-level blindness) and precisely the reason for the standing rule that no negative may be recorded from a surface without a positive control on that same surface.

**Curtis therefore remains UNREAD, and no finding whatever about Curtis is recorded in this document.**

One further distinction, easily fumbled: **the DWB cites Curtis 1871 (1st edition); the archive.org copy is the 1880 2nd edition.** Different editions. Do not conflate them, and do not cite one for the other.

## 6. Findings

1. **Griffith Jones died 8 April 1761, aged 77, at Madam Bevan's home in Laugharne**, having lived there since his wife Margaret's death on 5 January 1755 — carried by both language editions of the DWB, though by one author. *(Verified)*
2. **Neither DWB article names the house.** The standard national authority, written by the leading S.P.C.K.-in-Wales scholar, declines to name it twice. *(Verified-strong)*
3. **RCAHMW records "Madam Bevan's House" as a distinct place-name** at SN 30315 10850, "pulled down in 1859" — a *demolished* building with its own grid reference. *(Verified as to what the record says; the 1917 Inventory behind it is unread.)*
4. **The Island House / Madam Bevan association remains UNSOURCED** — now with a named rival candidate rather than mere silence. *(Verified-strong)*
5. **Curtis was not read**, the archive.org route delivered page chrome disguised as a healthy result, and the false negative it invited was not recorded. *(Method, Verified)*
