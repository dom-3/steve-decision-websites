Island House · The Flagship

The Wider Field

The story doesn't stop at the front door. This is the same evidence read outward — the connections that place one house inside the story of a whole coast, what the finds actually let us argue, and the open questions the next phase should settle.

Three moves: read it outward (the rings), read it deeper (the diagnoses), and know exactly what's still open.

The story page is the spine — what happened, layer by layer. This page is the field around it: how Island House connects to Laugharne, the estuary and the nation; what the physical evidence lets us conclude rather than just describe; and the honest list of what remains to be proved. It's what turns a good story into original work.

1 · The concentric rings — reading it outward

One quay, six scales. Each ring widens the audience and the significance — and every one is anchored to something we can point at.

1
The house

The buried river

Island House sits on a tidal inlet with a river channel still running beneath it. The same fact explains why a port grew here (sheltered, tidal access right at the town) and why the building is unstable and floods today.

The connection: the geology is the whole plot — it made the port, and it's the reason the restoration exists.
2
The keyside

A merchant's waterfront

The quays, the stone steps and jetty, and the high-status hall houses with their garderobe — plus pottery traded from Bristol, France and Spain. This wasn't a jetty for locals; it was a working commercial frontage owned by people with money and reach.

The connection: the finds put a wealthy, outward-facing merchant class on this exact spot.
3
The town

Laugharne, still medieval

The quay sat just outside the walls of a chartered borough that has never stopped being one. Laugharne is one of only two functioning medieval corporations left in the UK — a Portreeve in a gold cockle-shell chain, a court leet, and a 25-mile Common Walk beaten every three years.

The connection: the living town is the context — the medieval structure the quay served is still visibly running.
4
The estuary

The Three Rivers & the cockle coast

The Taf is one of three rivers (with the Tywi and Gwendraeth) draining into Carmarthen Bay — silting, mud-laden estuaries. This is the cockle coast: Laugharne's "Cocklemen," the gatherers of Ferryside and Penclawdd, an industry old enough to be woven into the town's own regalia.

The connection: the quay's rise and fall is a chapter in the estuary's own story of silt and shellfish.
5
Wales

A national first

These are the first medieval quays excavated in Wales. They join a tiny canon of Welsh medieval maritime archaeology — the Newport Ship (built c.1449), the Magor Pill boat (c.1194) — evidence that had, until now, come mostly from big English ports like Bristol and London.

The connection: this is the reason a university, Cadw and the national press should care — it fills a genuine gap.
6
The Atlantic world

The wine-and-wool sea

Medieval West Wales sat on the edge of a busy Atlantic economy — Gascon wine up from Bordeaux, wool and cloth out, salt and iron across from France, Spain and Ireland. The Bristol/French/Spanish pottery here is a thread of that whole trading sea, landed at one small Welsh quay.

The connection: the outermost ring — a local dig that touches a Europe-wide trade network.

2 · The diagnoses — what the evidence lets us argue

Not description, but reading. Each find is a symptom; here's the diagnosis it points to — the interpretive backbone of the book and the research.

Symptom
Successive quays, pushed ever outward

A run of stone quays, each built further into the estuary and infilled behind, rebuilt until the 1600s.

Diagnosis: a 400-year losing battle against silt — the slow, managed death of a port, caught in stone. Kidwelly did the same, moving its quays out to Frankland and Muddlescwm.
Symptom
Cockle shells packed behind the walls

Each quay was backfilled with cockle shells from the estuary beds.

Diagnosis: the town's staple economy is literally built into its harbour — the same cockles that feed the "Cocklemen" and the Portreeve's chain are the rubble holding the quay up.
Symptom
1437 timbers in a house built c.1610–1630

Roof timbers felled in 1437, reused in a house whose truss style is early-1600s.

Diagnosis: a salvage economy and continuous elite occupation — there was a high-status medieval building here first, and its timber was too good to waste when the present house went up.
Symptom
Hall house · garderobe · imported pottery

A central-hearth hall house, a first-floor toilet, and ceramics from Bristol, France and Spain.

Diagnosis: not a fishing hamlet — a genuinely wealthy merchant household plugged into Atlantic trade, on a par with Tenby's merchant houses.
Symptom
A 3m clay-sealed wall, later breached

A massive garden wall, waterproofed with clay to hold the tide back — now broken.

Diagnosis: the site was always engineered against the water; today's flooding is that engineering failing, not a new problem. It frames the restoration as continuity, not novelty.
Symptom
"Island" at high tide

The inlet all but cut the spot off from land when the tide was in.

Diagnosis: the name isn't decorative — it records a real tidal geography, and doubles as the perfect title-metaphor for the whole work: a place apart, made by water.

3 · The outer connections — the hooks that travel

The threads that lift this beyond a local restoration and give the press, a publisher and a funder a reason to lean in.

The living medieval town. Laugharne's surviving corporation — Portreeve, court leet, the triennial Common Walk, burgage-strip land still allotted to senior burgesses — is a national rarity. The dig has a living stage set around it. Documented
The Admiral's name. The de Brian lords of Laugharne produced Sir Guy Brian KG — Admiral of the West, Garter knight, standard-bearer to Edward III at Calais. A national-history name hangs over this lordship (though the town's charter was granted by an earlier de Brian, not the Admiral). Documented
The national first. "The first medieval quays excavated in Wales" is a headline in its own right, and slots into the story of the Newport Ship and the Magor Pill boat — Wales's small, prized maritime canon. Documented
Dylan's town. Laugharne is already a place people travel to — the Boathouse, the writing shed, the model for Under Milk Wood's Llareggub, his grave at St Martin's. Island House adds a deeper, older layer under the literary one. Documented
Deep time. A mile away, Coygan gives Neanderthal handaxes from an Ice-Age cave and an Iron-Age/Roman hillfort — the long backdrop against which the medieval port is only the most recent chapter. Documented

4 · What's still open — the diagnostic questions

The honest to-test list. Settling these is exactly what makes the work original rather than a retelling — and the science is already on its way.

The charter date. 1247 is when the de Brians got Laugharne; the borough charter is undated (c.1290–1307 in the sources). The Corporation's own records could pin it. To settle · archives
What the port actually shipped. Wine, wool and cloth are the regional norm, but not yet proved for Laugharne itself — the customs accounts and port books would be the first synthesis of its real trade. To settle · archives
The Admiral and the harbour. Did Sir Guy Brian's naval role ever touch Laugharne as a working harbour or muster point? A great hook, but inference until a record turns up. To test · unproven
Is the inlet the River Corran? Locally the watercourse is named the Corran; the dig reports just say "a small tidal inlet." Worth tying down before it's printed as fact. To confirm
The scientific dating. The post-excavation dating from the dig is due just after Christmas — it will firm up the quay sequence and the earliest date on the site. Incoming · this winter

5 · The town, read house by house — the 2026 survey

Through 2026 we read Laugharne's standing buildings and families one by one, at source. Island House sits inside this — the same evidence standard, widened to the streets around it.

The great houses, building by building. The King Street and Clifton Street terrace is now read house by house from the Cadw listings — the Grade II* Great House (an early-18th-century Queen Anne house with an exceptional Baroque doorcase), the “Big House” (The Limes and Moir House, one late-Georgian mansion divided about 1900), the Vicarage (clergy house of St Martin's), and the continuous listed frontage between them. Documented
A named lord between Perrot and the war. The lordship of Laugharne passed after Sir John Perrot to Sir Sackville Crowe (1595–1671) — Treasurer of the Navy and England's ambassador to Turkey — who held it on a royal lease from 1619; his family kept it about a century. Documented
A Seven-Bishops judge in the churchyard. Sir John Powell (1633–1696), one of the judges who acquitted the Seven Bishops in 1688, is buried in St Martin's; his son Sir Thomas Powell of Broadway secured the town's 1697 market-and-fairs grant. Documented
Dylan's houses, read as fabric. The Pelican (his parents' home), Sea View (whose statutory listing carries his 1938 move from a Gosport Street cottage) and the Boathouse & writing shed are all read at source — the literary town anchored in listed buildings. Island House itself stays, honestly, not a Dylan site. Documented
The working town in the trade record. Pigot's directories of 1830 and 1844 put the town's tradespeople on the page — the David family (corn and butter dealers, a butcher, a carrier, a druggist) and the monthly Bristol packets (the Mary, Betsy and Penelope) — the ordinary commerce the quay once served. Documented
What we corrected. The Strand "David brewery" is not borne out by the trade directories; the "Fern Hill" lane above the town is not Dylan's Fern Hill (that farm is near Llangain); and Laugharne's medieval "walls" are an inference from the town plan, not surviving stone. Corrected
