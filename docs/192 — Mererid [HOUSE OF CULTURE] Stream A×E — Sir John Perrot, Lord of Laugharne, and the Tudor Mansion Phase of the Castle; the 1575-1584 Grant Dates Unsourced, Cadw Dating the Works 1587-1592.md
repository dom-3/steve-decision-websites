## Five-line finding

1. Sir John Perrot (b. Haroldston 1528/9; d. in the Tower 1592) held Laugharne Castle among his estates and carried out its Tudor mansion phase — the standing lodging ranges, raised curtain wall, Tudor gatehouse bay window and cobbled courtyard.
2. Cadw dates that "refenestration and reconstruction by Sir John Perrot" to **1587–1592** — the only object-level date for his works at Laugharne, and a window that ends at his death.
3. The popular "**acquired 1575 / granted outright 1584**" pair is **unsourced at object level**: it appears only on secondary visitor/tourist websites; neither the History of Parliament nor the DWB gives any Laugharne grant date. Held at Referenced-pending.
4. The current DWB (Turvey 2020) **refutes** the tradition that Perrot was an illegitimate son of Henry VIII — the paternity myth is licensed only by the older, "reputed" phrasing.
5. On Perrot's 1592 attainder, Laugharne Castle **reverted to the Earl of Northumberland** (Carew to the Crown) — a clean next step in the lordship sequence.

## The man (object level)

Perrot was an Elizabethan statesman: sheriff and thrice mayor of Haverfordwest, MP for Pembrokeshire and later Haverfordwest, first president of Munster (1571–3), and Lord Deputy of Ireland (1584–8), dying in the Tower of London in 1592 after attainder for treason. Both the History of Parliament and the DWB carry full, independent biographies; F192-1 rests on both and is tiered **Verified-strong**.

On paternity, the sources split by date. The History of Parliament (1981) and the archived 1959 DWB both record him as the "**reputed** illegit. s. of Henry VIII". The current DWB article (Turvey, 2020) is explicit: "Contrary to the oft quoted myth … he was not an illegitimate son of Henry VIII. Perrot's mother, Mary Berkeley, did not serve as one of the royal ladies-in-waiting and his father, Thomas, was never knighted." Recorded as **Refuted** (F192-2). The royal-bastard story is colourful and it will keep surfacing; it should not be repeated as fact, only — if at all — as a named-and-corrected legend.

## The grant date — the thread's own question

The thread title asserts 1575; the parallel lordship thread asserts 1584. **Neither source that carries a full biography gives a Laugharne grant date at all.** The History of Parliament documents Perrot's acquisition of *Carew* in stages — a petition in 1554, the stewardship in 1559, and a succession of crown leases in 1562–6 rounding off his control — but does not name Laugharne in its narrative. The DWB likewise lists his offices and estates without a Laugharne date.

The "acquired 1575, granted outright 1584" formulation is reproduced on visitor/tourist sites (castlesfortsbattles, ancient-history-sites and similar). It is **plausible** — it matches Perrot's known staged pattern of lease-then-grant at Carew — but it is **unsourced at object level**. It is held at **Referenced-pending** (F192-4). Do not present 1575 or 1584 as verified Laugharne dates; resolving them needs a primary grant or lease (a Carmarthenshire Archives / crown-lease task for the human archive day).

What the object *does* date is the building work: Cadw's list description gives "**refenestration and reconstruction by Sir John Perrot 1587 to 1592**" (F192-3, **Verified**). That window is the safe, sayable frame for his mark on the Castle — and it is quietly telling that it ends in the year of his death.

## The Tudor mansion in the standing fabric

The Cadw record lets the mansion phase be described from fabric rather than romance (F192-5, **Verified**). Perrot raised the medieval curtain wall and gatehouse and threw a range of lodgings around the inner courtyard. In the list description the earlier medieval work carries "**late C16 Perrot alterations**": the "later C15 4-storey hall" was reworked; the "forward projecting 4-storey gatehouse" gained, over its pointed entrance arch, a "**fine 3-storey Tudor bay window**"; and excavation revealed "remains of **Tudor cobbled courtyard**". The result, in Cadw's summary, is "amongst the most substantial castle remains in Wales."

A caution on the source itself (F192-8): the list description opens "Late C18 Castle replaced probably C12 earthwork destroyed in 1215 by Llewelyn the Great" — the "Late C18" is a **Cadw list-text error** at the head of the History, already flagged at Doc 157. The fabric is medieval with a late-C16 Tudor phase; do not quote "Late C18 Castle" as fact.

## Carew, not Laugharne, was his seat

A restraint worth logging (F192-7, **Verified**). The premier scholarly biography — the History of Parliament — centres **Carew**, which Perrot "largely rebuilt, and eventually made his principal seat," and does not name Laugharne in its narrative at all. Laugharne was a **secondary** Perrot seat. The safe guardrail wording: Perrot rebuilt Laugharne into a Tudor mansion, *but Carew was his principal home*. This keeps the town's genuine Perrot connection without inflating it.

## After Perrot — the lordship steps forward

On his attainder (17 April 1592) and death in the Tower, Perrot's son Sir Thomas was restored in blood and inherited Haroldston, "but **not Carew and Laugharne castles, which reverted to the Crown and the earl of Northumberland respectively**" (DWB, 2020). Recorded as F192-6, **Verified**. This is a solid next link in the C16 Laugharne lordship sequence: Perrot held Laugharne to 1592, after which it passed to the Earl of Northumberland — a lead for the still-open lordship-gap thread (recJENO6G1HyCpHQf).
