## 1. The headline: the governing document, verbatim

The register's **Governing document** page records, in the Commission's own words:

> CHARTER OF GUIDO DE BRIAN (DATE UNKNOWN C.1300) AS AMENDED BY SCHEME DATED 27 MAY 2003

Three separate findings sit inside that one line.

### 1.1 The regulator will not date the charter either

**This is the important one for the do-not-claim register.** Our standing guardrail is: *never print 1278, 1290 or 1307 as the charter date — say "late thirteenth century".* That guardrail has until now rested on our own reasoning about conflicting secondary authorities.

It now has an **independent, official, and entirely disinterested corroborator**. The body that regulates the charity, that holds its governing document, and that had to describe that document on a public register, wrote **"DATE UNKNOWN C.1300"**.

The Commission is not a historian and its dating is not evidence *of* the date. But it is excellent evidence that **no confident date is available on the face of the instrument itself**. A regulator with the document in hand, obliged to describe it, declined to date it. That is a much stronger negative than anything we had.

- **Tier: Verified** — that the Commission's register so describes it (the object says so).
- **Tier: Verified-strong** — as a negative: *the charter is not confidently datable from the document itself.*
- Note the Commission's "c.1300" is *later* than any of 1278 / 1290 / 1307-as-usually-argued sits comfortably with. It does not endorse any of the three circulating dates. **It must not be quoted as "the Charity Commission dates the charter to 1300."** It says the date is unknown.

### 1.2 The grantor is named "Guido de Brian"

The register gives the Latin/latinised form **Guido de Brian**, not "Guy de Brian" and not "de Brian" alone. This is the form on the Commission's record and is worth noting because it suggests the description was taken from a Latin text or a translation preserving the Latin name-form, rather than from a modern secondary account.

- **Tier: Verified** that the register uses this form. **Not** evidence about the charter's own wording — we have not read the charter.
- Feeds the open thread on the Alcwyn C. Evans translations (`reccP9WazGCAkLGip`) and the "which translation circulates in Laugharne today?" thread (`recQGoCEJNLTMqEZp`).

### 1.3 A NEW, DATABLE MODERN INSTRUMENT: the Scheme of 27 May 2003

This is entirely new to the desk. The medieval charter is **"AS AMENDED BY SCHEME DATED 27 MAY 2003"**.

A Charity Commission Scheme is a formal legal instrument made by the Commission that alters a charity's governing document. So the operative constitution of the Laugharne Corporation today is: a charter of unknown late-medieval date, **as amended twenty-three years ago by an order of a modern regulator.**

That is a genuinely good story and it is fully sourced: *the oldest working constitution in the town was last rewritten in 2003, by a government body, in a document with a precise date.*

- **Tier: Verified** — the register states it.
- **New thread opened:** obtain the text of the 2003 Scheme. Schemes are usually obtainable from the Commission, and are often published in the London Gazette. Doc 171 established a working Gazette route on this desk; that route should be tried against May–July 2003.

## 2. The charitable objects — and what they say about the courts

The register prints the objects in full. The sixth is the one that matters:

> (6) SUCH OTHER CHARITABLE PURPOSES WITHIN LAUGHARNE OR FOR THE GENERAL BENEFIT OF THE INHABITANTS AS **THE GRAND JURY OF THE COURT LEET AND THE COURT BARON OF LAUGHARNE CORPORATION** FROM TIME TO TIME THINKS FIT

This is a substantial finding for thread `recNvdwX1owZCLFtQ` ("what has the legal standing of Big Court and Little Court been since 1886?").

The Municipal Corporations Act 1883 s.2 abolished the *jurisdiction* of corporate courts and officers. It did not, on this evidence, abolish the *bodies* — because in 2003 the Charity Commission made a Scheme which **names the Grand Jury of the Court Leet and the Court Baron as the body that exercises the charity's discretion**.

So the honest formulation is:

> Whatever became of its judicial jurisdiction after 1883, the Court Leet and Court Baron of Laugharne survive as the **decision-making organ of a registered charity**, expressly named as such in a Charity Commission Scheme of 2003.

- **Tier: Verified** — the objects as printed on the register.
- **Explicitly NOT claimed:** that the courts retain any judicial or manorial jurisdiction. The Scheme gives them a charitable-trustee function. That is a different thing and the two must not be run together.
- **Terminology note:** the operative legal names are **Court Leet** and **Court Baron**. "Big Court" and "Little Court" are the town's names. Do not present the vernacular pair as the legal pair without saying so.

The other five objects: relief of poverty/old age/disablement/sickness among the inhabitants; advancement of education; provision of recreational facilities in the interests of social welfare; provision of public amenities; and — object (5) — **the preservation and conservation of buildings and land of architectural, historical, scientific or educational merit, and land of outstanding natural beauty, for the benefit of the inhabitants.**

Object (5) is worth flagging to the House of Culture project on its own account: heritage conservation for the benefit of the inhabitants is an express charitable object of the Corporation. That is a fact about the Corporation, not an invitation to imply any relationship with the Globe. **No such relationship is claimed here and none has been sought.**

## 3. Area of benefit, and the word "FORMER"

Register, Governing document page:

> Area of benefit: **AREA OF THE FORMER CORPORATION OF LAUGHARNE**

Register, Overview page:

> The charity's activities are confined to the area of Laugharne and **Llansadurnen** and benefits are restricted to the inhabitants of the area. Assistance is given to organisations and occasionally to individuals. Exceptionally, assistance may be given to individuals with a close connection to Laugharne living in the surrounding area.

Two things.

**(a) "FORMER" is the Commission's word, and it is doing legal work.** The area of benefit is defined by reference to a corporation the Commission describes as former. This is consistent with 1883/1886 having ended the *municipal* corporation while the *charity* carrying its lands continued. It is **not** a statement that "the Laugharne Corporation no longer exists" — the same register carries LAUGHARNE CORPORATION as a live working name (§4). Handle with care; do not quote "former Corporation" as a bare fact without this context.

**(b) Llansadurnen.** The area of benefit reaches into the neighbouring parish. Doc 164a read Llansadwrnen (Cadw 25849) at object level. The spelling on the register is *Llansadurnen*, without the *w*. Both forms are in use; note the variant rather than silently normalising it — Doc 168b's fourth search trap (OCR-variant blindness) applies to place-name variants in indexes as much as to newspapers.

**Where the charity operates:** Carmarthenshire.

**The 1963 registration date answers a standing question.** The Charities Act 1960 created the modern register and repealed MCA 1883 s.20 — the saving provision under which Laugharne (and Malmesbury) survived. Our open thread asked what that repeal actually did to the Corporation. The register now supplies a dated answer to half of it: **the Corporation's lands were entered on the new statutory register of charities on 26 September 1963**, three years after the 1960 Act. The route out of the 1883 saving was the route *into* charity registration.

- **Tier: Verified** — the registration date, from the register.
- **Referenced-pending** — the causal reading (that the 1960 Act's repeal *produced* the 1963 registration). Plausible, chronologically tight, and consistent; but the register does not say so and we have not read the 1960 Act's transitional provisions against this charity. Do not state the causation as fact.

**"LAUGHARNE CORPORATION" is a registered working name.** The town's usage is not merely colloquial; it is on the regulator's record.

**Organisation type "Other"** — i.e. not a trust, not a company, not a CIO. The Commission's own dropdown has no box for a chartered medieval borough corporation. That is a small, true and rather good detail.

### 5.2 The trustee cohort turns over at Michaelmas — a real finding

The **Trustees** page records **22 trustees**. Of the twenty listed on page 1:

- **nineteen** were appointed **6 October 2025**
- **one** was appointed **6 November 2025**

Nineteen simultaneous appointments on a single day is not a coincidence of recruitment. It is an **annual, wholesale re-constitution of the governing body**.

And the date is the tell. **Michaelmas Day 2025 (29 September) fell on a Monday. 6 October 2025 was the following Monday** — and the charity's financial year had ended six days earlier, on 30 September, at Michaelmas.

So: the accounts close at Michaelmas, and the governing body is renewed on the Monday after Michaelmas. That is the autumn court, functioning, dated, and visible on a government register in 2025.

**Tiering this honestly, because it would be very easy to overclaim:**

- **Verified:** 22 trustees; nineteen of the twenty listed appointed 6 October 2025, one 6 November 2025; financial year ends 30 September. All read off the object.
- **Verified:** 6 October 2025 was the Monday following Michaelmas Day 2025.
- **Referenced-pending — NOT Verified:** that the 6 October 2025 appointments *are* the Big Court. The register records appointment dates; it does not name the meeting. The inference is strong (annual mass appointment, Michaelmas alignment, and object (6) naming the Court Leet and Court Baron as the discretionary body) but it is an inference. To promote it, we need a minute book, a Corporation notice, or press naming the 2025 Big Court date.
- **Not claimed at all:** anything about how trustees are chosen, whether all 22 are burgesses, or whether the Portreeve and Common Attorneys appear among them. The register lists every one of them simply as "Trustee". It records **no offices**. Anyone reading the register alone cannot tell which trustee, if any, is the Portreeve.

**Names.** Twenty trustee names are on the public register and can be read there by anyone. They are living private individuals and they are not needed for any historical claim in this programme, so they are **not reproduced here or logged as Entities**. If a later run needs them — for instance to test the burgess question — they are one page away at the cited URL. Recording where they are is enough; compiling them into our own register is not warranted by anything we are trying to establish.

**Guardrail restated:** one Portreeve, not two. Nothing above should be read as bearing on the number of Portreeves; the pair is the two Common Attorneys.

## 7. Method note — the register reads at object level by URL path

The Charity Commission register is a client-rendered portal, but each section of a charity's entry is addressable by a clean URL path and returns readable text without any form interaction:

```
/en/charity-search/-/charity-details/<number>
/en/charity-search/-/charity-details/<number>/what-who-how-where
/en/charity-search/-/charity-details/<number>/governance
/en/charity-search/-/charity-details/<number>/trustees
/en/charity-search/-/charity-details/<number>/financial-history
/en/charity-search/-/charity-details/<number>/governing-document
```

Read each path separately. The overview page alone does **not** carry the governing document, the registration date, or the trustee appointment dates — the three most valuable fields in this pass were each on a different sub-page. A run that reads only the landing page will conclude the register holds nothing and record a false negative. That is the same shape of error as Doc 171's sixth trap (tool-level blindness): the object was readable all along, at a path nobody requested.

Generalises immediately to any other charity relevant to the town.
