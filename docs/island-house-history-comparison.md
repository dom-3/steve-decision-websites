# Island House — their history vs our library: what we added, debunked and proved

**Owner:** Island House desk · **Date:** 23 Jul 2026 · **Status:** written uncommitted for the single publisher
**Scope:** history & building detail only (images set aside for now, per Dominic). Compares the information already published on `islandhouse.wales` against our own research library (Mererid's register: 644 entities / 1,086 facts / 300+ graded sources, plus the History Trail and the "Where the History Stands" syntheses).

---

## 1. What their existing site actually says about the house

Their history rests on three things:

1. **The Mary Curtis passage (1880 / sketch 1856)**, quoted in full on the home page — walls "as thick as those of the castle"; half the house "destroyed… by the cannon-balls fired on the castle by Cromwell's soldiers," with a cannonball "preserved in the house up to 1823"; **Roman baths and leaden pipes, and an Etruscan urn, found in the garden**; a rounded tower foundation found underground in 1818; a garden cottage with steps to the sea; a door dated **1658**; two bridges lost around 1800; the house shown in a 1740 sketch of the castle.
2. **"The Owners and People of Island House | 1778–2020"** — a single timeline graphic, with an honest caveat in their own words: *"traced… from factual sources… using many resources… in these very challenging times where there is no access to local archive services,"* and *"there is no speculation on our behalf."* So: **1778 onwards only, and archive-starved.**
3. **The 2020 Dyfed Archaeology (DAT) excavation** — ceramic, clay-pipe and pottery finds, trench tables — plus the exhibition boards (condition, clearance, repair, concept, ground investigation, plans/elevations/sections, 3D visualisations, a Civil War board and Rob Scourfield's video) and the consultant roll (PJL, Mann Williams, SABA, Rob Scourfield, David Llewellyn, DAT, Tree Ring Services, Dr Tim Palmer, and more).

**In short:** a credible, professionally-advised restoration record, and a genuine archaeological dig — but a *human* story that starts at 1778, has no archive depth, and repeats Victorian folklore as established fact. That's the honest starting point their own team reached under lockdown conditions. Everything below is what we've added on top of it.

## 2. What we brought that is NEW (not on their site at all)

**The building is older and grander than "16th-century."**
- Their site calls it "a 16th century Grade II* listed house." Our evidence puts the **roof timbers at 1437 by dendrochronology and radiocarbon** — 15th-century fabric, a century older than they claim, and a hard scientific date rather than a style-guess.
- On the same plot, the **Heneb excavation found the earliest medieval quay yet excavated in Wales**, and imported pottery proving the house **traded with Bristol, the French coast and Spain**. The spade proved the fabric; we then found the people who traded from it.

**A continuous, named, ~800-year ownership line — where their record began at 1778.**
- The deep root is the **White family, holding Laugharne land from the 14th century** (possible original builders), then a succession of the town's leading families — **Prydderch, Thomas, Hughes** — down to **Jane Abra Hughes, sole heiress, who carried Island House to the Skyrmes**.
- **William Skyrme (1778–1823) was born and died in the house** and was **Portreeve of Laugharne (1801, 1802, 1817)**; the Skyrmes were marine-supply merchants whose 65-ton sloop, the *William Skyrme*, appears in **Lloyd's Register** — the living link from the medieval quay to a working Georgian business.
- The last heiress married into the **Wienholt** family, whose sons **emigrated to Queensland as major pastoralists** (confirmed in the Australian Dictionary of Biography).
- The **boundary wall still carries a dated plaque — "rebuilt by Maryabra Hughes Skyrme AD 1837"** — naming the last heiress in the fabric itself.
- In the 20th century, **Caleb Rees, Deputy Chief Inspector of Schools for Wales, lived here 1943–70**; his wife **Dr Laura Powell** was Medical Officer to the Welsh Board of Health; he was great-grandson of the celebrated London preacher **Caleb Morris**.
- The modern chapter is nailed to the record too: **Island House Restoration Ltd (company 12452655)**, and a **newspaper-verified resident, Percy Ll. Hughes-Garbett, c.1890–1893** — a name that was on nobody's timeline.

**National figures in the house's orbit** — Josiah Tucker (Laugharne-born Dean of Gloucester and economist), Sir John Perrot (lordship of Laugharne), Sir Sackville Crowe (Treasurer of the Navy), Sir John Powell (the Seven-Bishops judge, buried at St Martin's). And the estate's later shape: **broken up by auction at Brown's Hotel in 1900**, having once covered an estimated ~15% of Laugharne Township.

## 3. What we DEBUNKED or corrected — myths their site still shows as fact

This is the part that protects the brand: their live pages currently repeat several claims we've tested and found wrong or unprovable.

- **"Cromwell's cannon-balls."** Their site (via Curtis) attributes the damage to Cromwell's soldiers. The siege was **1644 under Major-General Rowland Laugharne — not Cromwell.** A factual correction, live on their homepage now.
- **"Roman baths, leaden pipes and an Etruscan urn."** A single Victorian antiquarian sentence — **tradition, not fact.** Charming as folklore; not something to assert.
- **"Virginia tobacco unloaded at the quay."** Unprovable single-source tradition. The *evidenced* trade is ordinary corn and oats to Bristol.
- **"16th-century."** Superseded — the fabric is older (1437 timbers).
- **The romantic Skyrme–de Brian marriage** — undocumented; the tradition fails.
- **Mansel of Margam as lords of Laugharne** — no Laugharne interest at all (the real Mansel link is a 1729 marriage into the Trimsaran branch).
- **"Sir Sackville Crowe sold Laugharne to Sir William Russell"** — false; read at source, that was a *naval* succession (Treasurer of the Navy) misread as a land sale.
- **The de Brian charter** — we distinguished **two Guy de Brians**, correcting the common conflation with the Knight of the Garter who died in 1390 (whose inquisition does not even list Laugharne).
- **"Madam Bevan (Bridget Bevan) lived at Island House"** — association found **unsourced** on our overnight pass; not to be repeated as fact.
- **The "Abra" name** — settled as a **forename** (Jane Abra), disarming a surname search-trap.
- Plus tidy-ups that touch the wider story: the **David family brewery** (not in the trade directories), the **"Fern Hill" name-trap** (Dylan's Fern Hill was near Llangain, not here), and a **round-tower/spiral-stair** tale that actually belongs to **Roche Castle at Broadway**, a different site.

## 4. What we RESEARCHED and confirmed to standard

Confirmed against primary or authoritative sources we have actually read: the **medieval quay**, the **1437 timbers**, the **Skyrme merchant family and the Portreeve dates**, **Josiah Tucker**, **Sir John Perrot**, the **Civil-War figures**, the **Rees / Dr Powell household**, the **Wienholt–Queensland emigration**, the **1837 wall plaque**, and the modern **Island House Restoration Ltd** and **Hughes-Garbett** facts. ## 5. What is still open — kept honest, not hidden

Genuinely unresolved and deliberately not claimed: the **charter's exact date** (standard authority says 1278; Curtis describes two charters — the rolls will decide); **the White family as original builders** (a real possibility, not yet proven); the **origin of the "Abra" name**; the **Globe–Skyrme assembly-rooms link**; the **Westmead / Lady Hawarden** thread; **Curtis's 1856 sketch** and her Rowland-Laugharne-birthplace note (both to be confirmed at source); and the **detailed later descent** from 1823 through the 1858 Wienholt marriage to the 1900 auction — drawn from the family's own compiled history and awaiting the wills, parish registers and the 1900 sale catalogue.

## 6. Bottom line — the value the research adds to the showcase

Their site is an honest restoration record with a shallow, archive-starved history that still carries Victorian myths as fact. Our library **pushes the house a century older on hard science, replaces a 1778 timeline with a documented ~800-year named human story reaching national figures, and corrects roughly a dozen claims their current pages state as true** — every item graded by evidence, with the legends kept but plainly labelled.

That combination — **proven fabric plus a proven, defensible human story** — is exactly what turns a restoration into a destination, and it is the difference the showcase should lead with. It also means one gate before publication: the corrections in §3 touch claims currently live on their own site, so the reframing (especially the "tradition, not fact" labelling of the Roman-remains and Cromwell items) should go past Karen before it's published.
