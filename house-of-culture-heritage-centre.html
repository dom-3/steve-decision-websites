The building itself as the start and end of the visit — a small, well-made heritage centre and design shop where everything on the shelf has a real story behind it, and the whole site is threaded by the tour. Concept to work from, and to talk to a maker through.

2 · The rule that makes it work

The difference between a heritage shop and a souvenir shop is one question, asked of every single object.

Everything we sell must pass the provenance test: it is genuinely made here or nearby, to a real standard, and it connects to a true story from the record. No manufactured heritage, no claim the history won't support, nothing mass-imported with a Laugharne sticker on it. If it can't answer "why does this exist and what is it connected to," it doesn't go on the shelf.

"Real things, made by real hands, that carry a true story — not souvenirs."

This is the same chain-of-custody discipline we apply to the history, applied to the shelf. It is also the commercial edge: a shop where every object has a story is a shop people remember, photograph and come back to — and it can charge properly, because it isn't selling tat.

3 · What's on the shelf — product concepts

Concept directions, each anchored to verified material. Amber cards wait on research or rights before they can be made. These are starting points to hand a maker, not a fixed range.

Verified · the Globe

The Hundred Lives print

A single beautiful piece charting the building's real lives — inn, coach-house, assembly rooms, butcher's, today — dated from the record. Letterpress or screen-print by a local printer.

Why it exists: the 1830 advert, the 1835 assembly, the Gleeds to 1991 — all documented.
Verified · the Corporation

The cockle-shell & the Portreeve

Enamel pin, tea towel or card celebrating the town's medieval Corporation — the Portreeve, Big Court, the constables chosen by lot. A story almost nobody outside Laugharne knows.

Why it exists: the 1883 Act saved the Corporation; it still swears in a Portreeve each autumn.
Verified · the estuary

The cockle-women range

A print or ceramic drawn from Curtis's eyewitness line — "a busy scene, one fit for an artist… they have walked miles." Ties to the real cockle trade and the carved shell on The Strand.

Why it exists: Curtis, c.1830; the cockleshell emblem still carved on The Strand.
Verified · the name

Talacharn → Laugharne

A typographic piece on the name worn down across six centuries — and the pronunciation "Larn". Quietly local; the kind of thing a Welsh speaker buys as much as a visitor.

Why it exists: attested forms 1364–1535; the reduction is Verified-strong.
Verified · the kitchen

The Globe larder

Edible take-home made by the kitchen and bakery — preserves, biscuits, a bottled something — and, in time, the Globe Cook Book. The bakery people already rave about, in a jar.

Why it exists: made on site; the deli/kitchen/Cook Book are already in build.
Verified · the makers

The gallery editions

Signed originals and affordable prints from the rotating local artists, with the maker's own story on the label. The wall and the shop feeding each other.

Why it exists: real local artists, real work — the good-neighbour engine of the whole thing.
Pending rights · Dylan

His words, carefully

A line of Dylan's own words — a printed card, a cloth — if and only if the public-domain position is confirmed, and framed honestly. Never his voice, never a claim we can't hold.

Held until: the UK/US rights position is verified. Do not make ahead of that.
Pending research · the walk

The Common Walk keepsake

Something tied to the beating of the bounds and its offices — but only with the Corporation's blessing, and only once the detail is verified. A once-every-three-years thing.

Held until: Corporation blessing + verified detail. Embargoed for now.

4 · Guided all the way through

The tour isn't only outside — it threads the building too. The same voice that walks you round the town introduces the centre, hands you off to the trail, and welcomes you back to the shop.

InWelcome & orientThe centre sets the scene and hands you the tour. No hard sell — this is the start of the story.
OutOnto the trailThe app leads you into the town; the building becomes base camp you'll return to.
BackReturn & lingerThe reading room deepens what you just walked — sources, voices, the fuller story.
KeepThrough the shopYou leave past the things that carry the day home — each one a story you now recognise.

The quiet mechanism: by the time a visitor reaches the shop, they've heard the stories behind what's on the shelf. The Hundred Lives print isn't décor — it's the building they've just stood inside. That's why provenance sells: the tour does the storytelling, so the object only has to remind.

5 · Principles

Provenance or it's not stockedMade here, to a standard, tied to a true story. No exceptions, no imported tat with a sticker.
The makers are paid properlyA real, fair platform for local artists and makers — the town benefiting, not being mined. This is also Steve's standing.
Never overclaim on an objectNo "13th-century cellars" on a tea towel, no Dylan quote before rights, no manufactured legend. The shelf obeys the record.
A range at every priceA £3 card and a £300 original under one roof — so everyone leaves with something, and nobody's priced out of the story.
Quality over quantityFewer, better things. A small shelf of remarkable objects beats a wall of forgettable ones.
Interpretation is freeThe heritage centre's factual content is open and generous. We charge for the made thing, never for the truth.

6 · For whoever's coming in

What we bring to the table, and what we'd want from a design/heritage/maker partner.

What we already have — a verified historical record (1,086 facts, sourced), a building with two centuries of documented history, the app and trail concept, an approved visual identity (Fraunces, Aged Brass, Archival Paper), and a live kitchen, bakery and bar inside the building.
What we're looking for — a curator's eye for the shop and heritage centre, relationships with local makers, a feel for high-standard small-batch retail, and someone who understands that the provenance rule is the whole point.
What we won't do — mass-import, overclaim, or turn the story into cheap souvenirs. The bar is high on purpose, and it's the thing that makes it work.
