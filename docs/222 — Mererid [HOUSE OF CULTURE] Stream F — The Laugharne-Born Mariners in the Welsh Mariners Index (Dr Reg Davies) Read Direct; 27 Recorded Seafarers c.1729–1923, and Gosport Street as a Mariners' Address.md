## 0. The target and why it was taken

Most of Laugharne's high-value listed buildings and their occupants are now worked (the Globe itself at Doc 198, Brown's, the Ship & Castle, Great House, the New Three Mariners, Castle House, the Town Hall, Gwalia & Manchester House, St Martin's, the Castle). The **port-and-trade stream (F)** was under-served: the medieval quay was read at Doc 208, but the *people* who made Laugharne a working port had not been touched. This pass takes the registered Open thread on Laugharne shipping and spends it on a clean, structured, open-access object — the **Welsh Mariners Index** — reading every mariner the index records as **born at Laugharne**.

This matters to the House of Culture because it is the human evidence that Laugharne was a seafaring town, not merely a market and Corporation town. The Globe stood as a coaching inn and hotel in a port that bred certificated deep-sea masters across the whole nineteenth century — that is the economy the inn served.

## 1. The source

The Welsh Mariners Index is a compiled online index by the late Dr Reg Davies: **23,758 Welsh merchant masters, mates and engineers, 1800–1945** (with a separate index of ~3,000 Royal Navy mariners, 1795–1815). It is a **secondary compilation (T2)**, but it is disciplined: each entry carries the **primary reference it was built from** — the Board of Trade masters'/mates' certificate registers at The National Archives, Kew (BT122–124 here), the census, *Lloyd's List* and the *Shipping Gazette*, and Trinity House Petitions. So the index is a finding aid to primary record, and its individual Kew references are the route to object-level confirmation of any single life.

**Route (repeatable), F222-8:** `welshmariners.org.uk/search.php` — a POST search form. Set `search_birthplace` = `Laugharn`, `birthplace_type` = **Starts With** (`%`), `max_records` = 250. One query returns the whole `Laugharn`/`Laugharne` set. The site is server-rendered PHP; a plain fetch returns an empty shell, so it must be read through a JS-capable browser.

## 2. The finding — 27 Laugharne-born mariners, c.1729–1923

The single birthplace query returned **27 records** (F222-1). The field is town-stamped — every one reads *Laugharne, CMN* (Carmarthenshire) — so this is object-level identification on a structured datum, **not** a newspaper page co-occurrence. The WNO page-level and near-collision traps that govern our press work simply do not arise on this surface (F222-8).

The cohort is dominated by **certificated nineteenth-century master mariners** — Foreign-going and Home-trade competency certificates issued under the certification system that followed the 1850 Mercantile Marine Act — each with its Kew BT122–124 register reference (F222-2). Representative certificated masters: David Griffiths (b.1800, cert. 71497), Henry Hughes (b.1802), William Jardine (b.1802), Joseph Morgan (b.1826), Cornelius Edwards (b.1844), David Wilkins (b.1842).

The recorded birth-years run from **c.1729 to 1923**, so the index documents Laugharne seafaring across roughly two centuries.

### The individual lives worth naming

- **William Owen "of Langhorne" (b. c.1729)** — the earliest in the set, recorded from a **Trinity House Petition of 1795** (aged 66). Pre-certificate; pushes documented Laugharne seafaring back into the early eighteenth century (F222-3). Note the period spelling *Langhorne*.
- **Capt. David John Carr Jones (b.1871, cert. 025586)** — the most distinguished. Per a *Lloyd's List* retirement notice (15 Dec 1931) transcribed in the index: first to sea aged 13 in the *Rokeby Hall*; joined the Allan Line during the Boer War; served as lieutenant / staff captain on armed merchant cruisers including the ***Calgarian* (torpedoed 1 March 1918)** and the *Empress* liners; and commanded the ***Metagama*, *Duchess of Bedford* and *Duchess of York*** (F222-4).
- **James Williams (b.1838, cert. 93572)** — master of the barque **_Daring_**, supposed drowned when she was **lost with all hands off Pwlldu on 9 March 1871** (*Western Mail*, 14 & 20 Mar 1871). Alongside **Henry Hughes (b.1802)**, master of the *Eclipse of London*, who sailed for Mauritius in November 1855 and was never heard of again (*Shipping Gazette*, 4 Jul 1856): the human cost of the trade (F222-5).
- **James Roberts, Master Mariner (b. c.1825)** — recorded in the **1881 census at Gosport Street, Laugharne** (with wife Amelia). This is object-level evidence that the **Gosport quarter housed the town's mariners** (F222-7), and it hands a named datum to the open "Gosport quarter" thread.

### The town as a Bristol-Channel port

The 1881-census entries in the index show Laugharne-born masters commanding coasting vessels out of the **neighbouring Bristol Channel ports** rather than Laugharne itself — George Brown master of the *Matilda* at Milford Haven, David Griffiths master of the *Gem of the Sea* at Llanelli, John Brown mate of the *Marie Louise* at Cardiff (F222-6). The pattern fits a small tidal creek-port that bred seamen who then sailed from the deeper harbours around it.

## 4. What this does and does not establish

It establishes, at object level, that **Laugharne was a nursery of certificated master mariners across the nineteenth century**, with a documented seafaring tradition reaching back to at least the 1790s, and that **Gosport Street was a mariners' address**. It does **not** yet answer the sibling thread — *what Laugharne imported and exported, and in what quantities* (rec6GGKHzdx73ZyJN) — which needs port books and coastal-trade returns, nor does it establish vessels **built at** Laugharne (this index records men, not shipbuilding). Those remain open.
