## 0. What this pass was, and the one thing to take from it

The registered thread told me to read **CIPM vol. 5, no. 32**. I read it. It is not the Laugharne inquisition and has nothing to do with Wales. The thread's premise was wrong, and had I taken the number on trust and reported "the 1307 IPM" from it, a fabricated citation would have entered the register under a real shelfmark — the most dangerous class of error this desk can make, because it is *checkable-looking*.

Instead I ran the volume's own index as a finding aid and found the real entry: **CIPM vol. 5, no. 64**, the inquisition post mortem of **Guy de Brian**, taken at Carmarthen on **26 August 1 Edward II**. That entry is the single richest primary-record object on the Laugharne lordship this desk has yet read at object level, and it is free, open and readable.

**The one thing:** the Laugharne IPM is dated **1307**, and the 1307-vs-1308 question does not survive contact with the document — see §3, which closes it arithmetically rather than by preference.

## 2. Method — the index as allocator, and the negative that had to be proved

The thread pointed at a number. Numbers in a 600-page calendar are cheap to mis-copy and impossible to verify by eye. The method that worked, and that should be repeated for every Calendar volume this desk touches:

1. **Read the entry the thread names, and prove it wrong or right by its own text.** No. 32 is **"HENRY DE EDMUNDESTROP"**, writ missing. Its neighbours are no. 31 "HUGH DE GEROUNDE" and no. 33 "PATRICK DE HASEWELL *alias* DE HASELWELLE". No Welsh county, no Brian, no Laugharne under any spelling. The thread's premise is **REFUTED**.
2. **Do not then guess.** A near-miss ("perhaps it was 34?") is precisely how a false citation enters a register.
3. **Go to the volume's own Index of Persons and Places** — the editors' finding aid, made from the manuscript, and therefore the correct allocator.
4. **Search the index under every plausible head, not one.** Laugharne is indexed under **L**; the Welsh and Latin forms are indexed under **T** with cross-references. Had I searched only "Talacharn" I would have found three *See* references and no entry numbers; had I searched only "Laugharne" I would have missed the attested medieval spellings entirely. Both heads were needed.
5. **Run a positive control on the surface before recording any negative.** Before concluding "no burgages are calendared for Laugharne" (§6) I checked that this calendar *does* print burgages when it has them: entry **56** (Joan de Valencia, Countess of Pembroke) prints *"Teneberey. 20a. foreign land, **241 burgages**, 1a. meadow, 2 mills, **six stranger burgesses**, twenty farmers (sensar'), &c."* The surface is capable of the positive. This satisfies the sixth-trap rule (`rect2Qf4UOtSubOPd`) — no negative from any surface without a positive control run against that same surface in the same session.

## 3. THE DATE — 1307, and why the regnal year cannot be argued with here

The calendar dates the Laugharne inquisition by regnal year only. Converting regnal years to calendar years is where this desk has previously been vulnerable, because a regnal year straddles two calendar years and a careless conversion can be off by one. Here it cannot be.

Edward I died **7 July 1307**. **1 Edward II therefore runs 8 July 1307 – 7 July 1308.** The portion of that regnal year falling in calendar 1308 is **1 January – 7 July 1308** only.

The dates the calendar gives for no. 64:

| Instrument | Date as calendared | Calendar year | Ambiguous? |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| *Writ* to the justice of West Wales | **26 July, 1 Edw. II** | **1307** | No — July falls after 8 July 1307 and before 1 Jan 1308 |
| *Inq.* (Carmarthen) | **26 Aug. 1 Edw. II** | **1307** | No |
| *Inq.* (Pembroke) | **26 Aug. 1 Edw. II** (defective) | **1307** | No |
| *Mandate* to the chancellor | **15 Sept. 1 Edw. II** | **1307** | No |

All four instruments fall in the July–September window, which in 1 Edward II can only be 1307. **The 1307-vs-1308 question is closed: 1307.** This is not a preference between authorities; it is arithmetic on the document's own dating clause.

**Tier: Verified-strong.**

A further internal corroboration, independent of the regnal arithmetic: the writ is *"dated at Carlisle under the privy seal **which the king is using before taking up the government**"*, and the chancellor's mandate is *"under the privy seal, which the king is using **because he has no other yet ordained for the government of the realm**"*. Both clauses describe the interregnal weeks immediately after a king's death and before the great seal is settled — i.e. high summer 1307, with Edward II newly acceded and still in the north. The document dates itself twice over.

**A guardrail restated, not loosened.** This settles the date of *an inquisition*. It says nothing whatever about the date of the charter. The standing rule holds without qualification: **never print 1278, 1290 or 1307 as the charter date — say "late thirteenth century".** If anything this pass hardens that rule, because it shows how a real 1307 document sits in the record waiting to be mistaken for the charter by anyone working from secondary summaries. The "1307 tradition" for the charter (thread `recYz8Cfpm73s8eCp`, the uncited "Williams, n.d.") now has an obvious candidate *source of confusion*, which is not the same as a source.

## 4. THE NAME — medieval forms attested in a royal record

The single entry uses **two different spellings of Laugharne in the same paragraph**, and the volume's index prints five forms with cross-references. Verbatim:

From the Index of Persons and Places, L (p. 510ff):

> **Laugharne**, Tal-llacharn, *or* Talycharn, Talacherne, Tallauhern, Talleuharn, co. Carmarthen, 386.
> ………, barony, 64 (*p*. 32).
> …….., castle, 64 (*p*. 32).

From the Index of Persons and Places, T (p. 570ff):

> **Talacherne** [co. Carm.]. *See* Laugharne.
> **Talleuharn**, Tallauharn, co. Carmarthen. *See* Laugharne.

In the body of no. 64 the forms **Tallauhern** and **Talleuharn** both appear, of the same place, within a few lines; in no. 386 the form is **Talacherne**.

**What this earns.** A datable royal record of 1307 and 1312 attesting the name in the forms *Tallauhern / Talleuharn / Talacherne*, all recognisably the **Tal-** element. **Tier: Verified.**

**What this does NOT earn, and the guardrail holds.** The standing rule stands: *Tal-* ("end, brow") is safe; **leave the second element obscure, and never gloss the name's meaning.** Three medieval clerical spellings of a Welsh place-name in an English Chancery record are evidence of what clerks wrote, not of what the name means. The Owen & Morgan dictionary thread (`recGt9rr45yY9XgJv`) remains the only route to meaning and remains open.

Secondary value: this is a ready-made **OCR-variant set for the fourth trap**. Any future full-text search of medieval or early-modern printed calendars for Laugharne should run at minimum: Laugharne, Lacharn, Talacharn, Talacherne, Tallauhern, Talleuharn, Tallauharn, Tal-llacharn, Talycharn, Thalkan (the 1283 Welsh Rolls form already registered at thread `receDMoRO9Ie1lHu3`).

## 5. THE BARONY — what the 1307 extent actually says

Verbatim, the opening of the Carmarthen inquisition in no. 64:

> **Tallauhern.** The barony (extent given), including a castle, pastures called Talkil and Haynygtors, and mills, lands, &c., in Talleuharn, Marcros, Castelhely, Heyrwer, Karvan, Belhestre, Maisgorda, Egluskeffeg, and Lasedurny, and containing **12 1/4 knight's fees**, held of the king in chief by homage and service of **2 knight's fees**, viz.—with two barded horses or eight footmen according to the custom of those parts, for three days at his own charges at the summons of the king's bailiffs of Carmarthen (*Kermerd'*) whenever necessary, and doing suit at the county (court) of Carmarthen, and all his foreign tenants ought to follow the lord of Tallauhern or his bailiff for three days at their own charges on the like summons.

Points that carry, each tiered:

**5.1 The castle is in the record in 1307.** "including a castle". **Verified.** It tells us nothing about fabric or phase — it is a tenurial mention, not a description. It does not bear on the standing-fabric or siege threads.

**5.2 The barony's size and service.** 12¼ knight's fees held for the service of 2. **Verified.** Anchor this to the office and the obligation, never to the numeral in isolation: the lord of Laugharne owed the king two knights' fees of service, discharged as *two barded horses or eight footmen*, for *three days at his own cost*, on the summons of the king's bailiffs of Carmarthen — a light, local, short-service obligation of a March lordship, not a national levy.

**5.3 A military obligation running downward through the barony's own tenants.** "all his foreign tenants ought to follow the lord of Tallauhern or his bailiff for three days at their own charges on the like summons." **Verified.** This is the earliest object-level statement this desk holds of an obligation owed *to the lord of Laugharne by the people of his lordship*. It is the deep tenurial substratum beneath everything Stream B works on — but it is a **lordship** obligation, not a **borough** one, and must never be presented as an ancestor of the Corporation's customs without a document that joins them.

**5.4 Named places in the lordship, 1307.** Talkil and Haynygtors (pastures); Marcros, Castelhely, Heyrwer, Karvan, Belhestre, Maisgorda, Egluskeffeg, Lasedurny. Plus, among the fees: Egluskymin (Eglwys Cymmyn), Macrels, Rosgoch, Pulgogam, Castelloyd, Mertherkenang, Neuton, Pendyn, **Trefmoillet**, Hemmygis, Prestiltan, Casteltef, Pantclas, Cralond, Dolvornych, Kilwathkilnish, Egluskeffyg. **Referenced-pending** as a gazetteer: the forms are 1307 clerical spellings, several are defective in the manuscript (the calendar prints "(?)"), and identifying them to modern places is a job for a Welsh place-name authority, not for me. Do not publish modern equivalents.

**5.5 Named tenants of the barony, 1307.** John de la Roche; Thomas de la Roche; Thomas de Rossal and Nesta his wife; Robert le Malros; **Amabel Laundrey**; John Poucock; Philip ap David; David de Foresta; Stephen Baret; the heirs of Sir William de Caunville; William Felebesche; John de Sancta Brigida; **Guy de Brian, son of the deceased**; Meredith and Guy ap Traharn (?); and *"Egluskeffig. 2½ carucates land freely held by fourteen Welshmen"*. **Verified** as calendared. Note the last clause: fourteen free Welsh tenants holding by Welsh law inside an Anglo-Norman barony in 1307, and elsewhere *"Pendyn. 1 carucate land held by Meredith and Guy ap Traharn (?) **by Welsh law**"*. That is a real and publishable point about the lordship's mixed legal fabric — and it is one of the very few places where the Welsh-language layer of this project touches a document of this date.

**5.6 Trefmoillet is in the barony in 1307.** The index gives *"**Tremaillett**, Trefmoillet [in Eglwys Cymmyn], co. Carmarthen, 64 (p. 32)"*. This is a direct, datable hit on the "Tremoilet" element of thread `reczEYq17CkjlBRMr` (the 1893 London Gazette railway scheme touching the parish of Laugharne and "Tremoilet"). It establishes the place as a real, anciently-named holding of the Laugharne lordship in Eglwys Cymmyn, five and a half centuries before the railway notice. It does **not** establish anything about the railway scheme; it fixes the place-name only.

## 6. THE BOROUGH — the question the calendar will not answer, and why that is not a negative

There is **no mention of a town, a borough, burgages, burgesses, a market, a fair, a portreeve or a charter** anywhere in the calendared text of no. 64.

I ran the positive control before recording that (§2, step 5): this calendar prints burgages and burgesses when the extent contains them — entry 56 gives *"241 burgages … six stranger burgesses"* for Tenbury. So the surface is capable.

**But the negative does not stand, and I am not recording it as one.** The reason is on the face of the entry: the calendar writes *"The barony **(extent given)**, including a castle, pastures … and mills, lands, **&c.**"*. "Extent given" and "&c." are the editors' explicit signals that the manuscript's detailed valuation has been **summarised away**. Where entry 56 prints burgages it is transcribing a passage the editors chose to give in full. Absence from an abstract that announces its own abbreviation is not absence from the record.

**Tier: recorded as an OPEN QUESTION, not as a finding of any kind.** The only thing that can answer it is the manuscript: **TNA C 134, Edw. II File 6 (2)**. That is an archive-gated item and belongs to the human archive day, not to a desk run.

This matters more than it looks. If Laugharne's borough status can be shown *present* or *absent* in a dated royal extent of 1307, that speaks directly to `recCCJhON8dVEaHt3` (was Laugharne genuinely a walled borough?) and to `recYz8Cfpm73s8eCp` (the uncited "Williams, n.d." 1278–82 borough-status date). **I have not shown either.** I have identified the document that could, and named its shelfmark.

## 7. THREE GUYS DE BRIAN, SEPARATED BY ONE DOCUMENT

Thread `recuv6swMsplPOOZx` records that "Coflein and the Community Council number the Guys differently, which makes 'which Guy granted the charter' currently unanswerable." This entry does not number them, but it **separates three of them at a fixed date**, which is better than a numbering scheme because it rests on a document.

From the closing lines of the Carmarthen inquisition, verbatim:

> Of all which **Joan late the wife of Guy de Brian, grandfather of Guy who now is**, has a third part in dower. **Guy his son, aged 23, is his next heir.**

and, inside the extent:

> Kilwathkilnish (?). 3 carucates land held by **Guy de Brian, son of the deceased**, by homage and service of a rose yearly, doing suit of court as above.

So, as at 26 August 1307, the document requires three distinct men of the name:

| | Who | What the document says | Inferred dates |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **A** | Guy de Brian, **grandfather** of the current heir | Dead. His widow **Joan** is alive in 1307 and holds a third of the whole barony in dower. | d. before 1307 |
| **B** | Guy de Brian, **the deceased** — the subject of this IPM | Died shortly before 26 July 1307 (the writ). Son of A; father of C. | d. 1307 |
| **C** | Guy de Brian, **"Guy who now is"**, the heir | **Aged 23** in Aug 1307. Already holds Kilwathkilnish of his father for a rose a year. | b. c. **1283/84** |

**Tier: Verified** for the three-generation relationship and for C's age; **Verified-strong** for the fact that Joan, widow of A, was alive and in seisin of dower in August 1307 — an unusually long-surviving dowager, which is itself the mechanism by which a *grandfather's* widow still encumbers a third of the estate two generations on.

Consequences, stated carefully:

- **This does not tell us which Guy granted the charter.** It tells us that in 1307 there were at least three men of the name within living memory, two of them dead, and that any secondary source saying "Guy de Brian" without a generation number is under-determined. That is the honest state of `recuv6swMsplPOOZx`, and it is now evidenced rather than asserted.
- A dowager's third running across the whole barony is a live constraint on any C14 tenurial reading of Laugharne. Anyone reading a later Laugharne document must ask whether they are looking at the lord's two-thirds or Joan's third.
- The Pembroke half of the same IPM shows the family also held **Walwain Castle** (Walwyn's Castle, Pembrokeshire) — *"The barony (extent given), including a water-mill at Malros, and a curtilage at Benton, and containing 9 knights' fees and 3 carucates land, held of the earl of Gloucester (Glovernia) in chief"*, and in the king's hand in 1307 by reason of the minority of Gilbert de Clare. The de Brians of Laugharne were a two-barony family, one held of the king, one of Clare. **Verified.**

## 8. A SECOND LAUGHARNE ENTRY — no. 386, and a LORD'S COURT at Talacherne in 1312

The index gave a second entry number under Laugharne: **386**. It is the IPM of **MABEL (*Amabilla, Anabilla*) LONDREY *alias* LOUNDRE**, writ **8 Sept. 6 Edw. II** — 6 Edward II runs 8 July 1312 – 7 July 1313, so a September writ is unambiguously **1312**.

Verbatim, from the Carmarthen inquisition:

> **Landoveroi.** The manor held of the king in chief by homage and service of 1 knight's fee, viz.—an armed man with a barded horse or four footmen according to the custom of those parts, for three days at his own charges at the summons of the king's bailiffs of Kermerdyn whenever necessary, and doing suit at the county (court) of Kermerdyn; and all his tenants ought to come with their lord or his bailiff for three days at their own charges when required, to accompany the lord of Kermerdyn to war (*in expedicionem domini de Kermerdyn*).
> **Castel Toch.** 1/2 knight's fee held of **Guy de Bryona** by homage and knight's service and **suit at his court of Talacherne**.
> **Clogeuran.** 1 carucate land held of Sir Morgan ap Mereduth by service of 12*d*. or a sparrowhawk yearly.
> **Philip Londre her son, aged 55, is her next heir.**

Three things this earns:

**8.1 The same woman, five years apart, identified across two documents.** No. 64 (1307) records *"Casteltef. 1/2 knight's fee held by **Amabel Laundrey**"*. No. 386 (1312) records the IPM of *Mabel/Amabilla/Anabilla Londrey alias Loundre*, holding *"**Castel Toch**. 1/2 knight's fee held of Guy de Bryona"*. Same holding, same fraction of a fee, same lord, same forename (Amabel/Mabel/Amabilla are one name), five years apart. **Tier: Verified** — the identification rests on four independent matching elements, not on the name alone. Note the spelling drift *Casteltef → Castel Toch*: a live demonstration of the same trap that governs the newspaper work, in a source five hundred years older.

She is a **named female tenant of the barony of Laugharne with her own royal inquisition**. That is a rare and genuinely usable person for Stream D, and one who reaches the record in her own right rather than as a wife.

**8.2 A LORD'S COURT AT LAUGHARNE, ATTESTED 1312.** *"suit at his court of Talacherne."* A court of the lord, sitting at Laugharne, to which a tenant owed suit, in a royal record of 1312. **Tier: Verified-strong** — this is the calendar quoting the tenurial clause directly, not summarising.

**And now the discipline.** This is a **seignorial court of the lord of the barony**. It is **not** the Corporation's Big Court, it is **not** the Little Court, and there is at present **no document on this desk joining them**. The threads that ask what Big Court and Little Court are and when they sit (`recQj0rps9Thy1EBo`, `recZA5n1vVxyXobMl`) are untouched by this and remain open. Writing "the town's court has met since 1312" would be exactly the kind of bridge-by-assumption that produced the tithe-plot and Globe/Beynon retractions. **DO-NOT-CLAIM, added below.** The honest, publishable form is: *a court of the lord of Laugharne is recorded in 1312; whether any line runs from it to the Corporation's courts is unestablished.*

**8.3 Llanddowror in the same hand.** *Landoveroi* is indexed *"**Llandowrer**, Landoveroi [co. Carmarthen], manor, 386"* — held of the king in chief for one knight's fee, with the same three-day, self-funded, bailiffs-of-Carmarthen service formula as Laugharne itself, and the striking clause *in expedicionem domini de Kermerdyn*. **Verified.** The formula's near-identity to Laugharne's (§5) suggests a standard West Wales tenurial form rather than anything peculiar to Laugharne — a useful corrective to any reading of the Laugharne service clause as distinctive.

## 11. NINTH SEARCH TRAP — THE INHERITED ENTRY NUMBER

Registered here as a named trap, because it nearly fired and it will fire again.

**The trap.** A thread, note or secondary source hands you a *precise-looking* citation — volume and entry number, shelfmark, page. Precision reads as provenance. It is not provenance. An entry number carries no internal check: no. 32 looks exactly as authoritative as no. 64, and a document read at no. 32 and reported as "the 1307 Laugharne IPM" would have been *unfalsifiable by anyone who did not go and look*, because the citation format is correct and the volume is real.

**Why it is worse than the OCR traps.** The newspaper traps (1–8) produce *wrong text that looks right*. This produces *right text under a wrong label* — a real document, faithfully quoted, attached to the wrong subject. Nothing in the quotation betrays the error. It survives review.

**The rule.** *An entry, item or plot number inherited from anywhere other than the source's own finding aid is a HYPOTHESIS, not a citation. Before quoting from a numbered entry, confirm the number against the source's own index, table of references, or catalogue — and if the number proves wrong, do not guess an adjacent one; re-derive it from the index.*

This is the same failure mode as **the duplicated tithe plot number** (fifth trap) generalised: there, two apportionments made "plot 333" ambiguous; here, a single sequence made "no. 32" simply wrong. Both are cases of a **number treated as an identifier when it is only a coordinate**.

**Immediate application.** Every registered thread on this desk that names a specific entry, item or folio number sourced from a summary rather than from a catalogue is now suspect and should be re-derived before it is spent. Candidates visible in the current queue: `recd3aoDakj7HmD5k` (now corrected), `recAidK6kIYTrchiI` (NLW 10118E Vol. 1, "the 1307 IPM"), `receDMoRO9Ie1lHu3` (Cal. Chancery Rolls Various 1277–1326, p. 279), `recux18BShxf29Uau` (Cal. Charter Rolls vol. 2 index), `reczEYq17CkjlBRMr` (LG 26461 p. 6863). A method sweep is proposed as a new thread.

**Note on `recAidK6kIYTrchiI` specifically.** That thread describes NLW 10118E Vol. 1 as recording "the 1307 Inquisition post Mortem … 1000 acres of pasture in 'le Marcis'". The 1307 IPM is now read at calendar level and it does mention pastures — *"pastures called Talkil and Haynygtors"* — but no acreage is calendared and no "le Marcis" appears. That is **not a refutation**: NLW 10118E is a different object (a transcript or related manuscript), and the calendar suppresses the extent. But the two should now be compared rather than assumed identical, and the thread's note updated to say so.
