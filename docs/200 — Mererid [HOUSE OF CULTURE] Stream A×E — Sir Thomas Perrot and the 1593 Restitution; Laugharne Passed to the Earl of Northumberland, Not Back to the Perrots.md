### Five-line finding

1. **Sir Thomas Perrot (b. Haroldston Aug/Sept 1553; d. 1594)** was the only son of Sir John Perrot by his first wife, Anne, daughter of Sir Thomas Cheyne of Shurland, Kent. Soldier and courtier: knighted at Waterford in 1579; MP for Pembrokeshire (1581, 1593) and Cardiganshire (1586); deputy lieutenant of Pembrokeshire 1586–90 and mayor of Haverfordwest 1586; fought at Zutphen (1586). In 1583 he was gaoled in the Fleet for secretly marrying the Queen's lady-in-waiting Dorothy Devereux, sister of the 2nd Earl of Essex.

2. **The March 1593 Act restored him in blood but not in name**, after his father's 1592 attainder and death in the Tower. Both sources agree it was rushed through both Houses in four days and that he owed it to his brother-in-law Robert Devereux, 2nd Earl of Essex (the DWB adds that the Queen was reportedly minded to pardon Sir John before he died).

3. **The restitution was partial, not total.** He inherited Haroldston and much of the rest of the paternal estate — **but not Carew Castle, which reverted to the Crown** (both HoP and DWB), **and not Laugharne Castle** either.

4. **Laugharne did not return to the Perrots.** The DWB is explicit: he inherited the estates *"but not Carew and Laugharne castles, which reverted to the Crown and the earl of Northumberland respectively."* This confirms and extends Doc 192's Earl-of-Northumberland finding and fixes the succession: the 1592 attainder ended the Perrot lordship of Laugharne, and the 1593 restitution to Sir Thomas did **not** undo it for Laugharne. **Tier: Verified** (one court-grade explicit witness — the HoP article names only Carew in its descent passage and is silent on Laugharne, so this is not Verified-strong).

5. **A circulating claim is refuted.** The notion — found in secondary/tourist framing, and reproduced in an AI search summary during this run — that "Sir Thomas Perrot inherited Laugharne Castle through the March 1593 Act" is **Refuted**: the DWB expressly excludes Laugharne from what the Act returned to him. Reporting this as Refuted is the correct outcome, not a failure.

### The lordship succession, as now fixed

- **to 1592** — Sir John Perrot, lord of Laugharne; builder of the castle's Tudor mansion phase (Doc 192).
- **1592** — Sir John attainted for treason; dies in the Tower (November 1592). Laugharne and Carew forfeit.
- **March 1593** — Sir Thomas Perrot restored in blood by Act of Parliament; inherits Haroldston and most of the estate, **but not Carew (→ Crown) or Laugharne (→ Earl of Northumberland)**.
- **1594** — Sir Thomas dies; will dated 12 February 1594, proved three days later; estates divided (in default of male heirs, after the death of his son Robert) between his widow Dorothy and daughter Penelope; buried at St Clement Danes, the Strand.
- **Laugharne → Earl of Northumberland** — the DWB names the destination but not the mechanism. **Held Referenced-pending:** the likely route is Henry Percy, 9th Earl of Northumberland's marriage to Dorothy Devereux, Sir Thomas's widow, after 1594 (the DWB cross-references "Dorothy Percy, Countess of Northumberland"). Confirmation route: ODNB / Complete Peerage for the 9th Earl and for Dorothy Percy.

### Traps caught and guardrails honoured

- **Near-collision (naming):** the DWB records that Sir John Perrot's daughter Lettice married *"(1) Rowland Laugharne of St. Bride's."* This is the **Laugharne family of St Bride's, Pembrokeshire** — a gentry surname, distinct from Major-General Rowland Laugharne of the 1644 Civil War, and with **no bearing on the naming of the town** of Laugharne / Talacharn. Recorded to fence the trap (Doc 140 guardrail: the town is not named after Major-General Rowland Laugharne).
- **Source-divergence handled honestly:** HoP and DWB agree on the partial restitution but the HoP member article names only Carew reverting to the Crown and says nothing of Laugharne. The Laugharne→Northumberland point therefore rests on the DWB alone and is tiered **Verified**, not Verified-strong.
- **No overclaim on the mechanism:** the Dorothy-Devereux-to-Northumberland route is a strong lead, not read at object level; held Referenced-pending.
- Nothing here touches the Dylan material, the charter date, the siege, or any Doc-140-banned formulation. Additive-only: no existing record was edited, downgraded or deleted; nothing was published to the public History Trail.

### Aside (not attached to Laugharne)

The HoP article notes that Sir Thomas's right, as steward of **Cilgerran**, to appoint the town's officials was disputed in the Exchequer by John Garnons, who claimed the town was incorporate and could appoint its own officials. A neat contemporary parallel to the theme of incorporate towns governing themselves — but it concerns Cilgerran, not Laugharne, and is recorded here only as context, not as a Laugharne fact.
