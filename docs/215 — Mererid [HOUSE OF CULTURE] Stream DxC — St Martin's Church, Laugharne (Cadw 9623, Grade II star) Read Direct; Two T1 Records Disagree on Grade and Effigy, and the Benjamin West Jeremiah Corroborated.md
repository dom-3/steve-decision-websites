## 1. The building read from the record

St Martin's stands in an **elevated, roughly rectilinear churchyard** on the northern outskirts of the town centre, bounded by former strip fields. It is built of **limestone rubble**, part-coursed, with freestone dressings.

**Plan (both records agree):** cruciform — a **crossing tower with transepts**, a **three-bay chancel**, a **four-bay aisleless nave**, a north-east vestry / organ chamber of 1855, and gabled porches (NW, SW, SE). Coflein adds a gabled storehouse south of the chancel known as the **'Bone House'**, thought possibly to be a post-medieval charnel house.

**Date and fabric.** The church is of **thirteenth-century origin**, rebuilt in the **fourteenth century by Sir Guy de Brian**, lord of Laugharne; the standing nave, chancel, transepts and tower are thought to belong to a **single-phase mid-fifteenth-century rebuild**. During the 1873–74 restoration the footings of a shorter, narrower earlier nave were found. This ties the church fabric directly to the **de Brian lordship** already documented on this desk (cf. Doc 184, the de Brian IPMs). Coflein notes the degree of internal buttressing is **"unique in south Wales,"** and that the north transept's east window is the only surviving medieval window.

**The tower** is of two stages, **lowered in 1873**, with crenellated and corbelled parapets, two louvred bell-stage openings per face, and an advanced, taller stair turret known as **the 'King's Seat'**. Coflein records the tower is thought once to have had a fourth storey.

**Victorian restorations.** Two major campaigns, in **1855/6 and 1873**, both to the designs of **R. Kyrke Penson, Carmarthenshire County Surveyor**. Coflein sets these within a longer nineteenth-century sequence (re-roofing and re-seating 1853; vestry and organ chamber 1855), and records extensive **storm-damage repairs in 1954**.

## 2. The interior and its furnishings

- A **blocked squint retains a tenth/eleventh-century Celtic cross** with knotwork and cable moulding, in the south transept — a datable early-medieval object that predates the standing church. (Cadw: C10/11; Coflein: "ninth or tenth century.")
- **Rood screen and loft of 1909**; **pulpit of 1925**; a **fourteenth-century reredos** and restored piscina/sedilia.
- The **organ, by W. G. Vowles of Bristol, installed in 1821**, was **the gift of Admiral John Laugharne** — timber casing, foliage and cusping over gilded pipes.
- A **Victorian octagonal font** with a suspended canopy; largely Victorian glass, **except a fourteenth-century head** in the eastern window of the nave north wall, said to depict the head of Edward III.
- A **carved figure of St Martin by the Langs of Oberammergau (Bavaria), brought to the church in 1866**, set opposite the blocked north porch.
- A small **painted panel of George III's arms signed "Joseph Lewis painter."**

## 3. "The chief interest of this church lies in its specially fine collection of monuments"

That is Cadw's own phrasing. The monuments are mainly eighteenth- and nineteenth-century, and include:

- The **pedimented monument to Arthur Bevan on the south wall, dated 6 March 1649, signed "Bn Palmer Fec."**
- A **classical monument on the nave north wall to Admiral John Laugharne (d. 1819) and his wife.**
- Several eighteenth-century wall tablets in the north transept ("Palmer's aisle"), one dated 1690; and, in the south transept and chancel, variously pedimented monuments with classical and Baroque detail — one surmounted by a coat of arms, another ornamented with skulls and putti.
- **A memorial to Dylan Thomas** inside the nave (see §5 — distinct from the churchyard grave).

**The Benjamin West *Jeremiah*.** The nave holds a large unsigned canvas of **Jeremiah by Benjamin West (1780s), one of a set of eight painted for George III's Royal Chapel at Windsor Castle — and the only one of the set to remain in Britain.** This **independently corroborates Doc 202 / 163a**, which recorded the West painting now in St Martin's as formerly held in a museum at Great House. Cadw's own listing text confirms its presence in the church, on its own authority.

## 4. Two discrepancies caught between the two Tier-1 records

This is the substantive methodological yield of the pass. Reading Cadw and Coflein side by side surfaced two disagreements that a single-source read would have missed:

1. **Listing grade.** Cadw, the statutory listing authority, grades the **church Grade II\***. Coflein's record text describes the church as **"a Grade II listed building."** For the statutory grade, **follow Cadw (II\*)**; the churchyard is separately listed **Grade II** (Cadw 9624), which is the likeliest source of Coflein's slip. (F215-1, Verified-strong.)

2. **The medieval effigy.** The north transept ("Palmer's aisle") holds a **fourteenth-century recumbent effigy in a double-cusped recess**. **Cadw calls it "a monk"; Coflein calls it "a lady, dating to around 1400."** Two Tier-1 authorities, opposite identifications of the same object. **Held at Referenced-pending** — neither gender is asserted as fact; resolving it needs an on-site or antiquarian read. Reporting the disagreement *is* the finding. (F215-7.)

A third, minor divergence: the **bells**. Cadw dates them **1729 (Abraham Rudhall of Gloucester)**; Coflein reports **three bells cast in 1726 using clay from the Roaches, in Laugharne.** Noted on F215-9.

## 5. The churchyard, Dylan Thomas, and the guardrails

**Dylan Thomas (1914–1953) is buried in St Martin's churchyard.** The grave is marked by a **simple white wooden cross**; **Caitlin Thomas is buried with him.** A **memorial to Dylan also stands inside the nave** (the item Cadw lists among the monuments in §3). (F215-10, Verified.)

**Standing guardrails observed in the write-up:**

## 6. Traditions and one banned formulation — held at tier

Coflein carries several reported claims that are **not** promoted:

- The church's **original dedication may have been to St Michael** (so named in 1494 and 1849).
- A **Roman cinerary urn** was **reportedly** found beneath the south transept during the 1873–74 restoration; a medieval tile and part of a canopied tomb were found in the churchyard in 1927; cist burials and a wheel-topped stone are reported from grave-digging.
- Coflein prints the **borough charter as "1278."** **This is a Doc-140-banned formulation, caught here in an authoritative source.** The safe wording remains **"late thirteenth century"** — the numeral is not to be reproduced regardless of where it appears. (Same discipline as Doc 190, where three banned formulations were caught in the RCAHMW town record.)

All of the above are logged at **Tradition** (F215-11), not as fact.
