### One-paragraph finding

Castle House stands immediately north of Laugharne Castle and is entered through the same iron gates — an early-Georgian classical town house, Grade II* (Cadw 9657, designated 30 November 1966), said to be *c.*1730 with Regency alterations and enlargement *c.*1810. Its C20 significance is literary, and — importantly — that significance is carried **by Cadw's own statutory text, not by town tradition**. The listing names "the author Richard Hughes who occupied the house 1934/5 to 1946" and credits the architect Clough Williams-Ellis with the internal modifications made for him. In the castle grounds, on the SE edge high above the foreshore, a **separately listed** early-C19 gazebo (Cadw 9656, Grade II, designated 25 September 1986) is built onto a medieval round tower on the line of the late-thirteenth-century curtain wall; Cadw's own text for the gazebo describes its sea-view rooms as those "in which Dylan Thomas and Richard Hughes worked." This is a **genuine, statutory** Dylan association — distinct in kind from the retracted Globe/Beynon material — and it is anchored to the object, not to hearsay.

### The objects read (three, all at object level)

**1. Cadw 9657 — Castle House (Grade II\*).** Read direct via the Cadw FullReport endpoint and cross-checked against the British Listed Buildings mirror (300009657), which reproduces the text verbatim.

> **History.** "Early Georgian classical house said to be ca 1730; Regency alterations and enlargement ca 1810; internal modifications by Clough Williams-Ellis for the author Richard Hughes who occupied the house 1934/5 to 1946. Shallow U-plan."
> **Location.** "Prominently sited to N of the castle and entered through the same iron gates."
> **Exterior.** 3-storey, 5-bay rendered front with an advanced 3-bay frontispiece; low-pitched slate roof behind a freestone blocking course; broken pedimented doorcase to a large 8-panel door; flat Dutch-gabled side elevations; cross range to the right altered by Williams-Ellis with broader-glazed sashes reaching almost to ground level; a C19 colourwashed rubble former servants' hall extends east.
> **Interior.** Fine Regency interiors: entrance hall with barley-twist banding; "massive panelled double doors, which when folded form a ballroom to front of the house"; dining room with animal-head cornice; drawing room with Regency fireplace; a fine cantilevered staircase.

Designation 30/11/1966, no amendment. Broad Class Domestic. OS SN302108.

**2. Cadw 9656 — Gazebo in grounds of Laugharne Castle (Grade II).** Read direct via the FullReport endpoint.

> **History.** "Early C19 (shown in view of 1830) rubble gazebo built onto a medieval round tower on the line of the late C13 curtain wall."
> **Exterior.** "Pyramidal slate roof, brick chimney stack. 3-sided bay window with replaced 12-pane sash windows lighting rooms with wide sea views **in which Dylan Thomas and Richard Hughes worked**."
> **Location.** "Situated on the SE edge of the castle grounds high above the foreshore."
> **Reason for designation.** "In a Scheduled Ancient Monument site. Included for group value."

Designation 25/09/1986. Broad Class Gardens, Parks and Urban Spaces.

**3. Coflein / RCAHMW — Laugharne Castle Gazebo, NPRN 23036** (John Wiles, 29.01.08). Read direct.

> "This is an early nineteenth century gazebo built on the base of a tower at Laugharne Castle (NPRN 95634). It is chiefly noted for its literary associations — with the novelist Richard Hughes and the poet Dylan Thomas. It was built by 1830 and was a feature of the gardens laid out in the Castle grounds (NPRN 79039), attached to Castle House (NPRN 17184)."
> **Source line:** "CADW Listed Buildings Database (9656)."

The Coflein record **self-declares its dependence on Cadw** — its cited source *is* Cadw 9656 — so it is corroboration in wording only, not an independent object. Its value here is the extra RCAHMW cross-references it supplies: Castle House = **NPRN 17184**, castle gardens = **NPRN 79039**, castle = **NPRN 95634**.

### Method notes

- **The Dylan link is statutory, and that matters.** House style rightly quarantines the retracted Globe/Beynon material because that link was invented and then hedged as "tradition." The Castle-House-gazebo association is the opposite case: it is printed in Cadw's own statutory listing. The safe, sayable form for the trail is *"the sea-view gazebo where Dylan Thomas and Richard Hughes worked"* — Cadw's own words. What must **not** yet be printed is the popular, title-specific claim that Dylan wrote *Portrait of the Artist as a Young Dog* there; neither Cadw record says which work, only that he "worked." Held Referenced-pending until a biography is read at object level (F210-11).
- **Coflein dependence, again.** As with the Town Hall (Doc 160), the churchyard (Doc 162) and the Ship & Castle (Doc 196), the Laugharne Coflein record self-declares "Source: CADW…". Continue to treat these as Cadw restatements, not independent corroboration.
- **The `servants''''` artefact.** The quadruple-apostrophe string in the Cadw 9657 exterior text appears **identically** in the British Listed Buildings mirror — so, as with Doc 198's garbled 1834-survey phrase, it is Cadw's own encoding, not a scraper fault. BLB can be trusted as a faithful mirror of the Cadw list text.
- **Safe dating language observed.** The gazebo sits on "a medieval round tower on the line of the late C13 curtain wall" — recorded as *late thirteenth century*, per house style; no numeral charter/date printed.
