## 0. Scope note — why this is a House of Culture (town) target, not an Island House one

The quay was excavated *at* Island House, and the dig is sponsored by Island House Restoration Ltd (Philip Bond and Stephen Kirkwood) as part of the house's restoration. But the object itself is **town port-and-trade fabric**: a public quay on a tidal inlet *outside* the medieval town walls, serving "the castle and town" (Heneb's words). Its significance is Laugharne's medieval sea trade — the primary connection is to the town and the port, and Island House is the findspot and the sponsor's building, not the subject. Taken on that basis, with the overlap flagged. The Island-House-specific strand — the house's own **1437 roof-timber dendrochronology** — is left to the parallel Island House branch, and is expressly *not* claimed here.

## 2. What the record establishes

**The discovery (Verified).** Heneb excavated a medieval quay at Island House, Laugharne, and describes it as *the first medieval quay excavated in Wales*. That the dig happened and that Heneb makes this claim is verified from the excavating body's own account with a named supervisor.

**The quay and the inlet (Referenced-pending).** In Ken Murphy's words, the quay "would have been on a small tidal inlet just outside the medieval town walls and in the shadow of Laugharne Castle … the tidal inlet has long since silted up and is now a car park." This places the town's medieval waterfront *outside* the walls — it dovetails with the walled-borough picture recorded in Doc 190 (RCAHMW NPRN 33066).

**The sequence (Referenced-pending).** "The first quay was built in the twelfth or thirteenth century. The inlet slowly silted up, so quay walls were built further and further out into the inlet. Eventually in the seventeenth century boats could no longer get to the quay." A ~C12/C13-to-C17 working life for the waterfront, ended by siltation.

**The quayside buildings (Referenced-pending).** Stone buildings stood on the quayside; one was "a large, high status hall house" with a central fireplace venting through a high roof — interpreted as "probably the home of a wealthy merchant profiting from the coastal trade." An earlier 2020 season had already revealed stratified medieval archaeology, including a water course used as a midden by castle and town "from the 12th century."

**The trade reach (Referenced-pending).** "The pottery found during the excavation shows that extensive trade routes ran from Bristol, the French coast and as far south as Spain." Murphy: the find "makes us re-evaluate the importance of Laugharne within the trading network of the British Isles." Dr Carol Bell frames it the same way — Laugharne's "place in extensive trading networks to other parts of Britain and beyond."

## 3. Two corrections the object forces

**(a) The "1437 quay" is a conflation.** Our own thread was framed around "the 1437 medieval quay." The Heneb object is explicit that 1437 is a different thing: *"Island House itself has roof timbers dating back to 1437 AD (based on tree ring dating (dendrochronology) and radiocarbon dating)."* The 1437 date belongs to **the house's roof**, not to the quay. The quay's first phase is dated to the **twelfth or thirteenth century**. Going forward: never write "the 1437 quay." (Fact tiered Verified — the object states both dates plainly.)

**(b) "First in Wales" means first *excavated*, not *earliest built*.** Heneb's wording is "the first medieval quay **excavated** in Wales" / "the first of its kind **to be found** in Wales." This is a claim about discovery, not antiquity: it does **not** say Laugharne's is the oldest quay in Wales. Murphy is clear that medieval quays are already known and excavated at larger ports such as London and Bristol; what is new is finding one *here*. Safe wording: *"the first medieval quay to be excavated in Wales" (Heneb, 2025)* — attributed, and never inflated to "earliest/oldest quay in Wales."

## 4. Why it matters to the House of Culture

This is an **independent, physical** strand of Laugharne's medieval importance, and it lands squarely inside the town's documented late-thirteenth-century horizon. A quay first built in the C12–C13 sits beside the 1283 royal-record attestation of the town (Doc 188) and the de Brian lordship spine (Docs 182, 184): the charter-era town now has not only a lord, a court and a name in the rolls, but a **working port** reaching Bristol, France and Spain. The waterfront-outside-the-walls detail also strengthens the walled-borough reading (Doc 190). For the trail, the payload is a concrete, castle-shadowed image — small boats at a quay on an inlet that is now a car park — carried entirely on the town's own maritime past, with no need to touch the Dylan layer or over-claim.
