The study — where the record actually stands

Honest state of play. 382 facts Verified, 10 Verified-strong, 151 Referenced-pending, 12 Tradition, 8 Refuted.

The verified spine — and the documents behind it

The primary sources are the whole point. These are quoted exactly as they read.

The Globe, from 1830

The Globe's own documented story begins earlier than anything we previously had, and it stands entirely on its own without borrowing a poet. An auction advertisement in the Carmarthen Journal of 23 and 30 April 1830 names it, describes it as already long-established, and gives us the first human being on record at the door.

"The well established Inn, situate in High-street… called the Globe Inn, with two stables, a good Coach-house, yard and a small Garden, now in the occupation of the representatives of the late Mr. Thomas Edwards…"
Carmarthen Journal, 23 & 30 April 1830

Thomas Edwards, innkeeper, dead by April 1830 — the earliest named occupier of the Globe on record. His representatives held the inn at £30 a year, due to quit at Michaelmas 1830. The coach-house is now primary evidence, where the "horse and coach station" line had previously rested only on a modern planning summary.

Five years later the ballroom is working as the town's assembly rooms:

"Second Laugharne Assembly… at the Globe Inn, on Friday the 18th December next… Tickets to be had at the Globe."
The Welshman, 27 November 1835 — stewards Timothy Powell of Penycoed and Capt. Wm. Tringham RN

Then the eighty years that most of the town still remembers: a butcher's shop under the Gleed family, roughly 1911 to 1991, closing on Bobby Gleed's retirement. In March 2021 the rear barn was still fitted out as a working abattoir and the shop untouched since its last occupation.

The town that kept its medieval government

This is the strongest evidenced material we hold — read at object level from the King's Printer text of the Municipal Corporations Act 1883. Section 20 is headed, in the Act's own arrangement of sections, "Saving as to Laugharne and Malmesbury".

"…the corporation in the said place shall continue undissolved in like manner as if it were constituted by the said scheme."
Municipal Corporations Act 1883, s.20(1)

The Act's First Schedule names the body as "The portreeve and burgesses of the town and corporation of Laugharne." Malmesbury took a new charter in 1886 and became an ordinary borough; Laugharne did not, so the saving fell on Laugharne alone. Malmesbury kept the land. Laugharne kept the constitution.

The living machinery survives: the Portreeve sworn in at Big Court on the first Monday after Michaelmas, serving one year then becoming an Alderman; the Sunday breakfast and the oath reaffirmed at Divine Service before the Portreeve's Chaplain; four Petty Constables chosen by lot; two Common Attorneys collecting the rents; Court Leet and Court Baron still meeting fortnightly; and the timber dais in the Town Hall from which the Portreeve still conducts the court.

The charter, and the name

"…all the good laws and customs that the burgesses of Carmarthen have up to now used and enjoyed in the time of King John, the grandfather of the Lord Edward, the son of Henry… preserving the weights and measures that were in the time of Guido de Brione, the elder."
The Laugharne borough charter, granted to the burgesses of 'Thalacharn' — undated and unsigned

The charter carries no date on the instrument itself. Best current scholarship places it between November 1279 and March 1282.

And the name is one word worn down, not two: "Laugharne" is a reduction of the Welsh Talacharn — the loss of the first syllable. Tal- forms are attested from 1 March 1364; the reduced form appears by 1 April 1509; both ran side by side by the 1420s, and no pre-1600 document uses the modern spelling. The local pronunciation "Larn" preserves the loss. The 1535 statute says it outright: "Langeharne otherwise called Tallangherne."

The port, and the water

The first medieval quay excavated in Wales sits on Island House's plot; its roof timbers are dated to 1437, and imported pottery shows trade with Bristol, the French coast and Spain. The ordinary working trade is documented too — corn and oats "regularly shipped for Bristol" — with named coasters: the Mary (Thomas Lewis, master, monthly, 1830), the Betsy and the Penelope (fortnightly, 1844).

"…a busy scene, one fit for an artist… they have walked miles."
Mary Curtis on the Laugharne cockle-women, c.1830

The Strand still carries a ground-floor recess with a cockleshell emblem carved into it.

The history trail — the strongest stories

Ranked by strength of evidence, not by romance. Amber-edged cards are running on thin material and need more digging before they carry a stop.

01 · Verified-strong

The town that refused a charter

The 1883 Act named two towns. Malmesbury took a new charter and became an ordinary borough. Laugharne did not — so the saving fell to Laugharne alone, and its medieval corporation continued undissolved.

Primary statute read at object level; First Schedule naming the portreeve and burgesses.
02 · Verified

Big Court, and the year that turns on a Monday in October

The Portreeve sworn at Big Court on the first Monday after Michaelmas; a one-year term; the Sunday breakfast and the oath reaffirmed in St Martin's; four Petty Constables chosen by lot; the courts still sitting fortnightly.

Corporation records; Cadw on the surviving timber dais.
03 · Verified

Eighty years a butcher's

The Globe was a butcher's shop under the Gleeds for around eighty years, roughly 1911 until the shop closed in 1991. In 2021 the rear barn was still a fitted working abattoir.

Planning record; People's Collection Wales 554337 — Bobby and June Gleed serving in the shop.
04 · Verified

Thomas Edwards — the earliest name on the door

An 1830 auction advert gives us a "well established Inn", a coach-house, two stables and a garden — and an innkeeper already dead, his representatives holding at £30 a year until Michaelmas.

Carmarthen Journal, 23 & 30 April 1830; The Welshman, 27 Nov 1835.
05 · Verified

The siege won in the town, not at the castle

Surrender came on Sunday 3 November 1644 after a week. The decisive action was fought in the streets — and afterwards, in Cadw's own words, the castle was "subsequently pillaged for the building of town houses." The castle's stone is in the town's walls.

Cadw designation text. Tactical detail rests on a single source — keep the telling broad.
06 · Verified-strong

Talacharn worn down to Larne

One name eroding across six centuries — Tal- attested from 1364, the reduced form by 1509, both in parallel by the 1420s, and the local pronunciation still carrying the missing syllable.

Attested forms 1364–1535; Laws in Wales Act 1535.
07 · Verified

The medieval quay under Island House

The first medieval quay excavated in Wales, roof timbers dated 1437, and pottery from Bristol, France and Spain — a small Welsh town wired into Atlantic trade.

Excavation and dendrochronology; not a Dylan site — do not present it as one.
08 · Verified

The heiress and the end of a line

Mary Abra Hughes Skyrme, 1813–1885, sole heiress and last of her line, married at 45 and died without issue — with her name still carved on the wall she rebuilt in 1837.

Cadw listing text quoting the inscription.
09 · Verified

Two writers on one hill

Richard Hughes wrote at Castle House from 1934 to 1947; Dylan arrived in Laugharne that same year, reached Sea View in 1938 and the Boathouse in 1949. Hughes is the under-told literary root; Dylan is the draw.

Cadw Register of Parks & Gardens; the writing shed a 1920s garage built for the town's first car, a Wolseley.
10 · Thin — needs digging

The cockle women of Gosport

Women walking miles out onto the Taf mudflats, living in the Gosport quarter, and a cockleshell carved into a doorway on The Strand. A wonderful stop — but all three cockle threads are still open.

Build on Curtis's eyewitness sentence and the carved shell only. No quantities, no licensing, no Corporation role — none of it is evidenced yet.

What we must never say

The refuted list. These are not cautions — they are prohibitions, and several of them are claims we previously believed.

Never tie Under Milk Wood's Butcher Beynon to the Globe or to any Laugharne butcher. He draws on Carl Eynon, a butcher and publican of St Clears. Richard Hughes — Dylan's Laugharne friend — said Dylan "didn't use actual Laugharne characters", and that the Llareggub characters "ought to live in Laugharne, perhaps, but in fact not one of them does."
Never "13th-century medieval barrel-vaulted cellars". The vaulting and the four-foot walls are describable; the dating is not. Cadw dates the building late Georgian / mid-C19, and neither 2022 professional survey makes any medieval claim.
Never "the Laugharne Corporation was abolished in 1886" — true of the borough, false of the Corporation.
Never "the last surviving medieval corporation in the UK". Always "one of only two medieval corporations to survive in Britain, alongside the City of London."
Never "two portreeves". There is one Portreeve; the pair is the two Common Attorneys.
Never say the town was named after Major-General Rowland Laugharne — he was of St Brides, had no connection to the town, and the English form of the name predates his birth by a century. His family took its name from the place.
Never print 1278, 1290 or 1307 as the charter date. Say "a charter granted in the late thirteenth century by Sir Guy de Brian" or "probably around 1280".
Never gloss the meaning of the name. The "bright rock" reading cannot be sourced and appears to be an AI-generated summary circulating as fact. Tal ("end, brow") is safe; leave the second element obscure.
Never say the 1644 siege lasted five days — say six days, or a week at the end of October 1644.
Never present Island House as a Dylan Thomas location.
Never promise step-free or accessible routes. The centre slopes, the kerbs and thresholds are historic, and the castle car park floods at high spring tides. A physical access audit comes first.
Never promise Dylan's recorded voice — sound recordings carry their own term plus performers' rights. His written work entered the UK public domain in January 2024 but remains in copyright in the USA.

Say only as tradition

Cromwell watching the siege from Gosport House, and Van Dyck staying there.
John Wesley preaching from the Cross at The Grist.
Churchill and the bottle of whisky at the Great House.
Sea View built taller than the castle, its top storey blown off in a storm.
The bar "said to have been" made from the pulpit of the destroyed Congregational Chapel.
Roman baths at Island House, and Virginia tobacco unloaded at the quay — both single-source Curtis.
That Under Milk Wood was written at the Boathouse — contested: fewer than around 300 lines were written in Laugharne, and New Quay has a serious rival claim.

Unreconciled — do not smooth over

The 1830 advert places the Globe Inn in "High-street", while the building today stands on the corner of Market and Duncan Streets. Not yet reconciled. The 1834 survey map and the 1841 tithe apportionment should settle street and plot together.

The origin of the Globe's name and the history of its sign are undocumented in any accessible source.

Say "the Globe Inn" for the 1830s, not "the Globe Hotel". And note that Cadw's own castle record opens "Late C18 Castle…", which is an error in Cadw's record.

The people

Named humans are the storytelling anchors — a name does what a date can't.

Thomas Edwards — innkeeper of the Globe, dead by April 1830. The earliest name on the door.
Bobby & June Gleed — ran the butcher's shop at the Globe; it closed on his retirement in 1991.
Mary Curtis (1815–1895) — governess, painter and antiquarian; wrote Antiquities of Laugharne, 1871.
Mary Abra Hughes Skyrme (1813–1885) — sole heiress of Island House, last of the line.
Sir John Perrot (1530–1592) — turned the castle into a Renaissance mansion; died in the Tower.
Sir John Powell (1633–1696) — judge; one of those who acquitted the Seven Bishops in 1688. Buried here.
Josiah Tucker (1712–1799) — economist and Dean of Gloucester, born at Laugharne.
Richard Hughes (1900–1976) — A High Wind in Jamaica; at Castle House 1934–47.
Sir Sackville Crowe — Treasurer of the Navy, ambassador to Turkey, died in a debtors' prison.
Clementina, Viscountess Hawarden (1822–1865) — pioneering photographer; her family held Westmead.

The plan of attack

Where the digging goes next, in priority order.

1 · Find the 1834 survey map. Every dating of the Globe currently rests on it, and it has not been located. It would also close the High-street anomaly and fix the plot boundary — which the trail app needs regardless.Highest
2 · Chase the abattoir photographic record. Condition 5 of consent PL/03375 required a professional photographic survey lodged with Dyfed Archaeological Trust. It should exist. It is the visual spine of the butcher's story.High
3 · Read the unread heavyweight sources. Adcock's 2024 UWTSD thesis (only the abstract read), Avent 1987 on the siege (definitive, not online), Avent's 1995 Cadw guide, Archaeology Wales report 1581, and Owen & Morgan's Dictionary of the Place-Names of Wales.High
4 · Work the five bare areas. Cockles, the Common Walk, folklore, chapels and schools, and twentieth-century living memory have no completed threads between them. Living memory is the urgent one — it is the only category that disappears.Urgent
5 · Second-source the castle. Cadw is currently load-bearing, and Coflein and British Listed Buildings are Cadw-derived — not independent corroboration. The genuine second objects are the historic OS maps.Ongoing
