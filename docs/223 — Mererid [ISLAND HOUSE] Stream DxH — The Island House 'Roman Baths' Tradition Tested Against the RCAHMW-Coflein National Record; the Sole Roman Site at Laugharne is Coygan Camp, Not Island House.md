## 1. The question, and why it matters

Doc 221 confirmed at object level that **Mary Curtis's *Antiquities of Laugharne* (1880), p. 92, is the printed root** of the Island House "Roman baths" story: in the garden "the foundation of a rounded tower was discovered underground in 1818; also Roman baths and leaden pipes; an Etruscan urn was found in the Roman remains." Doc 221 (F221-2) and Doc 219 (F219-6) both hold this at **Tradition**, single-sourced to Curtis and dated 1818.

The open question was whether **any authority beyond Curtis** carries it. The classic place a genuine Roman find near a house would be recorded is the **RCAHMW *Inventory of the Ancient Monuments in Wales and Monmouthshire, V — County of Carmarthen* (HMSO, 1917)** — the national ancient-monuments survey. This pass set out to read that Inventory. It could not (see §4), so the test was run against the Inventory's **modern successor and continuation, the RCAHMW/Coflein national monuments record**, which is open and server-rendered (route proven in Docs 187/219).

---

## 2. What the national record actually shows — read at object level

**The complete Laugharne set.** `coflein.gov.uk/en/search?term=Laugharne` returns **181 site records**. All 181 were loaded on a single page (size=200, sorted by NPRN ascending) and scanned. The search matches both the record title and the **"Laugharne Township" community**, so a Roman findspot recorded at Island House would surface — the four Island House records (17457 house, 266112 garden, 23035 gazebo, 31614 garage) all appear in the set.

**Exactly one Roman-period site in the whole set (F223-1, Verified):**

> **NPRN 107373 — Coygan Camp — DEFENDED ENCLOSURE — Roman, Iron Age — Laugharne Township.**

Nothing else in the 181 records carries the Roman period. Read at object level (F223-2, Verified), the Coygan Camp record states:

> "A tapering oval enclosure, about 140m north-south by 60m, defined by stone-revetted ramparts, or walling, with an additional rampart and ditch on north, set on a steep-sided promontory, surrounded on all sides, except on the north, by former marshland: excavations, 1963-4, produced late 1st-4th century Romano-British material & structures, including the stock of a minting operation, together with some early medieval material. Greatly, if not terminally prejudiced by quarrying."

Coygan Camp sits at grid **SN2842009140**, roughly **2.3 km south-west** of Island House (SN3012010750 — computed from the two grid references: ΔE ≈ 1700 m, ΔN ≈ 1610 m). It is a genuine Romano-British site of the **wider township**, but it is a promontory hillfort near the Coygan/Pendine marshes, **not** the Island House garden, and it has no recorded connection to the house. It is registered here (`recQT4zIudulSTHRs`) precisely so the two are never conflated: whenever the "Roman Laugharne" question is raised, Coygan is the real Roman-period site — and it is 2.3 km away.

**No Roman remains at Island House or in the town (F223-3, Verified).** The entire Island House NPRN family — 17457 (house), 266112 (garden), 23035 (gazebo), 31614 (garage) — is **Post-Medieval**, consistent with the object-level reads at Docs 187/189/219. The only substantive RCAHMW description among them (garden 266112) is a **1906 kitchen garden, summerhouse, woodland and orchard**, with no Roman element. The only excavated archaeology at the house itself is the **medieval quay/hall-house** (Doc 185) — medieval, not Roman.

## 3. The finding, tiered honestly

**Curtis's 1818 "Roman baths" is uncorroborated by the authoritative modern record — and stays Tradition (F223-4).** The story traces to Curtis alone (Doc 221) and is **not carried** by the national monuments record. Crucially, this is *not* a refutation: **absence of a record is not disproof**, so the claim is **not** promoted to Refuted. It remains **Tradition**, now with an explicit note that the RCAHMW/Coflein record does not corroborate it. If it is ever told on the trail at all, it can only be *"a local antiquarian tradition first written down by Mary Curtis in 1880"* — never as fact, never as archaeology. The nearest genuine Roman site is Coygan Camp, 2.3 km off and unrelated.

This hardens the standing guardrail carried since Docs 185/219/221: no "Roman Laugharne" fact is licensed by the Island House garden tradition.

## 4. The 1917 Inventory is access-gated — recorded, not worked around (F223-5, Verified method/access)

The printed **1917 Carmarthen Inventory could not be read this pass**. It is **not** available as open full text on the Internet Archive: of the RCAHMW county series only **vol VI (Merioneth)** is openly digitised there (`inventoryofanci06roya`). The identifiers `inventoryofanci01`–`09roya` were checked — only `06` exists — and a broad title-plus-Carmarthen search returns no Carmarthen volume. The Carmarthen volume is gated behind the **RCAHMW eBook / Google Play** (pre-1965 inventories are free there, but not machine-readable through the desk's tools). This is an **archive/purchase-day** item.

The **Doc-221 archive.org `/stream` truncation trap was re-confirmed** in passing: a plain fetch of a `…_djvu.txt` `/stream` URL returns the Internet-Archive page shell plus only ~77 KB of text, not the ~1 MB OCR — anyone relying on it will draw a false negative. The full text must be loaded in a JS-capable browser and windowed.

**Honest limit:** because the 1917 volume was not read, this doc makes **no** claim about what it does or does not record at Laugharne. The negative established here is specifically that the **modern** national record (Coflein) carries no Roman remains at Island House or the town.
