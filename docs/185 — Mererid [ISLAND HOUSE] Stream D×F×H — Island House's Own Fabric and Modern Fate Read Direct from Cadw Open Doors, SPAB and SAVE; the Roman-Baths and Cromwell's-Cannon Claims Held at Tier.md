## 1. The three objects, read in full

### 1.1 Cadw, "Open Doors — Island House, Laugharne" (primary Cadw publication)

Read at object level at `https://cadw.gov.wales/open-doors-island-house-laugharne`. This is Cadw's own event listing for the site and its most direct public statement about what the excavation is. Operative text, verbatim:

> "The Island House archaeological excavations are of the first medieval quay/wharf found in Wales, together with a Hall House with central fireplace. The site is immediately below and adjacent to Laugharne Castle. Excavations are ongoing at this time."

Location, verbatim: "Island House, The Grist, Laugharne, SA33 4SA … Island House is located on Wogan Street and The Grist, directly below Laugharne Castle." Booking contact: **Stephen Kirkwood**, `info@islandhouse.wales`.

Two things follow. First, the excavation is framed **medievally** by the body actually publicising it — a quay/wharf and a hall house — with a claim of primacy ("the first medieval quay/wharf found in Wales"). Second, the site contact is Stephen Kirkwood: the engagement's own principal is the current steward of Island House.

### 1.2 SPAB, "Casework: Island House, Laugharne, Carmarthen" (23 Sep 2019)

Read at object level at `https://www.spab.org.uk/news/casework-island-house-laugharne-carmarthen`. The Society for the Protection of Ancient Buildings' casework note. The load-bearing fabric description, verbatim:

> "Grade II* Island House has many fascinating features. Dating from the 16th and 17th centuries, it is constructed on a Z-plan from rubble masonry, which is exceptionally thick in parts. There is a picturesque Gothic crenelated gateway and the charming street elevation (the rear of the building) has a projecting gable. The main range has a Regency bowed oriel window which Pevsner describes as 'extraordinary'. The interior has a large ballroom on the first floor with sea views and 19th-century interior details remain including a neo-Jacobean dog-leg staircase, curved shutters in some of the bedrooms and a good Regency fireplace. Foundations of Roman baths have been discovered on the site."

The final sentence is the one to hold carefully — see §2.

### 1.3 SAVE Britain's Heritage, "Building of the Month, May 2020" (4 May 2020)

Read at object level at `https://www.savebritainsheritage.org/news/Building-of-the-Month-May-2020-Island-House-Laugharne-Carmarthenshire`. Verbatim, the operative passages:

> "16th century Island House, a rambling and romantic building standing next to the ruins of Laugharne Castle on the Taf Estuary, has been acquired by a company. It has plans to restore it and convert it into a boutique hotel, restaurant and bar. The house is grade II* listed and dates from the 16th and 17th centuries, with 19th century additions. It has experienced many changes including being damaged by cannon fire by Cromwell's troops when they attacked the castle in 1644. It retains a charming collection of features including two fine oriel windows and a crenellated gatehouse … SAVE Britain's Heritage added it to the Buildings at Risk Register in 2011 … Island House Restoration Ltd acquired the house in March 2020 … one of the ranges suffering a collapsed roof, their first task is carrying out emergency repairs to stabilise the structure."

## 2. The two divergences, and why neither is promoted

The value of reading three objects on one building is that they disagree in two places, and both disagreements are the kind that quietly corrupt a record if not caught.

### 2.1 "Roman baths" (SPAB) versus a medieval excavation (Cadw)

SPAB's casework note says "Foundations of Roman baths have been discovered on the site." Cadw — the body actually running and publicising the dig — describes the very same site as a **medieval** quay/wharf and a hall house, and makes **no Roman claim at all.**

This is a single secondary mention, uncorroborated, and not supported by the excavating authority's own description. It is logged **Referenced-pending**, not Verified, and must not be printed as an established "Roman Laugharne" fact. The excavation's own framing is medieval; the Roman-baths line is the outlier and is treated as one.

### 2.2 "Cromwell's cannon" (SAVE) versus the register's own siege work

SAVE says the house was "damaged by cannon fire by Cromwell's troops when they attacked the castle in 1644." This is the popular attribution, and it fails against work already in the register. The siege account recovered from Phillips, *Memoirs of the Civil War in Wales and the Marches* (vol. I, p. 274) found that the castle **surrendered on terms after mining** — not a bombardment — with no casualties recorded and "the town secured from plunder." The Parliamentary besieger was **Rowland Laugharne**, not Cromwell.

So the specific claim — Cromwellian cannon fire damaging Island House itself — is unverified on two counts: wrong agent (Cromwell vs Laugharne) and wrong mechanism (cannon fire vs mining and surrender). It is logged **Tradition**, with the correction attached. Standing guardrails observed: the 1644 siege was a week at the end of October (28 Oct – 3 Nov); the town is not named after Rowland Laugharne.

## 3. What is Verified

Cross-read across the three objects and consistent with the Cadw 9671 listing read at Doc 158, the following stand as Verified:

- **Site.** Island House stands on Wogan Street / The Grist, Laugharne (SA33 4SA), on the Taf estuary, immediately below and adjacent to Laugharne Castle. (Cadw Open Doors gives the address; SAVE and SPAB agree on the position.)
- **Fabric.** Grade II*, 16th–17th century with 19th-century additions; Z-plan; rubble masonry exceptionally thick in parts; a crenellated Gothic gatehouse; a first-floor ballroom/room with sea views; a Regency bowed oriel window. (SPAB + SAVE, consistent with Doc 158.)
- **Modern fate.** On SAVE's Buildings at Risk Register since 2011; ~15 years of dereliction; one range with a collapsed roof; the previous owner had bequeathed the house to several beneficiaries who never took up responsibility. (SAVE + SPAB.)
- **Current stewardship.** Island House Restoration Ltd acquired the house in March 2020, intending to restore it as a boutique hotel, restaurant and bar; the Cadw Open Doors site contact is Stephen Kirkwood — i.e. the engagement's own principal is the current steward. (SAVE + Cadw Open Doors.)
- **The excavation, as Cadw states it.** Cadw describes the on-site excavation as "the first medieval quay/wharf found in Wales, together with a Hall House with central fireplace," excavations ongoing. Verified **as an accurate reading of Cadw's statement.** The primacy claim itself ("first in Wales") is only **Referenced-pending**: the underlying excavation record — report reference, dendrochronology, the basis of the claim — is archive/grey-literature gated and belongs to the human archive day. No specific date (e.g. "1437") is printed for the quay; that figure is not in any object read here.
