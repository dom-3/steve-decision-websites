## One-line finding

Read at object level, the *Brut y Tywysogion* does **not** call the 1116 castle "Abercorran": its form is **"Aber Cavwy"**, the leading authority (J. E. Lloyd) locates Robert Courtemain's castle **"near Carmarthen, situated perhaps at Abercywyn"** rather than at Laugharne, and so the familiar "Laugharne first recorded in 1116 as Abercorran" is a contested antiquarian identification, tiered **Referenced-pending** — not fact.

## Why this thread mattered

The base carried, at second hand, the popular tourist-history claim that Laugharne's earliest documentary appearance is a 1116 entry in the *Brut y Tywysogion* naming it **Abercorran**, when the Norman **Robert Courtemain** ("the Crook-handed") built a castle here and set **Bledri ap Cydifor** to keep it. That claim circulates on castle-history websites and in general Laugharne material. The desk rule is to read the object rather than repeat the report, so the task was simply to open the chronicle at the relevant annal and see what it actually says.

## The source, read direct

The *Brut y Tywysogion; or, The Chronicle of the Princes* was edited by the Rev. John Williams (ab Ithel) for the Rolls Series (no. 17, London, 1860), with parallel Welsh text and English translation. The printed English translation was read at object level via the public-domain transcription at maryjones.us (the Internet Archive item is `brutytywysogiono00cara`; the archive.org "full text" stream serves only a JavaScript viewer shell, so the transcription was used as the reading proxy for the printed edition).

The decisive sentence sits inside a long annal that the Williams ab Ithel edition prints **under the year 1113**. That annal narrates the whole rising of **Gruffudd ap Rhys** — the attacks on the castle "near Arberth" (Narberth), Llandovery (Llanymddyfri), Swansea (Abertawy), Carmarthen, Ystrad Peithyll and Aberystwyth. Within the passage describing how the French parcelled out the castles among the local chieftains, the text reads, verbatim:

> "…and Bledri, son of Cedivor, is appointed to keep the castle of Robert, the Crook-handed, at **Aber Cavwy**."

Two things follow immediately from the object.

**First, the name.** The chronicle's form is **"Aber Cavwy"**. The word **"Abercorran" appears nowhere in the text** — a full-text check of the translation returns no instance of it. "Abercorran" is a later English antiquarian name for Laugharne (from the Corran, the stream at the town); it is not the form the *Brut* uses. The spelling "Cavwy" is almost certainly a transcription of a form in *Cywyn / Cowyn* — which points the reader east, not at Laugharne.

**Second, the date.** These events are conventionally dated **1116** (the revolt of Gruffudd ap Rhys), but the Williams ab Ithel edition's chronology in this stretch runs about three years behind and prints them **under 1113**. Anyone citing "the 1116 entry" is citing the correct historical year but will not find it under 1116 in this edition. The safe form is "c.1116, printed s.a. 1113 in the Rolls Series edition."

## The authority, read direct

The identification of Courtemain's castle with a place — and therefore any equation with Laugharne — was tested against the **Dictionary of Welsh Biography**, whose article on **Bledri ap Cydifor (fl. 1116–30)** was written by **Sir John Edward Lloyd** (published 1959), the foremost historian of medieval Wales. Lloyd writes, verbatim:

> "The Normans entrusted the castle of Robert Courtemayn **near Carmarthen, situated perhaps at Abercywyn**, to him during the Welsh revolt of 1116."

Two hedges are doing real work here. Lloyd places the castle **"near Carmarthen"**, and even within that he writes **"perhaps at Abercywyn."** **Abercywyn** lies at the mouth of the river Cywyn (a tributary of the Taf) near **St Clears** — several miles east of Laugharne. Lloyd does not name Laugharne, and does not treat the site as settled.

Lloyd's article also fills out the keeper: **Bledri ap Cydifor**, son of Cydifor (Cadifor Fawr), was a Welsh chieftain of real standing who kept the castle for the Normans. He appears in the **1130 Pipe Roll** as "Bledericus Walensis," and the **Carmarthen Priory cartulary** styles him **"Latemeri"** — interpreter — between the two peoples; he gave four carucates in Eglwys Newydd (Newchurch) c.1129–34, and his descendants held land at Cil Sant, Pwll Dyfach, Mothlysgwm and Picton. (Lloyd notes the tempting but "not accepted on all hands" identification of this Bledri with Gerald of Wales's famous romancer "Bledhericus"; that is a literary aside, not a Laugharne fact.)

## What this does to the "1116 = Abercorran = Laugharne" claim

This is a textbook **near-collision** of exactly the kind the desk is trained to catch. Two river-mouth names sit close together on the map and closer still in careless prose:

- **Abercywyn** — mouth of the Cywyn, near **St Clears** — where Lloyd tentatively places Courtemain's castle.
- **Abercorran** — mouth of the Corran — the antiquarian name for **Laugharne**.

Castle-history websites (castlewales.com, medievalheritage.eu, Paul Martin Remfry, and the "Laugharne Lines" material) assert the Laugharne identification and gloss the *Brut* form as "Abercorran"/"Abercofwy." The primary text does not support that spelling, and the standard authority hedges the location away from Laugharne. The claim is therefore **not refuted** — a genuine case for Laugharne can be argued, and the two forms may ultimately be the same place — but it **cannot be tiered above Referenced-pending**, and it must never be printed as "Laugharne is first recorded in 1116 as Abercorran."

**Safe wording for any trail script or House-of-Culture copy:**

> "A Norman castle is recorded in this district in the early twelfth century, during the revolt of Gruffudd ap Rhys — the chronicle names it *Aber Cavwy* and says the Norman Robert Courtemain held it, with the Welshman Bledri ap Cydifor set to keep it. Whether that castle stood at Laugharne or at Abercywyn near St Clears is debated."

That keeps the genuinely striking material (a named Welsh keeper, a named Norman, an early-twelfth-century castle in the district) without staking Laugharne's origin story on a contested spelling.

## Guardrails observed

- **Name-meaning guardrail:** this doc concerns the English/river name *Abercorran* and the chronicle form *Aber Cavwy* — **not** the Welsh name *Talacharn*. The Tal-/-acharn element is not touched or glossed here.
- **No overstatement:** the Laugharne identification is reported as **contested / Referenced-pending**, with the opposing (Abercywyn) reading given equal room. Reporting the popular claim as unproven is the intended outcome, not a failure.
- **Chronology:** the event is cited as **c.1116**, with the edition's displaced year (1113) flagged so no false "1113" date enters the record.
