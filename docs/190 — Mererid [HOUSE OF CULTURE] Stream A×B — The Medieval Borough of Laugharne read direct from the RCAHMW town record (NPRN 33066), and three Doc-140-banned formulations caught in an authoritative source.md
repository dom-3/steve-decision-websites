### 1. The object

**Coflein / RCAHMW site record, NPRN 33066 — "Medieval and Later Borough, Laugharne;Lacharn"** (read direct, 23 Jul 2026).
Type of site: TOWN. Period: General. Grid reference **SN3019810832**. Community: Laugharne Township. Compiled by RCAHMW, 2009; cited source **Lloyd, Orbach & Scourfield, 2006, *The Buildings of Wales: Carmarthenshire and Ceredigion*.**

Read alongside it for the borough's physical setting:

**Gatehouse Gazetteer, Laugharne Castle (record 225)** (read direct, 23 Jul 2026) — which compiles Coflein, the Listed Building Report and the Scheduling Report, and quotes at length from **R. Avent, 1992, "Laugharne Castle", in *A Guide to Ancient and Historic Wales: Dyfed* (HMSO).**

Both are secondary/compiled records (T2). Neither is a primary borough charter or survey — those remain archive-gated and belong to the human archive day — but between them they give the RCAHMW's own object-level statement of the town's shape, which is what this pass set out to read.

### 2. What the record establishes (Verified)

**2.1 The walled borough, and where it lay.** The RCAHMW record describes Laugharne as *"Formerly a walled medieval borough"*, established on the landward side of its early-twelfth-century castle (NPRN 95634) on the western bank of the Taf. The Gatehouse/Avent scheduling account fixes the orientation: the inner court sat within *"a larger diamond plan outer court with a great gatehouse at its northern apex facing into the walled borough that had by now been established on the castle's landward side."*

This is the strongest thing the open record gives the walled-borough question: an authoritative statement that the borough was walled and that its gate-line faced the castle's **northern** apex. It does **not** give a surviving wall circuit or a mapped line. So the walled status is Verified *as the RCAHMW record's statement*, while the **physical town wall itself remains Referenced-pending** (cf. the standing entity "Town wall of Laugharne — inferred early enclosure; 1465 murage licence", Doc 140). The pass advances threads recJWeB6bzIkruQkf (the town gatehouse), rec6YrKrbWNJOOTod and recp44sRjVINO8xut (the town's 1644 defences) without closing them.

**2.2 The Landsker line and the town's language.** The castle is described as one of a series built at strategic points along the *"Landsker line"*, and RCAHMW credits this directly with the township being *"English speaking in a predominantly Welsh speaking part of Wales."* A usable object statement for the Welsh-language-layer threads (which otherwise wait on the 1891/1911 census language returns).

**2.3 The town's field systems.** RCAHMW links an **open-field system (NPRN 308928)**, some 900 m south-west of St Martin's Church, and a **strip-field system (NPRN 401707)**, south and east of the church, to the borough. These are new leads worth registering as their own objects; they bear on the burgage-land / strang work on Hugden and the Lees.

**2.4 Name forms.** The record gives *Abercorran* — explained as taken from *"the smaller Corran river [that] runs through the town"* — and *Talacharn* as alternative names; Gatehouse adds the historic forms *Abercorran / Abercorram / Talacharn / Talcharn / Talachar.* Per Doc 140 the second element of Talacharn is left obscure and is **not** glossed here. This complements the name-attestation work in Docs 186 and 188.

**2.5 A sourced literary link to the castle.** Avent's HMSO guide (quoted in Gatehouse) records Dylan Thomas's phrase *"the castle, brown as owls"*, from *"Poem in October"*, as a description of Laugharne Castle. This is a properly sourced Stream-G fact anchored to a **named poem** and a **named building** — the castle, not the Globe and not Brown's. It is exactly the kind of safe-ground literary material the branch is meant to prefer.

### 3. Three Doc-140-banned formulations, caught inside an authoritative-looking source (Refuted)

The value of reading this record is not only what it confirms but what it gets wrong. The RCAHMW town record — which looks and reads as authoritative — repeats **three** of the exact formulations Doc 140 bans. Each is logged as a **source-error (Refuted)**, not adopted:

1. **Charter "dating to 1278."** RCAHMW hard-codes the charter and annual fair to **1278**, granted by Guy de Brian IV. Doc 140 forbids printing 1278 / 1290 / 1307 as the charter date; the desk gives it as **"late thirteenth century."** This confirms the 1278 tradition is repeated even by the national record (the root of that tradition is being run down separately in thread recYz8Cfpm73s8eCp — "Williams, n.d.").

2. **"The last surviving medieval corporation in the UK."** RCAHMW writes exactly this. Doc 140's safe wording is **"one of only two medieval corporations to survive in Britain, alongside the City of London"** — never "the last surviving." The national record repeats the error.

3. **"Two Portreeves or port wardens."** RCAHMW states the charter *"makes provision for two Portreeves."* The Corporation has **one Portreeve**; the pair is the **two Common Attorneys.** RCAHMW appears to have conflated the single Portreeve with the paired officers.

That an RCAHMW record carries all three of these at once is itself a finding: it shows how deep the standard errors run, and it is a caution against ever treating a Coflein town record as clean court-grade text. (It also vindicates the standing rule not to trust a paraphrase: the earlier Doc 156 note already flagged Coflein 23036 as a "lossy paraphrase" of Cadw 9656.)

### 4. One contested item, held at tier (Tradition)

RCAHMW notes Laugharne is *"thought to have been an inspiration for the fictional town of Llareggub in 'Under Milk Wood'"*, that Dylan Thomas lived here from 1949 until his death, that the Boathouse (NPRN 17122) lies ~500 m upstream of the castle, and that he is buried in St Martin's churchyard (NPRN 418571). The Boathouse dates and the burial are plain fact. The **Llareggub = Laugharne** identification is **CONTESTED** (Doc 140; Richard Hughes recorded reservations, and the identification competes with New Quay). It is recorded here only as the RCAHMW record's own hedged "thought to" phrasing and is **not endorsed.**
