House of Culture · The Economics

The spend-flow model — and where the money goes

Top-down: pick the target spend per head, benchmark it honestly, then back-solve the experience to hit it — and design it so the income flows out to the town, not just into the till.

⚠ Model — sourced benchmarks vs stated assumptions, clearly separated

1 · The honest starting point

You set the target at £30–50 per head. Here's what the sector actually does, so we design to reality rather than hope.

The best UK dataset (AIM Economic Impact Study 2024) puts all-in on-site spend — admission + retail + café combined — at about £4–7.50 a head at a small museum, rising to £21.93 even at the largest independents. So £30–50 secondary spend per head is roughly 2–7× the sector norm.

That is not a reason to lower the ambition — it's the reason the ambition needs an unusual model. £30–50 is only reachable if every stage earns and we run a genuine "full day out," not a museum with a gift shop bolted on. And the move that unlocks it is counter-intuitive: give the trail away free, seed it across the town on QR codes, and put the money downstream — in the artisan bakery, the restaurant, the bar and the shop. Free isn't lost revenue; it's the widest possible funnel, and it's what lets us reach past the pilgrims to everyone passing through.

What's free, what's paid — the owner's lever. Only the app and the town QR layer are fixed as free. The heritage centre is free to enter on an invited donation (with Gift Aid) — the model most free museums use, and it still lifts per-head. Everything else — food, retail, gallery, events — is paid. That split is Steve's to set and adjust as the model beds in; nothing here is locked except the free app.

2 · The funnel — free, and everywhere

The trail is the acquisition engine, not a revenue line. Give it away, and put it everywhere the town already has footfall.

How it works. QR codes on the buildings and spots Steve holds around Laugharne — the castle approach, the estuary, the square, the shopfronts. Someone scans out of curiosity; the web app loads instantly, no download, no account; it tells them where they're standing and what happened there — and it quietly makes the Globe the place the walk begins and ends. Even a passer-by with no plan gets pulled into the loop, because the thing is genuinely good and genuinely free.

Why free beats paid here. A paid trail only sells to people who already came for Dylan. A free one, seeded across town, catches the castle visitor, the coast-path walker, the family killing an hour — a far bigger pool — and routes them all past our doors. We give away the £5 trail ticket to win the £30 day. And it doubles as the honest, award-worthy story: a free cultural gift to the town.

One practical guardrail: QR codes go on Steve's own property or with the owner's permission — not fly-posted on listed buildings or others' walls, which is an offence and the opposite of good-neighbour. A short "where can codes go" list is a quick job worth doing before printing.

4 · Selling forward — prompts along the journey

Because the app walks with the visitor, it can surface the next good thing at the right moment — gently, and always as a genuine tip, never a pop-up ad. Tiered so the message meets the mood.

Early — plant the seedOn the way out: "the bread's just out of the oven — we'll set a loaf aside for your walk back." The purchase is placed hours before it happens.
Mid-walk — the useful nudgePassing a stop near lunch: "hungry? the kitchen's doing X today." Passing the butcher's story: "the town's meat, if you're self-catering tonight."
The turn home — the reminder"On your way back: the deli, the wine bar, and that loaf we saved. Everything for tonight, under one roof."
At the end — the keepsake"Take the day home" — the larder, the print of the stop they lingered at, a bottle. The story becomes the object.

The rule that keeps it from being spam: every prompt is a real service to the walker, not an interruption — "we'll save you a loaf" is a kindness that happens to sell. If a prompt wouldn't be welcome from a helpful local, it doesn't ship. That's what makes the free app feel like a gift and still drive the spend.

6 · Where the money goes — out to the town

The part that matters most, and the part that makes it award-worthy rather than just profitable. A large share of every pound is designed to leave our till and land in Laugharne.

The good-neighbour engine. Standard gallery consignment is 50/50 to 40/60 — we lean it to the maker. The larder is sourced from named local farms. The oral-history voices and the guides are paid local people. And we send visitors to the town's other businesses, not around them. In a town of ~1,200, an anchor attraction that keeps its spend circulating locally is the difference between being resented and being defended — and it's the honest basis for raising the owner's profile: the town benefiting, not being mined.

7 · The levers that get us from £11 to £34

Free trail, seeded everywhereGive the app away and QR-code it across the town, so it catches passers-by, not just committed pilgrims — and every route quietly runs through the Globe. Free maximises the funnel; the money is downstream. The single biggest structural move.
Provenance lets you charge properlyA shop where every object carries a true story commands a higher average transaction than a souvenir rack — and it's the reason retail can run at £30 ATV, not £12.
Pre-book to lift secondary spendOnline pre-booking of the trail/experience lifts on-site spend by up to 24% (ACE) — the entry money is already spent, so the café and shop money is still in the pocket.
The café is the biggest single leverCatering is the largest and most reliable secondary spend at most attractions — and the most punished on reviews if over-priced. Get coffee-and-cake right and it carries the average.
A range at every priceA £3 card and a £300 original under one roof means nobody leaves empty-handed and the occasional big sale lifts the blended figure.
A reason to returnThe rotating gallery, the three-year Common Walk, the changing larder — repeat visits and locals, not only one-time pilgrims.

8 · Extra angles & benefits discovered

A CIC / community structureStructuring the cultural layer as a community-benefit body would strengthen grant and award eligibility, and make the "money flows to the town" claim formal and defensible — worth weighing against the simplicity of full ownership.
Replicable across the branchesThe flow model — arrive, warm up, guided experience, provenance retail, a table, take home — is a template. Prove it here and the same spend-flow can run at Island House and the other sites, each with its own story.
The app is a sellable assetA working, honest, bilingual heritage-trail engine has value beyond Laugharne — other towns and heritage bodies have the same problem and no chain of custody to solve it.
Events fill the quiet hoursEvenings and the off-season — readings, the reading room, private hire, festival tie-ins (Laugharne Weekend) — turn a daytime-pilgrim business into a year-round one.
All-weather — the rainy-day magnetThe Boathouse, the castle and the coast are exposed. A large, warm, indoor heritage centre is the thing to do when the weather turns — and in coastal Wales, that's a lot of days. It de-risks the whole model against the one thing it can't control.
It solves the reviews' actual complaintsOur own gap map found: dated exhibition, steep path/no parking, price-for-size, nowhere reliable to eat. A free, well-curated, level, indoor experience with good food answers every one — so the service spreads through the reviews itself.
Room to grow — building by buildingLarge indoor space now, and the real prospect of acquiring further buildings around the town as they come up. The model isn't one venue; it's a template that can spread across Laugharne, each site its own chapter.
