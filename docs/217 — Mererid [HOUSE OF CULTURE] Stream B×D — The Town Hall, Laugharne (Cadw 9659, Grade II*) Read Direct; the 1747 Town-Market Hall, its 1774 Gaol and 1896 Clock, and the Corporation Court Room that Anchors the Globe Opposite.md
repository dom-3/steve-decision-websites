## Why this target

The Corporation of Laugharne has been dug hard — its offices and courts (Doc/thread *recall*), the undated de Brian charter, the Municipal Corporations Act 1883 s.20 saving, the London Gazette negative, the OBB1 archive finding aid. But every one of those threads treats the Corporation as an institution. Nobody had read the **building it meets in**. The Town Hall is the one piece of town fabric that ties the whole Corporation story to a physical Stop on the trail: its first-floor court room is where the Portreeve still conducts the Corporation's fortnightly courts, and it stands directly opposite the Globe. It is a Grade II\* listed civil building with a readable open-source statutory record, and it had not been logged. It is squarely in the House of Culture lane (town fabric + Corporation), not Island House. Taken as the next un-done, high-value town-fabric target.

## The building

The present Town Hall **dates from 1747**, "although an earlier Town Hall existed" — Cadw's words. It does **not** say the earlier hall stood on the same site, so no continuity of site is claimed here. The building has been much altered and extended, and reads today as an accretion of dated phases:

- a 2-storey, 2-window **Town / Market Hall**, whitewashed rubble, with a **clock tower** to its south under a pyramidal slate roof;
- a low lean-to **gaol** of **1774** to the south (Cadw's text prints "goal", a recurring OCR/typo for *gaol*);
- a **bellcote** — originally carrying a bell by the Gloucester bell-founder **Abraham Rudhall** — and a **weathercock**, added to the tower in **1786**;
- recessed **Gothick sash windows of 1814**, set over the segmental openings that formerly led into the ground-floor market, later closed by iron railings;
- **clock faces** north and south whose keystones are dated **1896**, following a **contract of 1891** to renew "the clock tower and turret" by **M Williams and J Jones**, at a cost of **£30**;
- an adjoining gabled **porch of 1910** to the south-east, with bargeboards and finial;
- a much-restored **market lean-to** at the rear.

The dated fabric — 1747, 1774, 1786, 1814, 1896, 1910 — makes the Town Hall a useful spine for a Stop: it is a building you can read forwards through the town's own centuries.

## Why it matters for the programme

The interior is the prize. Cadw records that the building "retains court room to 1st floor; timber dais with ornate iron bracketed lamps **from which the Portreeve conducts the meetings of Laugharne Corporation** (unique in Wales in retaining this form of local government). Regency fireplace to E wall. At one time held a school and the Public Library."

Three things follow.

1. **It physically anchors the Corporation-ritual material.** The Big Court at which the Portreeve is sworn, and the fortnightly Court Leet and Court Baron, meet *in this room*. The abstract institution now has a Stop.
2. **It reinforces the standing "one Portreeve" correction.** Cadw says "the Portreeve" — singular — conducts the meetings. That is consistent with the register's Verified correction that there is one Portreeve (the pair is the two Common Attorneys), and it comes from an authoritative source, so it cannot slip.
3. **It supplies a safe, Cadw-sourced superlative.** "Unique in Wales in retaining this form of local government" is Cadw's own phrasing. It is **Wales-specific** and is kept deliberately distinct from the broader, Britain-wide formulation the programme uses elsewhere — "one of only two medieval corporations to survive in Britain, alongside the City of London." Cadw does **not** make that broader claim here; do not weld the two together.

## The Globe anchor, corroborated

The Town Hall's location text reads: "Forward to the street at the junction with Wogan Street, close to the castle; Market Lane to rear." The neighbouring **Globe** listing (Globe House, Cadw) reads: "Opposite the town Hall at the end of the street, on the corner with Duncan Street. Formerly the Globe Hotel, sold in 1869." This independently corroborates the standing **safe locational anchor** for the Globe — *"opposite the Town Hall, on the corner with Duncan Street"* — without touching the open "High-street" question (Doc 167b, Doc 198). The incidental "**sold in 1869**" detail is held **Referenced-pending**: it is the listing's aside, not a document read; the Globe's own record is Doc 198.
