1 · The proposition

What this business actually is.

Laugharne receives a steady stream of visitors who arrive already caring — literary pilgrims, coast-path walkers, castle visitors, people who have read Under Milk Wood and want to stand where it came from. They arrive with intent, and most of them leave within an hour.

The House of Culture is the business that captures that. It is not a museum and not a rival to any existing attraction. It is the base camp: the place a visit starts, the place it returns to, and the place it ends — with coffee and a fresh bake before the walk, the story and the trail in between, and a proper table at the end of it.

Three revenue effects follow from one idea. Visitors stay longer, so they spend across more of the day. They arrive with a reason to come here rather than anywhere, so footfall is intentional rather than incidental. And they carry something home, so the visit continues earning after they leave.

2 · The opportunity

Drawn from systematic analysis of visitor reviews across the town's attractions.

The day has no spine

Visitors piece the town together themselves from a handful of separate sites with no connecting narrative. There is no single thing that says: start here, and here is the whole story. That absence is our product.

The steep approach excludes people

The most-visited site sits up a steep path with no adjacent parking. A significant share of the literary audience is older or has limited mobility, and some of them simply cannot reach it. A level, town-centre anchor serves them properly.

Interpretation is dated

Reviews consistently describe the storytelling as tired. The material is extraordinary; the telling has not kept pace. Well-curated interpretation is a genuine differentiator here, not a nice-to-have.

Nothing is taken home

Almost no considered retail, and little that connects what a visitor has just seen to something they can keep. A trail that ends in a shop with real provenance behind every object is a straightforward gain.

The honest framing. Laugharne is not short of places to eat, and this plan does not depend on pretending otherwise. What it lacks is a single, dependable, all-day room on the trail itself — somewhere you can walk into mid-afternoon, wine-led and unhurried, between the tea rooms closing and dinner service beginning. That is the slot we take, and it is complementary to what the town already does well.

The bookend is the mechanism. Foot traffic to the principal literary site passes our door in both directions, because there is nowhere to park at the other end. We hold both ends of that walk by being genuinely useful at the start of it — the wifi where the trail downloads, the loo, the water bottle filled, the phone charged, the map explained, the level place a split party agrees to meet. Earn the outward leg by being helpful and the return leg looks after itself.

The discipline that keeps this from becoming an advert: the first stop carries no commercial content at all, and the test applied to every line we write is — would this still be here if the Globe didn't own it?

4 · The trails

One map, several journeys — so the town serves the ninety-minute visitor and the all-day walker equally.

The Township Loop

Short · level · town centre. The square, the Town Hall, the corporation town, the working port. Designed for visitors who cannot manage the steep approaches — the audience currently being lost entirely.

The Literary Loop

Medium. The classic pilgrimage, done properly and bookended by us — the writing places, the pub, the grave, the view, with the real story rather than the received one.

The Estuary Trail

Long · harder. The walk the poet actually walked — the heron-priested shore, the full day on foot for people who want the whole thing.

Beneath the trails sits the structural idea that makes this more than a list of plaques: a story is not tied to a single place. One building carries several stories; one story runs through several buildings. The trail is a web, not a line — so completing a story means walking the town, and the town reveals itself in layers rather than in one pass.

5 · The app

The delivery mechanism — and the thing that makes this defensible.

Simple on top

Built for someone who does not enjoy technology. One screen, one stop, one big listen button, one thing to look out for, one tap to the next. No account, no menus, and it never depends on a phone signal or on satellite positioning to work.

Deep underneath

Beneath every stop, optional layers for the curious — the fuller story, the archive images, the recorded voices of people who remember, and the actual sources. The same app serves a twenty-minute walker and a three-hour obsessive.

Ask it anything

At any stop a visitor can ask a question and get a real answer drawn from our own verified library, with the source shown. And — the part nobody else can do — it says plainly when we don't know: the record doesn't tell us, but here's what we do know.

Collect the town

An opt-in collecting layer: gather the stories as you walk, and completing one unlocks the full dossier behind it rather than a badge. Depth is the reward. It goes silent at the graveside and the other quiet places, by design.

Works without signal. The trail downloads at the Globe before you set off, and the coast path has no coverage — so the record itself travels with the walker, not merely a cached summary. If the phone loses signal, the stories and their sources still work.

Bilingual from the start. Welsh and English are written in parallel from the same source material, not translated afterwards. In a Carmarthenshire town a translated script sounds translated, and that reads as a credibility failure rather than a quality one.

6 · The foundation — why this is credible

The research is the moat. Anyone can build an app; nobody else has the record underneath it.

Behind the visitor experience sits a working historical archive with a full chain of custody: 563 recorded facts, 270 sources and 413 people, places and things, every claim graded by how well it is proven and linked to the document that proves it. Research runs continuously against a register of 100 open questions.

The rule is absolute and built into the software rather than left to memory: nothing is published while a claim behind it is unverified, and every published sentence traces back to its source in one tap. Where the record is contested, we say so. Where we have only tradition, we call it tradition.

This has already changed what we say. Claims we initially believed have been retired under evidence — including a literary connection to this building that turned out to belong to another town, and a dating of the cellars that the statutory record does not support. Being willing to lose a good story is what makes the remaining ones worth telling.

"The well established Inn, situate in High-street… called the Globe Inn, with two stables, a good Coach-house, yard and a small Garden, now in the occupation of the representatives of the late Mr. Thomas Edwards…"

The Globe does not need to borrow anyone else's story. It has an innkeeper by name in 1830, a coach-house, assembly balls in its own ballroom by 1835, and around eighty years as a butcher's shop under the Gleeds, closing in 1991. The full record is here →

9 · The principles we build to

These are constraints, not aspirations. Each one has already cost us something.

Verified or it doesn't publish

No claim reaches a visitor while the evidence behind it is unproven. When the record contradicts a good story, the story goes.

We are a good neighbour

Other attractions and businesses in this town are cited honestly and sent visitors, not undercut. A rising tide here genuinely does lift everyone, and we have to live here.

The town is the attraction

Built as a cultural resource for Laugharne to use, not a marketing device wearing a museum's clothes. That is also why it will still be worth something in ten years.

Nobody's voice is a commodity

Recorded testimony is given on clear, separable consent, held by this project, and never traded to anyone. A neighbour's voice is not currency.

Promise only what we can deliver

No accessibility claim without a physical audit. No promise of erasure we cannot honour. Where the truth is less impressive, we say the truth.

Quiet where it matters

The playful layer switches off at the graveside and the other places that deserve silence. One tone failure there would define us.

10 · Why this is defensible

A trail app can be copied in a fortnight. What cannot be copied is what sits underneath this one.

The archive. Hundreds of verified facts assembled from primary documents — newspaper advertisements, wills, statute, listing records and parish registers. That took real work and it compounds.

The voices. The people who remember this town, recorded properly while they are here. Nobody else can obtain that material, and each year it gets smaller.

The honesty. An interpretation that shows its sources and admits its gaps is rare enough in heritage to be a position in itself — and it is exactly what serious institutional partners respond to.

The building. Nine bedrooms, a ballroom, a courtyard and a coach-house on the corner of the town's principal streets, opposite the Town Hall, with two centuries of documented history and a bar, bakery and kitchen already trading inside it.
