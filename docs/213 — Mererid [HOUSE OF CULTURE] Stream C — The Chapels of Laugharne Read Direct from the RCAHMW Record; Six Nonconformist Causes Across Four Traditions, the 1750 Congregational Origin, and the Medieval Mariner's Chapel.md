### The count: six Nonconformist causes, four traditions, one medieval chapel

Read against the RCAHMW record, the town of Laugharne (Laugharne Township community) carried at least **six Nonconformist meeting-houses across four traditions**, alongside the parish church of St Martin — plus the ruined **medieval Mariner's Chapel** by the shore. Every date below is taken verbatim from the RCAHMW description; where RCAHMW is the sole authority the fact is tiered **Verified**, not Verified-strong.

| Chapel | Tradition | Street | Built | Rebuilt | Fate | NPRN |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Old English Congregational (Providence) | Congregational / Independent | Cliff Street | **1750** | 1809, 1853 | demolished c.1950 | 6308 |
| New Congregational English Chapel | Congregational / Independent | King Street | **c.1890** | — | **standing** (only survivor in the record) | 6307 |
| Philadelphia Independent | Independent | (not given) | pre-1800 | 1800 | status unknown (2000) | 97164 |
| Tabernacle | Calvinistic Methodist / English Presbyterian | Wogan Street | **1833** | 1872 | demolished by 1989 | 6311 |
| English Methodist (Wesleyan) | Wesleyan Methodist | New Bridge Street | **1810/11** | — | demolished by 1998 | 6312 |
| Quakers' Burial Ground & meeting-house | Religious Society of Friends | Cross Inn | — | — | demolished by 1989 | 6310 |
| Mariner's Chapel *(medieval)* | pre-Reformation | south of the Grist, by the Castle | medieval | — | ruinous by 1880; untraced after 1912 | 417871 |

Two further chapels sit inside the Laugharne Township community but out in its rural edges rather than in the town: **Bwlchnewydd Baptist** (NPRN 6309) and **Bethel English Baptist Chapel, Plashet, Llanmiloe** (NPRN 6583). They are noted here for completeness but are not town fabric.

### The four traditions, in the town's own order

**1. Congregational / Independent — the senior cause (1750).**
The oldest Nonconformist foundation the record dates is the Old English Congregational Chapel at **Providence, Cliff Street, built 1750** — a generation before Methodism reached the town. It was rebuilt in **1809** and again in **1853**, and demolished about **1950** (NPRN 6308). A **New Congregational English Chapel** then appears on **King Street, c.1890**, in the Romanesque style: a gable entrance, full-length round-headed windows, and a gallery carried on cast-iron columns spanning one wall (NPRN 6307). This King Street chapel is the **only** town chapel Coflein records as still standing, and it is the only one carrying photographs and archive items in the record. It is very likely the later home of the same Congregational cause that began at Providence — but the RCAHMW records do not state that link, so it is **not asserted here**; the two are logged as separate buildings with an open question over the relationship. A second Independent cause, **Philadelphia**, was **rebuilt in 1800** (implying an earlier origin), its later status unknown to RCAHMW as of 2000 (NPRN 97164).

**2. Wesleyan Methodism (1810/11).**
The **English (Wesleyan) Methodist chapel on New Bridge Street** was built **1810/11** and demolished by 1998 (NPRN 6312) — the arrival of organised Methodism in the town in the decade after 1800.

**3. Calvinistic Methodism / English Presbyterianism (1833).**
The **Tabernacle** on **Wogan Street**, styled "English Presbyterian and Calvinistic Methodist," was built **1833**, rebuilt **1872**, and demolished by 1989 (NPRN 6311).

**4. The Quakers (Society of Friends).**
On the town's northern edge at **Cross Inn** was a **Quaker burial ground with a meeting-house** — the quietest and probably the oldest-rooted dissent — recorded by RCAHMW as demolished by 1989 (NPRN 6310).

### The medieval outlier: the Mariner's Chapel

Distinct from the Nonconformist chapels is the pre-Reformation **Mariner's Chapel** (NPRN 417871), recorded by RCAHMW (N. Vousden, 2012) as having stood **near the Castle, immediately south of the open space known as the Grist**. The record summarises antiquarian observation rather than a standing object: in **1880** only ivy-covered walls remained, with a reported **stoup and piscina** (neither traceable by **1912**); the remains were rectilinear, oriented east–west, about **35 ft × 20 ft**, on ground that by then formed part of a business premises and lay **below the high-water mark**. Because this rests on the 1880 and 1912 observations and not on anything now visible, it is held at **Referenced-pending**, with a Curtis (1880) back-check flagged as the next step.

### Tiering

- **Verified (RCAHMW authoritative record):** the existence, denomination, street, and build/rebuild/demolition dates of the six Nonconformist chapels (NPRN 6307, 6308, 6311, 6312, 97164, 6310), and the synthesis that the town held at least six such causes across four traditions.
- **Referenced-pending:** the Mariner's Chapel — medieval origin and physical remains rest on 1880/1912 antiquarian description as relayed by RCAHMW.
- **Not asserted:** that the c.1890 King Street chapel is the direct successor of the 1750 Providence cause (probable, but unstated in the source).

These are single-authority facts. To lift the enumeration to **Verified-strong**, the obvious second source is the **1851 Religious Census returns for Laugharne** (Jones & Williams, eds., *The Religious Census of 1851: A Calendar of the Returns Relating to Wales*, Vol. 1, South Wales, UWP, 1976), which would independently confirm which chapels were open in 1851 and give congregation sizes. It has **not** been read.
