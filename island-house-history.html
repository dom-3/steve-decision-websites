800 years of one shell

The quays were infilled with cockle shells; the same cockle economy fed Laugharne's Victorian "cockle-women" and still runs today. One shell, threaded from the medieval quay to now.

Heneb excavation · Mary Curtis (1855)

Mary Curtis — the lost historian

A former governess and painter who moved to Laugharne in 1855 and wrote the first real history of the town — including the cockle-women no one else recorded. Her book is free to read online.

Narberth Museum · archive.org

Your house was a chandlery

The Skyrme family — three generations of ship's-chandlers (marine suppliers) — lived at Island House 1742–1823. A maritime through-line running straight through the building.

Wikipedia, citing Francis Jones / Curtis

Two medieval survivals, one town

Laugharne is the only excavated medieval Welsh port and one of Britain's last two working medieval corporations (with the City of London). No other UK town pairs the two.

Heneb · Laugharne Corporation

The 1658 door

A date carved on the yard door marks the rebuild after the house was damaged in the 1644 Civil War siege of the castle — a scar you can put a finger on.

Wikipedia, "Island House, Laugharne"

The quay reached Spain

Pottery from the dig links this small quay to Bristol, France and Spain — proof it plugged into the medieval Atlantic economy, not just local trade.

The Library

Five collections, from the house outward. Each entry tagged and sourced.

IThe House

Built c.1610–1630, on older bones

The roof timbers dendro-date to a 1437 felling, but the truss style is early-1600s — so medieval timber was reused when the present house went up. There was a high-status building here before.Verified

Excavation dendrochronology (Heneb)

Why it's called Island House

It sits on a tidal inlet with a river channel still running beneath it — near-cut-off at high tide (the name), and the reason the building is structurally unstable and floods.Verified

Heneb desk assessment / excavation

A cast of owners

Possibly built by the White family (14th c.); later Vaughan of Golden Grove, Mansel of Margam, Rhys Prydderch (High Sheriff 1608), the Skyrme ship-chandlers (1742–1823), then Major Claude Congreve and the writer Caleb Rees; acquired for restoration in 2020 by Steven Kirkwood & Philip Bond.Verified

Wikipedia, citing Curtis / Francis Jones

The 3-metre garden wall

A massive clay-waterproofed wall held the tide back; its later breach is why the site floods today. Engineering against the water is the house's whole story.Verified

Excavation (Heneb)

IIThe Port & Trade

The first medieval quays found in Wales

Under the house: a run of stone quays — the first medieval quays ever excavated in Wales. Ken Murphy (Heneb): parallels only in the big ports, London and Bristol.Verified

Heneb, 2025; Nation.Cymru

The silting "arms race"

Successive quays were pushed further out as the inlet silted, each infilled behind with cockle shells — a 400-year losing battle until boats could no longer reach by the 1600s.Verified

Excavation (Heneb)

A harbour for 350-ton ships

Before siltation, the adjacent Gosport Harbour could shelter vessels of ~350 tons — this was a real working port, not a landing beach.Verified

Wikipedia, "Island House, Laugharne"

Where the trade is written down

The port's real cargoes would sit in the customs particular accounts (E 122) and port books (E 190) at The National Archives, Kew — Laugharne likely a "creek" under the head port of Carmarthen. A desk-research goldmine to open.To verify

IIIThe Town

A living medieval corporation

Laugharne Corporation — with the City of London, one of the last two working medieval corporations in Britain. Established 1291 by Sir Guy de Brian; abolished on paper (1886) yet still operating under its original charter.Verified

Wikipedia / Laugharne Corporation

The Portreeve & the cockle-shell chain

Headed by a Portreeve in a gold chain of cockle shells — each holder adds an engraved shell. Two surviving courts (Leet & Baron); the 76 senior burgesses still hold life strips on Hugden — one of only two working open-field survivals in Britain.Verified

Wikipedia / WikiShire

The Common Walk

Every three years, a ~25-mile Whit-Monday perambulation of the bounds; at set landmarks a walker who can't name the spot is hoisted upside-down and beaten three times. Genuinely still done.Verified

WikiShire

The de Brian lords

The French-origin de Brian family held Laugharne from 1247; one Guy was captured in Llywelyn ap Gruffudd's 1257–58 attacks on the castle. The charter that made the town survives (translations at the National Library of Wales).Verified

Laugharne Township CC · NLW

A de Brian carried the name into national history: Admiral of the West, Knight of the Garter, bearer of the King's Standard against the French at Calais (1350); his tomb is at Tewkesbury Abbey.Verified identity vs the Laugharne Guys to confirm

The Victorian incomer who wrote it all down first — the Corporation, the port, the cockle-women — pioneering social history a woman of her time wasn't expected to.Verified

VThe Wider Sea

The quay vs the ships

The Newport Ship (c.1450) and Magor Pill boat (1239/40) are Wales's famous medieval vessels. Island House is different and complementary — the first medieval port infrastructure excavated in Wales. The place the ships came to.Verified

Museum Wales · Newport Ship

The Atlantic economy

Medieval West Wales sat on the edge of a busy Atlantic trade — Gascon wine, wool and cloth, salt and iron across from France, Spain and Ireland. The Bristol/French/Spanish pottery here is a thread of that whole sea.Verified (context)

Bristol Channel trade scholarship
